Here's a practical, lender-ready plan you can follow to ask for a business loan for Deadhang Labor LLC, tailored to your background in labor contracting and backend/AI tooling.

## 1) Clarify your loan goal and amount

Before you talk to lenders, get crystal clear on:

- **Purpose:** Working capital, equipment (truck + trailer), hiring, marketing, or building out your AI/backend tools as billable services?
- **Amount:** Exact dollar figure (e.g., $75,000 for a used truck + trailer + initial operating buffer).
- **Repayment source:** Which revenue streams will cover the payment (labor contracts, recurring admin-tool subscriptions, project margins)?
- **Term preference:** 3–5 years for equipment, 1–3 years for working capital, etc.

Lenders want to see that the loan has a specific use and a clear path to repayment from cash flow.[1][2]

***

## 2) Gather core documents (LLC loan checklist)

Most lenders will expect:

**Business basics**
- LLC formation documents (Articles of Organization, Operating Agreement)
- EIN confirmation letter
- Business license(s) relevant to labor contracting in Michigan
- Any key contracts (current or pipeline) with clients

**Financials**
- Last 12–24 months of business bank statements
- Profit & Loss (P&L) and Balance Sheet (even if simple)
- Business tax returns (if filed) or personal tax returns if the LLC is new[3][4]
- 12-month cash flow forecast showing how the loan payment fits[5]

**Personal**
- Personal financial statement (assets, liabilities, monthly expenses)
- Last 2–3 years of personal tax returns
- Government ID, proof of address

**Collateral (if applicable)**
- Details on the truck/trailer you plan to buy (quotes, listings)
- Any other assets you could pledge (equipment, receivables)

Having these organized in a single folder (PDFs + simple spreadsheet) makes you look professional and reduces back-and-forth.[6][3]

## 3) Build a concise, loan-focused business plan

You don't need a 40-page document. Aim for 8–12 pages that hit what lenders care about.

### a) Executive summary (1–2 pages)
Lead with:
- What Deadhang Labor LLC does (labor contracting + backend/AI tools for labor businesses)
- Current traction: revenue, key clients, contracts in pipeline
- Loan request: amount, purpose, term
- Repayment logic: “Projected DSCR post-loan is X.x” or “Loan payment is Y% of monthly net cash flow.”[2][1]

Example structure:
- Business: “Deadhang Labor LLC provides skilled labor contracting and builds AI-powered admin tools for small trades businesses in Michigan.”
- Traction: “2025 revenue: $___;  active clients: __;  average monthly net cash flow: $___.”
- Request: “Seeking a $___ term loan for equipment (truck + trailer) and 3 months of operating capital.”
- Repayment: “With projected monthly net cash flow of $___, the loan payment of $___ represents ~__% of cash flow; projected DSCR = __.”  

### b) Company overview
- Legal structure: Michigan LLC, ownership breakdown
- Location: Westland, MI
- History: when formed, key milestones (first contracts, website launches, tools built)
- Your background: highlight your experience in labor contracting plus your tech/AI edge (this is a differentiator).[7][1]

### c) Market analysis
Focus on:
- Local demand for labor contracting (construction, industrial, maintenance)
- Your niche: small businesses that need both labor and better back-office/AI tools
- Competitors: other labor contractors, staffing agencies
- Your edge: faster quoting, AI scheduling, automated invoicing, etc.[2][7]

### d) Products and services
- Labor contracting services (types of jobs, pricing model: hourly, per-project, etc.)
- AI/backend tools: what you sell or plan to sell (e.g., scheduling, job tracking, estimating tools)
- Margins: rough gross margin on labor vs. tools
- Any recurring revenue (maintenance contracts, tool subscriptions)[2]

### e) Marketing and sales
- How you get jobs: referrals, local networks, online presence, partnerships
- Pipeline: list of likely/quoted jobs with expected value and timing
- Plans to scale: more outreach, targeted ads, partnerships with GCs or facilities managers[1][2]

### f) Organization and management
- You as owner/operator: summarize your experience in labor + tech
- Any key contractors/subcontractors you rely on
- If you plan to hire, outline roles and timing (e.g., additional laborers, part-time admin)[8][7]

### g) Financials and projections
Include:
- Historical P&L (if available) or a clear “startup to date” summary
- 12–24 month projections:
  - Revenue by stream (labor, tools)
  - Direct costs (labor, materials, software)
  - Operating expenses
  - Net profit
- Cash flow forecast showing:
  - Existing obligations
  - Proposed loan payment
  - Resulting cash buffer[3][5]

Lenders especially care about **cash flow** and **Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)**:  
- DSCR = (Annual net operating income) / (Annual debt service)  
- Many lenders like to see DSCR ≥ 1.25.[1][3]

### h) Funding request and use of funds
Be specific:
- “Requesting $75,000 term loan, 5-year term.”
- Use of funds:
  - $45,000 – used truck
  - $15,000 – pull-behind trailer
  - $10,000 – initial operating capital (fuel, insurance, payroll buffer, marketing)
  - $5,000 – software/tools to scale admin/AI services
- How this drives revenue:
  - More jobs per week due to mobility and equipment
  - Ability to take larger contracts
  - Sell more backend tooling as you scale[1][2]

### i) Collateral and guarantees
- List assets you can pledge (truck/trailer, equipment, receivables)
- Note that you’re willing to provide a personal guarantee (common for small LLC loans)[6][7]

## 4) Strengthen your profile before applying

Lenders look at a few key areas:

**Credit**
- Pull your **personal credit report**; fix errors, reduce high-utilization cards if possible.
- If you have a business credit file (Dun & Bradstreet, Experian Business), check it too.
- Scores above ~680 personal and strong business credit improve terms.[4][5]

**Cash flow and bank statements**
- Avoid large, unexplained deposits/withdrawals right before applying.
- Show consistent inflows from contracts or clients.[4][3]

**Legal/compliance**
- Ensure licenses, taxes, and any required registrations in Michigan are current.[4]

***

## 5) Choose the right type of lender

Given you’re an LLC in labor contracting with some tech angle, consider:

- **Local banks and credit unions** (Michigan-based):  
  Often better for relationship-based small business loans and equipment financing.
- **SBA 7(a) or 504 loans** (via local SBA lenders):  
  Good for equipment + working capital, longer terms, lower rates, but more documentation.
- **Online small-business lenders**:  
  Faster, sometimes easier approval, but higher rates; useful if you need speed or have shorter history.
- **Equipment financing companies**:  
  Specifically for the truck/trailer; the equipment itself often serves as collateral.

Ask each:
- Minimum time in business
- Minimum revenue
- Required credit score
- Typical rates/fees
- Whether they fund LLCs in labor contracting and equipment purchases[3][4]

***

## 6) Prepare your “ask” and pitch

When you meet a lender (in person, video, or email), have:

- A **1-page summary**:
  - Business description
  - Loan amount and purpose
  - Key financial highlights (revenue, cash flow, DSCR)
  - Collateral and guarantees
- Your **business plan** (PDF)
- A simple **slide or one-pager** showing:
  - Current setup
  - What the loan enables (e.g., “+X jobs/week”, “+Y revenue/month”)
  - Repayment plan

Verbally, hit:
- Why this loan now (specific opportunities/contracts)
- How you’ve managed money so far
- Your experience and why you’re a low-risk borrower[2][1]
