# Rigging Hardware Identification and Safety Inspection
## Course Development — Lesson Content Draft

---

### Lesson 1: Introduction to Rigging Hardware

---

#### Lesson Overview

Every suspended load at a live event — whether a lighting truss, speaker array, or scenic element — is only as safe as the hardware holding it. This lesson introduces the foundational components of rigging systems, establishes the language professionals use to discuss them, and sets the non-negotiable baseline for how this equipment must be treated.

---

#### What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Identify the core components of a rigging system and explain the function of each
- Distinguish between the primary sling materials used in live event rigging
- State the baseline safety rules that govern all rigging hardware regardless of type

---

#### Why This Matters in Live Events

Rigging is the discipline that puts everything in the air. Trusses, motors, chain hoists, and the hardware that connects them operate over audiences, crew, and performers every single show. There is no tolerance for equipment failure in this environment. Understanding what the hardware is, what it is made of, and what condition it must be in before it ever leaves the ground is the entry point for everything else in rigging safety.

---

#### Core Components of a Rigging System

A rigging system moves loads from the ground to the air — and keeps them there safely throughout the event. Each component in the system plays a specific role.

**Slings** are the connecting element between a load and the lifting equipment. They wrap, choke, or basket around a load and transfer its weight to the hoist or crane. Slings are not decorative or secondary — they are a primary structural component.

**Shackles** are load-rated connectors used to join slings, hardware, and structural elements. They come in various patterns (bow, dee) and are rated for specific capacities.

**Hoists** — including chain motors commonly used in live events — are the mechanical devices that raise and lower loads. They are rated for specific capacities and must never be operated beyond those limits.

**Cranes** are machines designed for lifting, lowering, and moving loads horizontally. In venue and outdoor festival settings, cranes may be used during load-in for structural steel, roof systems, or large scenic elements.

---

#### Sling Materials: Not All Slings Are the Same

OSHA recognizes four primary sling material categories, each with distinct characteristics, applications, and limitations:

| Material | Key Characteristic | Notable Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| Alloy Steel Chain | High strength, durable, resists heat | Heavy; requires careful inspection for stretch |
| Wire Rope | Flexible, strong, widely used | Susceptible to crushing, kinking, broken wires |
| Synthetic Web (nylon/polyester) | Lightweight, wide bearing surface, load-friendly | Vulnerable to cuts, abrasion, UV, and chemicals |
| Metal Mesh | Abrasion-resistant, conforms to load shape | Less flexible than other types |

**Critical point:** These materials are not interchangeable. A synthetic web sling suitable for a delicate curved surface has entirely different temperature tolerances and failure modes than an alloy steel chain. Treating them as equivalent is a foundational error — and a dangerous one.

---

#### Baseline Safety Rules

These rules apply to all rigging hardware, all the time, without exception:

1. **Damaged or defective equipment must be immediately removed from service.** There is no "use it carefully" option. The moment a piece of hardware shows signs of damage, wear, or defect, it comes out of the system.

2. **Slings and hardware must never be loaded beyond their rated capacities.** A rating is not a suggestion or an approximation. It is the maximum safe load under specified conditions. Exceeding it creates conditions for catastrophic failure.

---

#### Key Terms

**Sling** — A flexible assembly used to connect a load to a hoist or crane. Slings may be made from chain, wire rope, synthetic web, or metal mesh.

**Shackle** — A U-shaped load-rated connector used to link components in a rigging system. Available in bow and dee configurations.

**Hoist** — A mechanical device used to raise and lower loads. In live events, electric chain hoists (chain motors) are the most common type.

**Crane** — A machine used for lifting, lowering, and moving loads horizontally.

---

#### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Assuming all slings and connectors are interchangeable.** Different sling materials have fundamentally different strengths, weaknesses, and environmental tolerances. A synthetic web sling rated for a given load does not behave the same way as an alloy steel chain rated for the same number — particularly in high-heat environments, in contact with abrasive edges, or when chemical exposure is a factor. Knowing the material is knowing half the story.

### Lesson 2: Understanding Equipment Ratings and Identification

---

#### Lesson Overview

Rigging hardware carries information directly on its body — stamped, tagged, or engraved markings that communicate exactly how much it can safely hold and under what conditions. This lesson covers how to find those markings, how to read them accurately, and how the geometry of a lift changes the math of what a piece of hardware can actually carry.

---

#### What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Locate and interpret identification markings on slings and shackles
- Define Working Load Limit and explain how it governs safe rigging practice
- Calculate the capacity reduction caused by angular loading on shackles

---

#### Why This Matters in Live Events

Live event rigging rarely involves a perfectly straight vertical pull. Bridles run at angles. Shackles get loaded off-axis. Every time the geometry of a lift deviates from vertical, the actual capacity of the hardware decreases — sometimes dramatically. A rigger who cannot read the WLL stamped on a shackle, or who does not understand that a 90-degree loading cuts that number in half, is operating with incomplete and dangerous information.

---

#### Identification Markings: What the Hardware Tells You

All rigging equipment must carry permanently affixed, legible markings that identify its safe working capacity. These markings are not optional, and hardware without them cannot be used.

**On shackles specifically, the following information must be present:**

- Manufacturer's name or trademark
- Rated load (Working Load Limit)
- Size designation

The **shackle pin** must additionally show the grade, material type, or load rating. This matters because pins are replaced in the field — a mismatched pin is a failure point.

If any of these markings are missing or illegible, the hardware is immediately out of service. Period.

---

#### Working Load Limit (WLL): The Number That Governs Everything

The **Working Load Limit** is the maximum load a piece of hardware is designed to support under specific, stated conditions. It is established by the manufacturer, verified through testing, and stamped or tagged on the equipment.

WLL is not a suggestion. It is not a starting point for negotiation. It is the ceiling — and that ceiling assumes the load is applied correctly under the conditions specified.

When conditions change — particularly when the angle of loading changes — the effective WLL changes with it.

---

#### Angle of Loading: When Geometry Reduces Capacity

A shackle loaded straight down from its bow (at 0 degrees from vertical) performs at its full rated capacity. The moment force is applied at an angle, capacity drops.

The established reduction factors are:

| Loading Angle from Vertical | Capacity Reduction | Effective Capacity |
|---|---|---|
| 0° (straight vertical) | None | 100% of WLL |
| 45° | 30% reduction | 70% of WLL |
| 90° (horizontal / side-loaded) | 50% reduction | 50% of WLL |

**Practical example:** A shackle rated at 1,000 kg loaded at 45 degrees from vertical effectively carries a maximum of 700 kg. Loaded at 90 degrees — a dead side-pull — that same shackle is only safely rated to 500 kg.

This is not a theoretical edge case. Bridle angles, truss connections, and motor pick points regularly create off-axis loading in live event environments. Every rigger needs to understand this math before touching a shackle in a working system.

---

#### Key Terms

**Working Load Limit (WLL) / Rated Capacity** — The maximum load in force, torque, stress, or other units that a piece of rigging hardware is authorized to support under specific conditions, as established by the manufacturer.

**Angle of Loading** — The angle between the direction of applied force and the centerline axis of the rigging hardware. As the angle increases from vertical, the effective capacity decreases.

---

#### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Assuming WLL stays constant regardless of how force is applied.** A shackle's rating assumes a specific load direction. Apply force off-axis and that rating drops — significantly. Beginners also frequently confuse the *side-load angle on a shackle* (how force is applied to that single piece of hardware) with the *included angle between two sling legs* in a bridle. These are related but distinct concepts that require separate understanding.

---

#### Safety Note

**Never substitute a shackle pin with a bolt, nail, or improvised fastener.** The pin is a load-bearing component engineered specifically for that shackle. Improvised replacements have unknown ratings, may not seat properly, and represent a serious failure risk under load.

### Lesson 3: Pre-Use Safety Inspection Procedures

---

#### Lesson Overview

Inspection is not a formality. It is a structured, legally defined process that must occur on a specific schedule and be performed by a person with verified authority to act on what they find. This lesson establishes the regulatory framework for rigging inspections and explains what each inspection type requires.

---

#### What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Identify the two OSHA/ASME-recognized inspection classifications and their required intervals
- Describe the role and authority of a Competent Person in rigging inspections
- List the key criteria evaluated during a pre-use inspection

---

#### Why This Matters in Live Events

Every load-in is time-compressed. Calls are long, crews are moving, and the pressure to get equipment in the air quickly is constant. That pressure does not change what the law requires — or what the physics demands. A sling that fails during a pick because no one checked it costs far more time, and potentially far more, than the five minutes a proper inspection takes. Inspection is a professional obligation, not an optional step.

---

#### The Two Inspection Classifications

OSHA and ASME recognize two formal inspection types for rigging equipment:

**Frequent Inspection**
- Interval: Daily to monthly
- Type: Visual check before each use
- Who performs it: A designated Competent Person
- Purpose: Identify damage, deformation, or defects that would make the equipment unsafe for the upcoming use

**Periodic Inspection**
- Interval: 1 to 12 months (based on usage and conditions)
- Type: Thorough, comprehensive inspection that goes beyond a visual check
- Who performs it: A designated Competent Person
- Documentation: Must be formally recorded — this is not a mental note, it is a written record

Both inspection types are mandatory. Frequent inspections protect the crew on the day. Periodic inspections catch cumulative wear and systemic issues that daily checks might not surface over time.

---

#### The Competent Person: A Legally Defined Role

The term "Competent Person" is not informal language for "an experienced crew member" or "whoever is available." Under OSHA standards, a Competent Person is specifically defined as an individual who:

- Has the knowledge and training to identify existing and predictable hazards
- Has the authority to take prompt corrective measures to eliminate those hazards

Both criteria must be met. Knowledge without authority does not satisfy the standard. A Competent Person is not just allowed to remove equipment from service — they are obligated to do so when a hazard is identified. Overriding that determination is a regulatory violation and a serious safety failure.

---

#### What a Frequent Inspection Covers

Before any sling or rigging attachment goes into use, the Competent Person must inspect for:

- **Visible deformation** — bending, twisting, or distortion of any component
- **Cracks** — in welds, links, hooks, or hardware bodies
- **Missing or illegible labels** — WLL tags and identification markings must be present and readable
- **Proper mechanical operation** — latches, hooks, and closures must function correctly

Any of these findings requires the equipment to be taken out of service immediately and tagged accordingly.

---

#### Key Terms

**Competent Person** — An OSHA-defined individual who possesses the knowledge and training to identify rigging hazards and holds the authority to take immediate corrective action, including removing equipment from service.

**Frequent Inspection** — A visual pre-use inspection of rigging equipment performed daily to monthly by a Competent Person before equipment is put into service.

**Periodic Inspection** — A thorough, documented inspection of rigging equipment conducted every 1 to 12 months by a Competent Person, designed to identify cumulative wear and systemic deficiencies.

---

#### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Treating a glance as an inspection.** A real inspection is a methodical, deliberate evaluation of specific criteria. Sweeping your eyes over a pile of chain on the way to the motor bag does not constitute a pre-use check.

**Misunderstanding the Competent Person designation.** This is not a title given to whoever has the most experience in the room. It is a defined role with specific authority. Periodic inspections must also be formally documented — a mental note or verbal confirmation does not satisfy the requirement.

### Lesson 4: Diagnosing Damage and Fatigue in Rigging Equipment

---

#### Lesson Overview

Knowing that an inspection is required is only the first step. The harder skill is knowing exactly what to look for — and understanding why specific types of damage are automatic removal criteria. This lesson provides the specific, measurable indicators of damage and fatigue in wire rope, alloy chain, and shackles.

---

#### What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Identify the specific removal criteria for wire rope, chain, and shackles by damage type
- Recognize heat damage indicators across metal rigging components
- Conduct a safe physical inspection of wire rope without risk of hand injury

---

#### Why This Matters in Live Events

Rigging hardware accumulates wear invisibly. A chain that has made a hundred picks looks nearly the same as one that has made ten. A wire rope with internal wire breaks may look intact from the outside. The signs of damage are specific and — in many cases — subtle. The standard exists precisely because a visual estimate is not sufficient. You need to know the threshold numbers and apply them consistently.

---

#### Wire Rope: Removal Criteria

Wire rope is one of the most common rigging materials in production environments. It is also one of the most diagnostic — its failure patterns follow recognizable rules.

**Remove wire rope from service immediately if:**

- There are **10 or more randomly distributed broken wires** in one rope lay
- There are **5 or more broken wires in a single strand** within one rope lay
- The rope shows **bird caging** — a condition where the outer strands flare outward, indicating internal structural failure
- There is evidence of **heat damage**, including weld spatter, arc burns, or blue discoloration of the metal

**Safe inspection technique:** Never drag bare hands along wire rope. Broken wires protrude outward and will puncture or lacerate skin. Use a rag or gloved hand, move deliberately, and watch for snagging.

---

#### Alloy Chain: Removal Criteria

Chain is durable and high-strength, but it is not immune to damage — and its failure modes are distinct from wire rope.

**Remove alloy chain from service immediately if:**

- There is **excessive wear** visible on chain links
- Any links show **stretching or elongation** — a sign that the chain has been stressed beyond its elastic limit
- There are **nicks or gouges** in the link material — these create stress concentration points where cracks initiate
- There is evidence of **heat exposure** that may have compromised the metal's structural integrity

---

#### Shackles: Removal Criteria

Shackles are compact but carry significant load — and their failure criteria are tightly defined.

**Remove shackles from service immediately if:**

- There is a **10% or greater reduction** in the original cross-sectional dimension at any point on the body or pin. If a shackle was originally 20mm at its thinnest point and now measures 18mm, it is out of service.
- The body shows **visible deformation, bending, or distortion**
- The **pin is stretched, bent, or will not seat properly**
- There is evidence of **heat damage**, including weld spatter or discoloration

---

#### Heat Damage: A Systemic Risk

Heat damage is particularly dangerous because it is not always visible from the outside — but its effects on metal integrity are severe. Two specific indicators to look for:

- **Weld spatter**: Small droplets of melted metal adhered to the surface from nearby welding operations. These indicate the hardware was exposed to extreme localized heat.
- **Bluing or discoloration**: A blue or straw-colored tinge in the metal indicates it was heated to a temperature that alters its temper and reduces its structural strength.

Any hardware showing either indicator must be removed from service regardless of its apparent physical condition.

---

#### Key Terms

**Bird Caging** — A wire rope defect in which the outer strands of the rope separate and flare outward, creating a birdcage-like appearance. This indicates internal structural failure and requires immediate removal from service.

**Rope Lay** — The distance along the length of a rope over which one strand makes a complete spiral around the rope core. Used as the reference distance for counting broken wires during inspection.

---

#### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Dismissing minor visible damage as acceptable.** A small nick in a chain link, a slight stretch in a link, or a couple of broken wires in wire rope can appear insignificant. These are not cosmetic issues. They are indicators of localized stress concentration and progressive failure. The removal thresholds exist because what looks minor on the surface often reflects significant internal damage.

### Lesson 5: Adhering to Manufacturer Specifications for Safety

---

#### Lesson Overview

Manufacturer specifications define the operating envelope of every piece of rigging hardware — not just how much it holds, but under what conditions it can safely be used. This lesson covers the environmental and temperature limits that govern different sling types, and establishes the strict protocols that govern what happens when hardware needs repair.

---

#### What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- State the temperature thresholds at which specific sling and hardware types must be removed from service
- Explain why field repairs on load-bearing rigging hardware are prohibited
- Describe the only acceptable repair and return-to-service process for damaged rigging equipment

---

#### Why This Matters in Live Events

Outdoor festivals present conditions that indoor arena shows do not: direct sun exposure, heat from generators and production equipment, weather extremes, and ambient temperatures that affect hardware performance. Touring shows move equipment through repeated thermal cycles. In these environments, knowing the limits of each material type is an operational requirement. Similarly, the pressure to keep gear in service during a long run can create the temptation to "fix it in the field" — a practice that is both prohibited and genuinely dangerous.

---

#### Temperature Limits by Material Type

Different sling and hardware materials have specific temperature thresholds beyond which they must be permanently removed from service:

**Fiber-Core Wire Rope Slings**
- Must be removed from service if exposed to temperatures **above 200°F (93°C)**
- Fiber cores provide cushioning and lubrication but are highly vulnerable to heat degradation

**Alloy Steel Chain**
- Must be **permanently removed** if heated above **1,000°F (538°C)**
- At this temperature, the metallurgical properties of the steel are irreversibly altered — the chain may look intact but its structural integrity cannot be trusted or restored

These thresholds are manufacturer-established and OSHA-referenced. They are not approximations. Do not estimate, average, or speculate about temperature exposure — if there is doubt about whether a piece of equipment was exposed to damaging heat, it comes out of service.

---

#### Field Repairs: Prohibited Without Exception

The appeal of a field repair is understandable. A sling is compromised, a replacement is not immediately available, the show is in eight hours. The answer is still no.

OSHA standards and manufacturer guidelines both prohibit makeshift repairs on load-bearing rigging hardware. Specifically:

- **Shortening slings with knots** is prohibited. Knots create stress concentration points and dramatically reduce capacity in ways that cannot be rated or predicted.
- **Using bolts or improvised pins** in place of rated hardware is prohibited. A standard bolt has no rigging rating. Its failure mode under dynamic loading is unpredictable.
- **Unauthorized welding or modification** is prohibited. Welding introduces heat that destroys the heat-treatment of the base metal, alters its grain structure, and voids any manufacturer rating. It also violates OSHA standards regardless of the skill of the person performing it.

---

#### The Only Acceptable Repair Process

When rigging hardware is damaged and repair may be possible, the correct process is:

1. Remove the equipment from service immediately
2. Return it to the **manufacturer or an equivalent entity** — a facility with the technical capability and authority to assess, repair, and recertify the specific equipment type
3. **Proof test** the repaired equipment before it is returned to service

A proof test verifies that the repaired hardware can withstand the loads it will be asked to carry. Without it, even a structurally sound-looking repair has no verified rating.

---

#### Key Terms

**Proof Test** — A load test applied to repaired or recertified rigging equipment to verify that it can safely bear its rated capacity before being returned to service.

**Equivalent Entity** — A repair facility with the technical capability, equipment, and authorization to repair and recertify rigging hardware to manufacturer or industry standards.

---

#### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Believing field repairs are acceptable if performed carefully.** This is the most dangerous misconception in this area. There is no version of "welding a cracked hook in the field" or "replacing a load pin with a grade-8 bolt" that is acceptable. These actions are prohibited — not because the industry lacks trust in skilled people, but because the resulting hardware has no verifiable rating and its failure profile is unknown.

### Lesson 6: Designing a Safety Inspection Checklist for Live Events

---

#### Lesson Overview

The rules covered in previous lessons mean nothing if they are not applied consistently in the field. A well-designed inspection checklist converts regulatory requirements and damage criteria into a repeatable, daily workflow that produces consistent results regardless of crew fatigue, time pressure, or venue. This lesson covers how to build and use an effective rigging inspection checklist — with specific attention to the environmental conditions that make live event rigging more demanding than warehouse or controlled-environment applications.

---

#### What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Identify the required elements of a rigging inspection checklist
- Apply automatic failure criteria to checklist design
- Account for live event-specific environmental factors in inspection protocols

---

#### Why This Matters in Live Events

Consistency is the enemy of human error. A crew chief who relies on memory to inspect gear will miss things — not because they are careless, but because memory is degraded by fatigue, distraction, and time pressure. All three of those factors are constants in live event production. A checklist removes the cognitive load from the inspection process and makes the criteria explicit, documented, and defensible.

---

#### What a Rigging Inspection Checklist Must Include

An effective inspection checklist for live event rigging should address the following categories for each piece of hardware:

**Identification and Documentation**
- Is the WLL/rated capacity tag present and legible?
- Is the manufacturer identification marking present?
- Does the shackle pin match the shackle body (manufacturer-matched)?
- Is there a current certification record on file for this equipment?

**Physical Condition — Metal Hardware (Shackles, Chain, Hooks)**
- Any visible deformation, bending, or distortion?
- Any cracks — in welds, at pin holes, in link bodies?
- Any wear exceeding 10% reduction of original dimension?
- Any evidence of heat damage (weld spatter, arc burns, discoloration)?
- Does the pin thread and seat fully and correctly?

**Physical Condition — Wire Rope**
- Broken wire count: Fewer than 10 randomly distributed wires in one lay? Fewer than 5 in a single strand?
- Any bird caging or core protrusion visible?
- Any kinks, crushing, or evidence of overloading?
- Any heat damage indicators?

**Physical Condition — Synthetic Slings**
- Any cuts, tears, or abrasion through the webbing?
- Any chemical contamination, UV bleaching, or discoloration?
- Labels intact and legible?

**Mechanical Function**
- Do hooks, latches, and safety catches operate freely and seat positively?
- Does the shackle pin turn freely without binding?

---

#### Automatic Failure Criteria

Some checklist findings are immediate, non-negotiable disqualifiers. Hardware that triggers any of these must be removed from service before any lift:

- **Missing or illegible WLL/identification tag** — no exceptions, regardless of apparent physical condition
- **Any visible crack** in a load-bearing component
- **10% or greater dimension reduction** on shackles
- **10 or more broken wires** in one rope lay (wire rope)
- **Evidence of heat damage** anywhere on the assembly
- **Deformed, stretched, or mismatched pin** on a shackle

The physical condition of the metal is irrelevant if the tag is missing. If there is no legible WLL marking, there is no verifiable rated capacity — and the hardware cannot be used.

---

#### Accounting for Live Event Environments

Indoor warehouse storage and climate-controlled facilities represent the best-case scenario for rigging hardware. Live events routinely present conditions that accelerate wear and introduce damage risks:

- **Moisture and humidity** — accelerates corrosion in steel hardware and degrades synthetic slings
- **Rapid temperature changes** — particularly outdoor festivals where equipment goes from cold nights to direct sun exposure, affecting metal fatigue rates
- **Outdoor weather exposure** — rain, ice, and UV exposure over multi-day festival runs
- **Ground contact and transport abuse** — equipment loaded into trucks, dragged across concrete, and staged on gravel is subject to wear that warehouse-stored gear never sees
- **Heat from production sources** — generators, LED arrays, and other high-output equipment can create ambient heat conditions that approach sling temperature limits

Checklist design for touring and festival environments should reflect this reality. Inspection frequency should be calibrated to actual exposure conditions, not default minimum intervals.

---

#### Key Terms

**Certification Record** — The formal documentation associated with a piece of rigging hardware that records its inspection history, proof test results, and any repairs or recertification performed. Required for periodic inspections.

---

#### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Designing a checklist for ideal conditions and applying it to festival reality.** A checklist built around indoor theatre or arena work may underestimate the inspection requirements for a five-day outdoor festival where equipment is exposed to moisture, temperature swings, and rough handling every day. The checklist is only useful if it accounts for the actual operating environment.

### Lesson 7: Conclusion and Course Summary

---

#### Lesson Overview

The details covered across this course — materials, ratings, inspection intervals, damage criteria, temperature limits, and repair protocols — connect to a single principle: rigging safety depends on human behavior. Equipment does not fail spontaneously. It fails because warning signs were missed, tolerances were exceeded, or shortcuts were taken. This final lesson ties the technical content together and establishes the professional mindset that every rigger must carry into every call.

---

#### What You Will Learn

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Connect the technical standards covered throughout the course to a coherent safety culture
- Explain the concept of shock loading and why dynamic loads exceed static ratings
- Articulate the professional obligation to act on inspection findings, not defer them

---

#### Why This Matters in Live Events

The equipment is in the air over people. Not hypothetically — over real audiences, crew members, and performers, every show. The technical standards in this course are not bureaucratic requirements invented to complicate operations. They exist because rigging failures at live events have caused serious injuries and deaths, and the analysis of those failures consistently returns to the same finding: human error. Inspections missed. Damage overlooked. Tolerances pushed. The course content you have just completed is the practical countermeasure to each of those failure modes.

---

#### The Human Error Factor

Research into crane and rigging accidents consistently attributes **up to 90% of failures to human error** — not to spontaneous material failure or unforeseeable circumstances. The most common mechanism: early warning signs of equipment distress were present and were not acted on.

This is not an indictment of anyone's character. It is a finding about how unstructured environments and time pressure affect human judgment. The protocols in this course — written checklists, defined inspection intervals, mandatory removal criteria, the Competent Person framework — exist to compensate for the entirely predictable ways that human attention degrades under real working conditions.

---

#### Understanding Shock Loading

Static load ratings describe what a piece of hardware can hold when force is applied gradually and consistently. Real rigging environments rarely produce that ideal condition.

**Shock loading** occurs when a load is applied suddenly — a motor that engages abruptly, a load that drops and jerks to a stop, a sling that catches a moving piece of scenery. In these events, the dynamic force applied to the hardware can vastly exceed the static weight of the load. A 500 kg load that stops suddenly can generate forces several times that weight in a fraction of a second.

This is why the rated capacity of hardware is not a comfortable margin — it is a ceiling, and shock loading can breach that ceiling with a single event. Rigging systems must be operated smoothly, deliberately, and with full awareness that dynamic forces are always a factor.

#### The Professional Standard

When in doubt about a piece of rigging hardware — remove it from service. This is not a conservative posture or an abundance of caution. It is the correct professional response to uncertainty in a life-safety system. A questionable shackle is never worth the pick. Retire the gear, document it, and replace it.

The goal of this course is not to produce riggers who know the rules. It is to produce riggers who understand why the rules exist — and who apply them consistently, even under pressure, even at the end of a long call, even when no one is watching.

That is what a safe rigging culture looks like. That is the standard.

---

#### Key Terms

**Shock Loading** — The sudden, rapid application of force to rigging equipment, typically caused by abrupt engagement of loads, dropped loads that jerk to a stop, or dynamic movement during a lift. Shock loading can generate forces that significantly exceed the static weight of the load and may cause immediate failure of hardware that appears to be within its rated capacity.

---

#### Common Beginner Mistakes

**Treating inspection as a setup task rather than a daily practice.** Rigging inspection is not something that happens once at the start of a run and is then considered complete. It is a pre-use requirement for every shift, every day. The load you inspected yesterday was moved, transported, and handled overnight. Inspect it again.

     8	# Reading Uploaded Files
     9	
    10	## Why this skill exists
    11	
    12	When a user uploads a file in claude.ai, Claude Desktop, or Cowork,
    13	the file is written to `/mnt/user-data/uploads/<filename>` and you are told the path
    14	in an `<uploaded_files>` block. **The content is not in your context.**
    15	You must go read it.
    16	
    17	The naive thing — `cat /mnt/user-data/uploads/whatever` — is wrong for
    18	most files:
    19	
    20	- On a PDF it prints binary garbage.
    21	- On a 100MB CSV it floods your context with rows you will never use.
    22	- On a DOCX it prints the raw ZIP bytes.
    23	- On an image it does nothing useful at all.
    24	
    25	This skill tells you the right first move for each type, and when to
    26	hand off to a deeper skill.
    27	
    28	## General protocol
    29	
    30	1. **Look at the extension.** That is your dispatch key.
    31	2. **Stat before you read.** Large files need sampling, not slurping.
    32	   ```bash
    33	   stat -c '%s bytes, %y' /mnt/user-data/uploads/report.pdf
    34	   file /mnt/user-data/uploads/report.pdf
    35	   ```
    36	3. **Read just enough to answer the user's question.** If they asked
    37	   "how many rows are in this CSV", don't load the whole thing into
    38	   pandas — `wc -l` gives a fast approximation (it counts newlines,
    39	   not CSV records, so it may over-count if quoted fields contain
    40	   embedded newlines).
    41	4. **If a dedicated skill exists, go read it.** The table below tells
    42	   you when. The dedicated skills cover editing, creating, and advanced
    43	   operations that this skill does not.
    44	
    45	## `extract-text`
    46	
    47	For docx, odt, epub, xlsx, pptx, rtf, and ipynb the first move is
    48	`extract-text <file>`. It emits markdown for docx/odt/epub (headings,
    49	bold, lists, links, tables), tab-separated rows under `## Sheet:`
    50	headers for xlsx, text under `## Slide N` headers for pptx, fenced
    51	code cells for ipynb, and plain text for rtf. Pass `--format <fmt>`
    52	when the extension is wrong or absent (e.g., `--format xlsx` on an
    53	`.xlsm`). If it errors on a file, `pandoc <file> -t plain` is a
    54	fallback; for xlsx/pptx, fall back to the dedicated skill's
    55	Python-based approach (openpyxl / python-pptx).

    83	## PDF
    84	
    85	**Never** `cat` a PDF — it prints binary garbage.
    86	
    87	Quick first move — get the page count and determine whether the PDF
    88	has an extractable text layer:
    89	
    90	```bash
    91	pdfinfo /mnt/user-data/uploads/report.pdf
    92	pdffonts /mnt/user-data/uploads/report.pdf
    93	```
    94	
    95	`pdffonts` tells you whether text extraction will work before you try it:
    96	
    97	- **No fonts listed** (empty table, just the header) → the PDF is a
    98	  scan or raster export. `pdftotext` and `PdfReader.extract_text()`
    99	  will return nothing useful. Go straight to page rasterization or OCR
   100	  — see `/mnt/skills/public/pdf-reading/SKILL.md` → "Scanned
   101	  documents".
   102	- **Fonts listed** → there is a text layer; extract it:
   103	  ```bash
   104	  pdftotext -f 1 -l 1 /mnt/user-data/uploads/report.pdf - | head -20
   105	  ```
   106	
   107	The reason to check `pdffonts` first is user-facing: running
   108	`pdftotext` on a scan produces an empty result, and in a visible
   109	transcript that reads as a failed first attempt before you fall back
   110	to OCR. The two-line diagnostic above costs one tool call and avoids
   111	that — you arrive at the right method on the first try, which is what
   112	a user perceives as "it just read my file."
   113	
   114	That also shapes how to open your reply. The diagnostic commands are
   115	plumbing, not content; lead with what the user asked about. On a
   116	scanned receipt that might be "This is a 3-page scanned invoice; the
   117	amount due on page 2 is $1,845.00," and on a digitally-authored report
   118	it might be "The Q3 report runs 28 pages; revenue on p. 4 is $12.3M,
   119	up 9% YoY." What you're steering away from is the "I'll examine the
   120	PDF" / "Let me check if this is extractable" preamble — the answer to
   121	their question is the first thing they should see.
   122	
   123	For anything beyond a quick peek — figures, tables, attachments,
   124	forms, scanned PDFs, visual inspection, or choosing a reading strategy
   125	— go read `/mnt/skills/public/pdf-reading/SKILL.md`. It covers
   126	content inventory, text extraction vs. page rasterization, embedded
   127	content extraction, and document-type-aware reading strategies.
   128	
   129	For PDF form filling, creation, merging, splitting, or watermarking,
   130	go read `/mnt/skills/public/pdf/SKILL.md`.

   150	The `xlsx` skill covers formulas, formatting, charts, creating. Read
   151	it if you need any of those. For a quick look at an `.xlsx`:
   152	
   153	```bash
   154	extract-text /mnt/user-data/uploads/data.xlsx | head -100
   155	```
   156	
   157	For `.xlsm`, add `--format xlsx` (same zip structure; only the
   158	extension differs). When you need a structured preview in Python:
   159	
   160	```python
   161	from openpyxl import load_workbook
   162	wb = load_workbook("/mnt/user-data/uploads/data.xlsx", read_only=True)
   163	print("Sheets:", wb.sheetnames)
   164	ws = wb.active
   165	for row in ws.iter_rows(max_row=5, values_only=True):
   166	    print(row)
   167	```
   168	
   169	`read_only=True` matters — without it, openpyxl loads the entire
   170	workbook into memory, which breaks on large files. Do not trust
   171	`ws.max_row` in read-only mode: many non-Excel writers omit the
   172	dimension record, so it comes back `None` or wrong. If you need a row
   173	count, iterate or use pandas.
   174	
   175	**Legacy `.xls`** — openpyxl raises `InvalidFileException`. Use:
   176	
   177	```python
   178	import pandas as pd
   179	df = pd.read_excel("/mnt/user-data/uploads/old.xls", engine="xlrd", nrows=5)
   180	```
   181	
   182	**`.ods` (OpenDocument)** — openpyxl also rejects this. Use:
   183	
   184	```python
   185	import pandas as pd
   186	df = pd.read_excel("/mnt/user-data/uploads/data.ods", engine="odf", nrows=5)
   187	```

   292	## Archives (ZIP / TAR / TAR.GZ)
   293	
   294	**List first. Extract never — unless the user explicitly asks.**
   295	Archives can be huge, contain path traversal, or nest forever.
   296	
   297	```bash
   298	unzip -l /mnt/user-data/uploads/bundle.zip
   299	tar -tf /mnt/user-data/uploads/bundle.tar
   300	```
   301	
   302	GNU tar auto-detects compression — `tar -tf` works on `.tar`,
   303	`.tar.gz`, `.tar.bz2`, `.tar.xz` alike. Don't hard-code `-z`.
   304	
   305	If the user wants one file from inside, extract just that one:
   306	
   307	```bash
   308	unzip -p /mnt/user-data/uploads/bundle.zip path/inside/file.txt
   309	```
   310	
   311	**Standalone `.gz`** (not a tar) compresses a single file — there is
   312	no manifest to list. Just peek at the decompressed content:
   313	
   314	```bash
   315	zcat /mnt/user-data/uploads/data.json.gz | head -50
   316	```

   381	### Critical Rules for docx-js
   382	
   383	- **Set page size explicitly** - docx-js defaults to A4; use US Letter (12240 x 15840 DXA) for US documents

   169	#### Subscripts and Superscripts
   170	
   171	**IMPORTANT**: Never use Unicode subscript/superscript characters (₀₁₂₃₄₅₆₇₈₉, ⁰¹²³⁴⁵⁶⁷⁸⁹) in ReportLab PDFs. The built-in fonts do not include these glyphs, causing them to render as solid black boxes.
   172	
   173	Instead, use ReportLab's XML markup tags in Paragraph objects:
   174	```python
   175	from reportlab.platypus import Paragraph
   176	from reportlab.lib.styles import getSampleStyleSheet
   177	
   178	styles = getSampleStyleSheet()
   179	
   180	# Subscripts: use <sub> tag
   181	chemical = Paragraph("H<sub>2</sub>O", styles['Normal'])
   182	
   183	# Superscripts: use <super> tag
   184	squared = Paragraph("x<super>2</super> + y<super>2</super>", styles['Normal'])
   185	```
   186	
   187	For canvas-drawn text (not Paragraph objects), manually adjust font the size and position rather than using Unicode subscripts/superscripts.
