# Lesson 1: Introduction to Bridle Math and Angle Tension

---

## Lesson Overview

**Course:** Bridle Math & Angle Tension
**Lesson:** 1 of 9
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Audience:** Stagehands and riggers — festival and event production environments

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Explain why rigging and production safety depend on more than just knowing the load weight
- Describe what a bridle configuration is and how it differs from a simple vertical hang
- Identify how sling angle affects tension in each bridle leg
- Recognize the safety culture behaviors that prevent rigging incidents in high-pressure production environments

---

## Why This Lesson Matters

Every festival rig, truss hang, and production lift carries a hidden variable that doesn't show up on a weight ticket: **the force your angle creates.**

A 1,000 lb load doesn't automatically put 500 lb on each sling leg. Depending on how flat or steep your bridle legs are, that same load could be putting 700, 900, or even 1,000 lb of tension on each leg — while looking perfectly normal from the ground.

Bridle math is how you see what's actually happening inside your rigging system. It is not an advanced specialty skill reserved for engineers. It is a **fundamental jobsite competency** for anyone working in a production environment where loads are suspended overhead.

---

## What Is a Bridle Configuration?

A **bridle** is a rigging arrangement using two or more sling legs connected to a common load, typically meeting at a master link or shackle above the load. The legs splay outward and downward to reach the pick points.

Compare that to a **vertical hang**, where a single line runs straight down from the hoist to the load — no angle, no spread.

| Configuration | Sling Legs | Angle Effect | Tension per Leg |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vertical hang | 1 | None — straight down | Full load weight |
| Symmetrical bridle | 2+ | Increases with shallower angle | Depends on angle |

The critical difference: in a vertical hang, physics is simple. In a bridle, **angle changes everything.**

---

## The Core Concept: Angle Drives Tension

In a symmetrical two-leg bridle, tension in each leg is calculated as:

$$T = \frac{W}{2 \sin(\theta)}$$

Where:
- $T$ = tension in each sling leg
- $W$ = total load weight
- $\theta$ = sling angle measured from horizontal

Here's what that looks like with a **1,000 lb load**:

| Sling Angle (from horizontal) | Tension Per Leg | What Changed? |
|---|---|---|
| 90° (straight down) | 500 lb | Baseline — vertical |
| 60° | 577 lb | +15% over vertical |
| 45° | 707 lb | +41% over vertical |
| 30° | 1,000 lb | Each leg carries the full load |
| Below 30° | Exceeds full load | Rapidly dangerous |

> **Key takeaway:** The load weight never changed. The angle did. That's the whole lesson.

Nothing about the weight ticket, the gear list, or the visual appearance of the rig tells you what the legs are actually seeing. Only the math does.

---

## What the Math Doesn't Automatically Tell You

The formula above assumes a **perfect system** — equal leg lengths, a centered load, and a known center of gravity. In real festival and event production, those conditions are rarely guaranteed.

Watch for these real-world complications:

- **Unequal leg lengths** shift more force into one leg than the other
- **Off-center loads** cause the load to hang tilted, rotating tension unevenly between legs
- **Misread center of gravity** means the pick point isn't actually over the balance point
- **Dynamic forces** — sudden starts, stops, wind, snagging, or shock loading — add force beyond the static calculation

Every component in the system — the sling, hook, shackle, master link, hoist, lift point, and structural attachment — must be rated for the **actual tension in that leg**, not just the load weight. The weakest rated component controls the lift.

---

## Standards That Define the Baseline

Bridle math gives you the predicted force. Standards define the **minimum safe practice** for acting on it. Two regulatory frameworks apply directly to this work:

**OSHA (Enforceable Law)**
- *29 CFR 1910.184* — Slings: covers use, inspection, and removal from service
- *29 CFR 1926.251* — Rigging equipment for material handling in construction
- *29 CFR 1926 Subpart CC* — Cranes and derricks, qualified riggers, signalpersons

**ANSI/ASME (Industry Consensus Standards)**
- *ASME B30.9* — Slings: fabrication, marking, use, inspection
- *ASME B30.26* — Rigging hardware: shackles, links, rings, swivels
- *ASME B30.10* — Hooks: inspection, use, removal criteria

> **How to think about these:** OSHA sets the legal floor. ASME/ANSI standards describe how the industry defines safe practice in detail. Both matter. Neither replaces the other.

---

## Safety Culture: The Layer Math Can't Cover

Research on crane and lifting incidents consistently points to the same finding: **most incidents involve a combination of technical, planning, and human factors** — not a single isolated mistake.

Knowing the formula isn't enough if the crew doesn't ask the right questions before the lift.

Before any bridle lift, competent riggers ask:

- **"Do we know the actual load weight?"**
- **"Do we know where the center of gravity is?"**
- **"What is the sling angle, and have we calculated leg tension?"**
- **"Are all components tagged, inspected, and rated for the actual load?"**
- **"Who has authority to stop this lift?"**
- **"Has anything about this lift plan changed since we reviewed it?"**

These are not bureaucratic checkboxes. They are the difference between a production-pressure shortcut that works 99 times and the one time it doesn't.

A strong safety culture treats bridle math as a **normal jobsite conversation** — not a specialist-only calculation, and not something that gets skipped because the clock is running.

# Lesson 2: Fundamentals of Angle Tension in Rigging

---

## Lesson Overview

**Course:** Bridle Math & Angle Tension
**Lesson:** 2 of 9
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Audience:** Stagehands and riggers — festival and event production environments

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Define sling angle, sling tension, and load angle factor
- Explain why sling tension increases as sling angle decreases
- Calculate sling-leg tension for a balanced two-leg bridle using the standard formula
- Use a load angle factor chart to verify results
- Recognize the difference between angle measured from horizontal vs. from vertical
- Identify when a rigging setup requires a qualified rigger, lift plan, or engineered review

---

## The One Rule That Runs This Lesson

Before the math, plant this:

> **The load weight stays the same. The sling tension changes with angle.**

That's the whole concept. Everything else in this lesson is just understanding *why* — and learning to calculate exactly *how much* the tension changes so you can make safe decisions on the deck.

---

## The Core Formula

For a balanced two-leg bridle, with the sling angle measured **from the horizontal**:

$$T = \frac{W}{2 \sin(\theta)}$$

Where:
- $T$ = tension in each sling leg
- $W$ = total load weight
- $\theta$ = sling angle from horizontal

For a bridle with more than two **equally loaded** legs:

$$T = \frac{W}{N \sin(\theta)}$$

Where $N$ = number of load-sharing sling legs.

> **Important:** In real three- and four-leg bridles, legs do not automatically share equally. Load distribution depends on sling length, load stiffness, center of gravity, and connection geometry. Never assume equal sharing without a system designed to equalize it.

---

## Why the Angle Changes the Tension

Each sling leg carries two force components at once:

$$\text{Vertical component} = T \sin(\theta)$$

$$\text{Horizontal component} = T \cos(\theta)$$

The **vertical components** are doing the actual work — holding the load up. For two legs to support the full load weight, their vertical components must add up to $W$:

$$2T\sin(\theta) = W$$

Solving for $T$:

$$T = \frac{W}{2\sin(\theta)}$$

The **horizontal components** aren't lifting anything. They pull inward on the load, the lift points, the shackles, and the hooks. At shallow angles, the horizontal force grows significantly — stressing hardware that may not be rated for that direction of loading.

---

## The Load Angle Factor Method

Many rigging references present the same math using a **load angle factor (LAF)** — a multiplier that tells you how much extra tension each leg carries compared to a straight vertical lift.

$$\text{Sling tension} = \left(\frac{W}{N}\right) \times \text{LAF}$$

$$\text{LAF} = \frac{1}{\sin(\theta)}$$

| Sling Angle (from horizontal) | Load Angle Factor | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| 90° | 1.000 | No penalty — same as vertical |
| 60° | 1.155 | Each leg carries 15.5% more |
| 45° | 1.414 | Each leg carries 41.4% more |
| 30° | 2.000 | Each leg carries double its share |

Use either method — they produce the same result. The LAF chart is useful in the field when you want a quick multiplier without punching through the full formula.

---

## The Tension Curve: Why Shallow Is Dangerous

The relationship between angle and tension is **not linear.** It stays relatively flat at steep angles, then climbs rapidly as the angle approaches horizontal.

For a **1,000 lb load** on a two-leg bridle:

| Sling Angle | Tension Per Leg |
|---|---|
| 90° | 500 lb |
| 75° | 518 lb |
| 60° | 577 lb |
| 45° | 707 lb |
| 30° | 1,000 lb |
| 20° | 1,462 lb |
| 15° | 1,932 lb |
| 10° | 2,879 lb |

Notice what happens below 30°. Tension doesn't just creep up — it accelerates. At 10°, a 1,000 lb load is putting nearly **3,000 lb** of tension on each sling leg.

> **Industry practice:** Many rigging programs treat **30° from horizontal as a practical minimum angle** for general lifts. Below that threshold, the lift typically requires a qualified rigger review or an engineered lift plan.

---

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Two-Leg Bridle at 45°

A 4,000 lb load. Two-leg symmetrical bridle. Sling angle: 45°.

$$T = \frac{4000}{2\sin(45°)} = \frac{4000}{1.414} \approx 2{,}828 \text{ lb per leg}$$

Every sling, shackle, hook, and lift point in this system must be rated for at least **2,828 lb** under these conditions.

---

### Example 2 — Same Load, Shallower Angle

Same 4,000 lb load. Angle dropped to 30°.

$$T = \frac{4000}{2\sin(30°)} = \frac{4000}{1.0} = 4{,}000 \text{ lb per leg}$$

The load didn't change. The angle did. Each leg is now carrying the **full load weight**.

---

### Example 3 — Same Load, Steeper Angle

Same 4,000 lb load. Angle increased to 60°.

$$T = \frac{4000}{2\sin(60°)} = \frac{4000}{1.732} \approx 2{,}309 \text{ lb per leg}$$

Steeper angle, lower tension. The slings are working more efficiently.

---

## A Critical Angle Measurement Warning

This formula uses the angle measured **from the horizontal.** Some references and on-site crews measure from **the vertical** instead. These are not the same number, and using the wrong one will produce a dangerously incorrect result.

If your angle is measured from vertical ($\alpha$), the formula becomes:

$$T = \frac{W}{2\cos(\alpha)}$$

**60° from horizontal = 30° from vertical.** Same physical geometry, completely different numbers in the formula. Always confirm which reference the measurement is taken from before calculating.

---

## What the Math Still Can't Tell You

The formula gives you accurate tension *if* your inputs are accurate. In production environments, those inputs are not always confirmed:

- **Load weight** — Is it actually known, or estimated?
- **Equal leg sharing** — Are the legs truly equal length and geometry?
- **Hardware ratings** — Is every component — sling, shackle, hook, lug, anchor — rated for the calculated tension at the actual loading angle and hitch type?
- **Dynamic forces** — Starting, stopping, snagging, wind, and shock loading can exceed your static calculation

> **When to escalate:** If the load weight is uncertain, the angle is below 30°, the load is asymmetrical, or you're unsure any component is rated for the calculated tension — stop and get a qualified rigger involved. OSHA 29 CFR 1926 Subpart CC and ASME B30.9 both establish requirements for qualified rigger involvement in critical lifting operations.

| When to escalate | Below 30°, unknown CG, unequal legs, or uncertain component ratings |

# Lesson 3: The Danger of Shallow Angles and Trigonometric Impact

---

## Lesson Overview

**Course:** Bridle Math & Angle Tension
**Lesson:** 3 of 9
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Audience:** Stagehands and riggers — festival and event production environments

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Explain why sling tension increases as the bridle angle decreases
- Identify the vertical and horizontal force components in each sling leg
- Use sine and cosine to calculate both components for a given angle
- Read a load angle factor table and apply it to a practical scenario
- Recognize the shallow angle danger zone and describe why 30° is a critical threshold
- Calculate sling tension and horizontal force for a real bridle scenario

---

## Start Here: The Geometry Problem

Shallow angles don't just *feel* riskier — they're mathematically more dangerous in a way that compounds quickly and non-obviously.

The load weight doesn't change. The geometry does. And geometry determines how much force your sling legs, shackles, hooks, and structural attachment points are actually seeing.

To understand *why*, you need two trig values: **sine** and **cosine.**

---

## Two Jobs, One Sling Leg

Every sling leg in a bridle is doing two things at the same time:

$$\text{Vertical component} = T \sin(\theta)$$

$$\text{Horizontal component} = T \cos(\theta)$$

Where:
- $T$ = total tension in the sling leg
- $\theta$ = sling angle measured from horizontal

The **vertical component** is the useful work — it supports the load. The **horizontal component** is spreading force — it pulls inward on pick points, side-loads hooks, and stresses the load structure itself.

For two legs to support a load $W$, their vertical components must sum to the full load weight:

$$2T\sin(\theta) = W$$

Solving for sling tension:

$$T = \frac{W}{2\sin(\theta)}$$

The sling has to work harder and harder as the angle drops — not because the load got heavier, but because a shrinking share of each leg's tension is actually lifting anything.

---

## Why Sine Is the Key Number

The sine value tells you exactly how efficient each sling leg is as a lifting force.

| Sling Angle (from horizontal) | Sine Value | Lifting Efficiency |
|---|---|---|
| 90° | 1.000 | 100% — all tension lifts |
| 60° | 0.866 | 86.6% lifts |
| 45° | 0.707 | 70.7% lifts |
| 30° | 0.500 | 50% lifts |
| 20° | 0.342 | 34.2% lifts |
| 10° | 0.174 | 17.4% lifts |

At 10°, only **17 cents on the dollar** of sling tension is doing useful lifting work. The sling must pull nearly six times as hard to hold the same load.

---

## Why Cosine Makes It Worse

The horizontal force component — driven by cosine — grows as the sling flattens. That sideways force doesn't disappear. It goes directly into the structure:

$$H = T\cos(\theta)$$

| Sling Angle (from horizontal) | Cosine Value | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| 90° | 0.000 | No horizontal force |
| 60° | 0.500 | Moderate spreading force |
| 45° | 0.707 | Significant spreading force |
| 30° | 0.866 | High horizontal force |
| 20° | 0.940 | Very high horizontal force |
| 10° | 0.985 | Extreme — nearly all tension is sideways |

At shallow angles, the bridle stops behaving like a lifting system and starts behaving like a **sideways compression system.** Lift points get pulled inward, hooks get side-loaded, thin structures get bent, and loads can be crushed by the inward squeeze.

---

## The Full Force Picture: 1,000 lb Load

For a 1,000 lb load on a two-leg bridle — $T = 1000 \div (2\sin\theta)$:

| Sling Angle | Tension Per Leg | Vertical Component Per Leg | Horizontal Component Per Leg |
|---|---|---|---|
| 90° | 500 lb | 500 lb | 0 lb |
| 75° | 518 lb | 500 lb | 134 lb |
| 60° | 577 lb | 500 lb | 289 lb |
| 45° | 707 lb | 500 lb | 500 lb |
| 30° | 1,000 lb | 500 lb | 866 lb |
| 20° | 1,462 lb | 500 lb | 1,374 lb |
| 15° | 1,932 lb | 500 lb | 1,866 lb |
| 10° | 2,879 lb | 500 lb | 2,836 lb |

Notice that the **vertical component per leg never changes** — it stays at 500 lb because two legs always share a 1,000 lb load. What changes is the total sling tension required to *generate* that 500 lb of vertical support. Everything above 500 lb is the angle penalty — and it goes entirely into sideways force.

---

## Scaled Up: 5,000 lb Load

When the load weight scales up, the consequences of shallow angles scale with it:

| Sling Angle | Tension Per Leg | Horizontal Force Per Leg |
|---|---|---|
| 90° | 2,500 lb | 0 lb |
| 60° | 2,887 lb | 1,443 lb |
| 45° | 3,536 lb | 2,500 lb |
| 30° | 5,000 lb | 4,330 lb |
| 20° | 7,310 lb | 6,869 lb |
| 10° | 14,397 lb | 14,179 lb |

At 10°, each sling leg is seeing nearly **three times the full load's half-share.** That's the difference between a sling safely within its Working Load Limit and one that's catastrophically overloaded — on the same lift, with the same gear, just a different angle.

---

## The Load Angle Factor Table

A quick-reference method that packages the same math:

$$T = \left(\frac{W}{2}\right) \times \text{LAF}$$

$$\text{LAF} = \frac{1}{\sin(\theta)}$$

| Angle from Horizontal | Load Angle Factor | Practical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 90° | 1.000 | No penalty |
| 75° | 1.035 | Negligible increase |
| 60° | 1.155 | +15.5% per leg |
| 45° | 1.414 | +41.4% per leg |
| 30° | 2.000 | Double — each leg = full load |
| 20° | 2.924 | Nearly triple |
| 15° | 3.864 | Nearly quadruple |
| 10° | 5.759 | Nearly six times |

Use this table in the field when you need a fast multiplier. It doesn't replace calculating — it confirms your calculation and flags when something needs a second look.

---

## The Danger Zone

A practical field framework used across rigging programs:

| Angle Range | Status |
|---|---|
| 60° and above | Preferred operating range |
| 45°–59° | Acceptable — verify all ratings |
| 30°–44° | Serious caution — recalculate, recheck every component |
| Below 30° | Danger zone — requires qualified rigger review or engineered lift plan |

**Why 30° is the hard marker:**

$$\sin(30°) = 0.5$$

$$T = \frac{W}{2(0.5)} = W$$

At 30°, each sling leg carries the full load weight. Below 30°, each leg carries *more* than the total load weight. This is not a gray area — it's a mathematical threshold that demands a higher level of review.

---

## Worked Problem: 3,000 lb Load at 20°

A 3,000 lb load. Two-leg bridle. Sling angle: 20° from horizontal.

**Sling tension per leg:**

$$T = \frac{3000}{2\sin(20°)} = \frac{3000}{2(0.342)} = \frac{3000}{0.684} \approx 4{,}386 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Horizontal force per leg:**

$$H = T\cos(20°) = 4386 \times 0.940 \approx 4{,}123 \text{ lb per leg}$$

A 3,000 lb load is creating **4,386 lb of sling tension** and **4,123 lb of horizontal spreading force** on each leg. Every component in the system — sling, shackle, hook, lug, anchor — must handle that full tension. The 3,000 lb load weight is almost irrelevant to the hardware rating question.

---

## The Plain-Language Version

> Think of each sling leg as doing two jobs. One part of the force lifts upward. One part pulls sideways. The vertical part is what supports the load. At a steep angle, most of the sling tension is useful lift. At a shallow angle, only a small fraction of the sling tension lifts the load — so the sling must pull much harder to create the same upward support. That extra force goes into the sling, shackles, hooks, lift points, and the load itself.

That's the whole lesson. The math just tells you exactly *how much* harder.

---

## Lesson Summary

| Concept | Key Point |
|---|---|
| Vertical component | $T\sin\theta$ — the share of sling tension actually lifting the load |
| Horizontal component | $T\cos\theta$ — spreading force that stresses structure, hardware, and pick points |
| Core formula | $T = W \div (2\sin\theta)$ — tension per leg in a balanced two-leg bridle |
| Sine and efficiency | Lower angle = lower sine = less lifting efficiency = higher required tension |
| 30° threshold | Each sling leg carries the full load weight — mandatory review below this point |
| Load angle factor | Quick multiplier: $\text{LAF} = 1 \div \sin\theta$ |

# Lesson 4: The 120-Degree Rule and Rigging Comparisons

---

## Lesson Overview

**Course:** Bridle Math & Angle Tension
**Lesson:** 4 of 9
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Audience:** Stagehands and riggers — festival and event production environments

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Define the included angle and explain how it relates to sling angle from horizontal and vertical
- State the 120-degree rule and explain why that threshold matters
- Derive why each sling leg equals the full load weight at a 120° included angle
- Compare tension across included angle configurations for a given load
- Identify common misunderstandings about sling ratings and load sharing
- Apply the 120-degree rule as a practical field warning in production rigging

---

## What Is the 120-Degree Rule?

The 120-degree rule is the most widely cited angle warning in rigging. It states:

> **When the included angle between two sling legs reaches 120°, each sling leg carries tension equal to the full load weight.**

Beyond 120°, each leg carries *more* than the total load weight — a condition that can overload slings, shackles, hooks, master links, and structural attachment points while the load itself appears stable and nothing looks obviously wrong from the ground.

It's a trig fact translated into a field-usable warning.

---

## The Geometry: Three Angles, One Shape

A two-leg bridle creates an inverted V. That shape can be described three ways, and all three are the same geometry:

| Measurement | Symbol | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Included angle between legs | $\beta$ | The angle *between* the two sling legs |
| Sling angle from vertical | $\alpha$ | Each leg's angle measured from straight down |
| Sling angle from horizontal | $\theta$ | Each leg's angle measured from flat |

They are related by:

$$\beta = 2\alpha \qquad \theta = 90° - \alpha \qquad \theta = 90° - \frac{\beta}{2}$$

So when the included angle is 120°:

$$\alpha = 60° \qquad \theta = 30°$$

**A 120° included angle is the same geometry as a 30° sling angle from horizontal.** Different numbers, same lift. This is why Lessons 3 and 4 connect directly — the 30° danger threshold and the 120-degree rule are describing the same critical point from different reference frames.

---

## Why 120° Is the Breaking Point

Start with the balanced two-leg bridle formula. Using included angle $\beta$:

$$T = \frac{W}{2\cos\left(\frac{\beta}{2}\right)}$$

Plug in 120°:

$$T = \frac{W}{2\cos(60°)} = \frac{W}{2(0.5)} = \frac{W}{1} = W$$

Each sling leg tension equals the full load weight — derived directly from the math, no estimation involved.

For a **5,000 lb load** at a 120° included angle:

$$T = 5{,}000 \text{ lb per leg}$$

$$\text{Combined sling-leg tension} = 10{,}000 \text{ lb}$$

The system contains **twice the load weight** in total sling tension, distributed equally across two legs — each of which must be rated accordingly, along with every shackle, hook, master link, and attachment point in the system.

---

## Tension Across the Full Angle Range

For a **1,000 lb load**, two-leg balanced bridle:

| Included Angle | Sling Angle (from horiz.) | Tension Per Leg | Tension as % of Load |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0° | 90° | 500 lb | 50% |
| 30° | 75° | 518 lb | 52% |
| 60° | 60° | 577 lb | 58% |
| 90° | 45° | 707 lb | 71% |
| **120°** | **30°** | **1,000 lb** | **100%** |
| 140° | 20° | 1,462 lb | 146% |
| 150° | 15° | 1,932 lb | 193% |
| 160° | 10° | 2,879 lb | 288% |

Notice the curve: tension climbs gradually from 0° to 90°, then accelerates sharply. By 150°, each leg carries nearly **twice the load.** By 160°, nearly **three times.**

For a **5,000 lb load**:

| Included Angle | Tension Per Leg | Combined System Tension |
|---|---|---|
| 60° | 2,887 lb | 5,774 lb |
| 90° | 3,536 lb | 7,071 lb |
| **120°** | **5,000 lb** | **10,000 lb** |
| 140° | 7,310 lb | 14,619 lb |
| 150° | 9,659 lb | 19,319 lb |
| 160° | 14,397 lb | 28,794 lb |

At 160°, a 5,000 lb load is producing over **28,000 lb** of combined sling-leg tension in the system.

---

## Four Geometry Benchmarks

Use these as practical reference points when evaluating any bridle setup:

---

### ✅ Preferred — 60° Included Angle

$$\beta = 60° \quad \theta = 60° \quad T \approx 0.577W$$

Each leg carries about **58% of the load.** Steep slings, high vertical efficiency, low horizontal force. This is the comfortable operating range.

---

### ⚠️ Acceptable with Checks — 90° Included Angle

$$\beta = 90° \quad \theta = 45° \quad T \approx 0.707W$$

Each leg carries about **71% of the load.** Common in production rigging. Requires confirmation that all components are rated for actual leg tension — not just load weight.

---

### 🔴 Critical Threshold — 120° Included Angle

$$\beta = 120° \quad \theta = 30° \quad T = W$$

Each leg carries **100% of the load.** This is the 120-degree rule limit. Any setup at or approaching this geometry requires a full rating check of every component, and serious consideration of whether the geometry can be improved.

---

### 🚫 Danger Zone — Beyond 120°

$$\beta > 120° \quad \theta < 30° \quad T > W$$

Each leg carries **more than the full load weight.** Without a qualified rigger review and engineered lift plan, this geometry should not proceed.

---

## Visual Way to Think About It

Picture the inverted V of a two-leg bridle:

- **Narrow V** = steep slings, small included angle, most tension works vertically = efficient
- **Wide V** = shallow slings, large included angle, most tension becomes horizontal force = dangerous

When the V opens to 120°, the legs are spread so flat that each one has only 50% vertical efficiency. Both legs together are just barely supporting the load — and each individual leg must equal the full load weight to do it.

When the V opens past 120°, the geometry is working *against* the lift. The slings are pulling more sideways than upward.

---

## Four Common Misunderstandings

**"Two slings split the load in half."**
Only true when both slings are perfectly vertical. At 120°, each leg carries the full load. At 150°, nearly double.

---

**"The included angle is the same as the sling angle."**
No. The included angle is measured *between* the two legs. The sling angle is measured from horizontal or vertical on a *single* leg. Confusing these produces incorrect calculations.

---

**"If the sling is rated for the load weight, the lift is safe."**
The sling must be rated for the **calculated leg tension** under the actual hitch type and angle — not the load weight on the weight ticket. At 120°, those two numbers are the same. At 150°, the leg tension is nearly double the load weight.

---

**"The crane capacity is the only rating that matters."**
The crane may be well within its capacity while a single shackle, lifting lug, or sling leg is overloaded. The weakest rated component controls the lift — regardless of what the crane chart says.

---

## Classroom Discussion Prompt

> *"A load weighs 1,000 lb. The rigging crew uses two sling legs at a 150° included angle. Each leg is rated for 1,500 lb. Is the lift safe?"*

Calculate first:

$$T = \frac{1000}{2\cos(75°)} = \frac{1000}{2(0.259)} = \frac{1000}{0.518} \approx 1{,}932 \text{ lb per leg}$$

Each leg is rated for 1,500 lb. The calculated tension is 1,932 lb. **The slings are overloaded by nearly 29%** — and the load still weighs exactly 1,000 lb.

The load didn't change. The angle made it unsafe.

|---|---|
| Included angle | The angle between two sling legs — not the same as sling angle from horizontal |
| Angle relationships | $\beta = 2\alpha$, $\theta = 90° - \beta/2$ |
| 120-degree rule | At 120° included angle, each leg tension = full load weight |
| Formula | $T = W \div (2\cos(\beta/2))$ |
| Beyond 120° | Each leg carries more than the total load — requires engineered review |

# Lesson 5: Equipment and Hardware Inspection

---

## Lesson Overview

**Course:** Bridle Math & Angle Tension
**Lesson:** 5 of 9
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Audience:** Stagehands and riggers — festival and event production environments

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, learners will be able to:

- Identify required markings on shackles, synthetic roundslings, and master links
- Explain the difference between WLL, rated load, and calculated sling-leg tension
- Inspect shackles, spansets/synthetic roundslings, and master links against removal-from-service criteria
- Recognize conditions that require immediate removal from service
- Explain why component compatibility matters beyond individual ratings
- Connect inspection findings to the lift plan before the load leaves the ground

---

## The Central Principle

> **Inspection is part of the load calculation.**

The previous lessons showed how to calculate exactly what your slings and hardware are seeing. This lesson is about confirming that your hardware can actually handle it.

A mathematically correct lift plan means nothing if the shackle pin is bent, the sling tag is missing, or the master link is point-loaded on the hook tip. Inspection is not a bureaucratic step that happens before the real work. It *is* real work — and skipping it turns a well-planned lift into an uncontrolled one.

---

## Inspection Types: When and How Often

**Initial Inspection**
Before equipment is first placed into service. Verify the item matches its specification, has proper identification and markings, shows no shipping or storage damage, and has documentation where required.

**Pre-Use / Frequent Inspection**
Before each use. Usually visual and tactile. Check for obvious damage, deformation, missing tags, heat exposure, corrosion, cuts, and any condition that creates doubt.

> *Before the lift, inspect what will carry the force.*

**Periodic Inspection**
More formal. Performed by a designated or qualified person based on service severity, frequency of use, and environment. Documented according to OSHA requirements, ASME standards, employer policy, and manufacturer instructions. Minimum frequency follows the most restrictive applicable requirement.

---

## Shackles

Shackles are connection points throughout a bridle system — linking sling eyes to master links, connecting to lifting lugs, and transferring load between components. They are frequently subject to multi-directional forces and must be inspected accordingly.

### What to Check

**Markings**
The shackle must have legible manufacturer identification and WLL or rated load marking. If either is missing or unreadable, the shackle cannot be used — there is no way to verify its capacity.

**Pin Condition**
The pin must be the correct pin for that shackle body. Never substitute a bolt or unapproved replacement. Inspect for bent pins, worn or damaged threads, mushroomed heads, shoulder damage, corrosion, and incomplete engagement. A pin that does not seat fully can disengage under load.

**Body Deformation**
Spreading at the ears, bending, twisting, elongation, or any visible distortion indicates the shackle has been overloaded or side-loaded. Deformed shackles are removed from service.

**Wear**
Inspect the crown and pin contact surfaces. Wear reduces cross-sectional area, which directly reduces load capacity.

**Cracks, Nicks, and Gouges**
Cracks are an immediate removal condition. Deep gouges and sharp notches become stress risers — points where metal is more likely to fail under load.

**Heat Damage**
Weld spatter, arc strikes, discoloration, or evidence of unauthorized heating compromise the metallurgy of the shackle, reducing its strength in ways that are not visible from the surface.

**Corrosion**
Surface rust may be acceptable depending on severity. Pitting, heavy corrosion, or visible metal loss requires removal.

**Side Loading**
Most shackles are rated for in-line loading only. Side loading can reduce capacity significantly. Only use a shackle for side-loaded configurations if the manufacturer explicitly permits it and the appropriate capacity reduction is applied.

### Shackle Removal-from-Service Criteria

| Condition | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Missing or illegible markings | Capacity cannot be verified |
| Bent, twisted, stretched, or distorted body | Evidence of overload or misuse |
| Bent, damaged, or incorrect pin | Pin may fail or disengage |
| Damaged threads | Pin may not seat properly |
| Excessive wear | Reduces load-bearing cross-section |
| Cracks | Immediate failure risk |
| Severe corrosion or pitting | Reduces metal section |
| Weld spatter or arc strike | Metallurgical damage |
| Unauthorized modification | Original rating no longer valid |
| Any condition creating doubt | Evaluate before use |

---

## Synthetic Roundslings and Spansets

**A note on terminology:** *SpanSet* is a manufacturer name that has become widely used in the field as a generic term for synthetic roundslings, similar to how "Sharpie" refers to any permanent marker. In this course, the correct term is **synthetic roundsling** — but you will hear "spanset" on nearly every production floor.

A synthetic roundsling consists of load-bearing core yarns enclosed inside a protective outer cover. The cover's job is to protect the core. Damage to the cover is always taken seriously because it may indicate core damage that is not visible from the outside.

### What to Check

**Sling Tag**
The tag is the sling's identity. It identifies rated capacities for each hitch type, material, manufacturer, length, and other critical information. A missing or unreadable tag is an immediate removal condition — without it, you cannot verify the sling's capacity for the actual configuration you're using.

**Cuts, Tears, Holes, and Punctures**
Any breach of the cover that reaches or exposes the core is a removal condition. Snags and tears may also indicate internal fiber damage that is not visible.

**Abrasion**
Heavy abrasion wears down the cover and may damage the load-bearing core beneath it. Pay attention to areas that contact sharp truss edges, concrete, or rough steel.

**Heat Damage**
Melting, charring, glazing, hardened fibers, or burn marks indicate that the sling has been exposed to heat that may have weakened its synthetic fibers. This is a removal condition.

**Chemical Damage**
Discoloration, brittleness, softening, stiffening, or visible fiber breakdown may indicate chemical exposure. Acids and caustic materials can degrade synthetic fibers rapidly. OSHA 29 CFR 1910.184 specifically lists acid and caustic burns as a removal condition.

**UV Damage**
Fading, surface powdering, brittleness, or loss of flexibility can indicate ultraviolet degradation. Slings stored outdoors or in sunny environments for extended periods should be inspected carefully.

**Knots**
Synthetic slings must never be knotted. Knots create severe stress concentration and dramatically reduce rated capacity.

**Exposed Core Yarn**
For roundslings, exposed core yarn is a removal condition. The load-bearing fibers are no longer protected.

**Embedded Particles**
Metal chips, grit, glass, concrete, or abrasive debris embedded in the cover can cut internal fibers during loading — damage that is completely invisible from the outside.

**Edge Protection**
Where slings contact sharp edges, corners, or rough surfaces, edge protection must be used. A sling routed across a sharp truss plate without protection may fail at a tension well below its rated capacity.

### Synthetic Roundsling Removal-from-Service Criteria

| Condition | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Missing or illegible tag | Rated capacity cannot be verified |
| Acid or caustic burns | Chemical weakening of fibers |
| Melting, charring, or weld spatter | Fiber strength loss |
| Holes, tears, cuts, punctures, or snags | Possible core damage |
| Exposed load-bearing yarns | Sling integrity compromised |
| Excessive abrasive wear | Strength reduction |
| Knots | Severe strength reduction |
| Chemical damage | Fiber degradation |
| UV degradation | Reduced fiber integrity |
| Distorted or damaged fittings | Load path compromised |
| Any condition causing doubt | Remove until evaluated |

## Master Links and Oblong Links

Master links collect multiple sling legs into a single point for connection to the crane hook or hoist. In multi-leg bridles, the master link is one of the most heavily loaded components in the system — it must be properly sized, correctly rated, and properly seated in the hook.

### What to Check

**Identification and Rated Load**
The link must have legible manufacturer markings, size, grade, and rated load. Without these, the link cannot be confidently rated for the actual forces it will experience.

**Wear**
Inspect the bearing surfaces where hooks, shackles, or sling eyes contact the link. Wear reduces the link's cross-sectional area and load capacity. In high-use production environments, master links can wear faster than visual inspection suggests.

**Elongation and Stretch**
A master link that has stretched indicates it has been overloaded. Compare dimensions to manufacturer specifications where possible.

**Deformation**
Twisting, bending, flattening, spreading, or any visible distortion of the link shape indicates the load path has been compromised.

**Cracks and Gouges**
Cracks are an immediate removal condition. Deep gouges and sharp notches create stress concentration points.

**Corrosion**
Pitting and heavy corrosion reduce the link's effective cross-section. Surface rust should be evaluated for severity.

**Heat Damage**
Discoloration, weld spatter, arc strikes, or unauthorized welding weaken the link's metallurgy.

**Hook Fit and Seating**
The master link must seat properly in the bowl of the crane hook — not on the tip, not jammed into the throat. A master link balanced on a hook tip is point-loaded and unstable. The hook size must be compatible with the link dimensions.

### Master Link Removal-from-Service Criteria

| Condition | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Missing or illegible identification | Cannot verify rating |
| Stretch or elongation | Evidence of overload |
| Bending, twisting, or distortion | Load path no longer valid |
| Excessive wear | Reduced cross-section |
| Cracks | Immediate failure risk |
| Severe gouges or notches | Stress concentration |
| Heat damage or weld spatter | Metallurgical weakening |
| Corrosion or pitting | Strength reduction |
| Poor hook fit or point loading | Unstable connection |

---

## WLL vs. Calculated Leg Tension

A critical and frequently misunderstood distinction:

> **Component ratings must be checked against calculated sling-leg tension — not total load weight.**

**Example:** A 2,000 lb truss lifted on a two-leg bridle at 30° from horizontal.

$$T = \frac{2000}{2\sin(30°)} = \frac{2000}{1.0} = 2{,}000 \text{ lb per leg}$$

Every sling, shackle, and master link in this system must be rated for at least **2,000 lb** in the actual configuration. A shackle rated 1,500 lb fails this check — even though "half" of a 2,000 lb load is only 1,000 lb. The angle eliminated that arithmetic entirely.

This is where Lessons 2, 3, and 4 directly connect to Lesson 5. You calculate the leg tension first. Then you inspect and verify that every component can handle it.

---

## Compatibility Between Components

A component can be fully rated and still be unsafe if it is connected incorrectly or paired with incompatible hardware. Before finalizing any rigging system, ask:

- Does the sling eye fit the shackle correctly — no bunching, pinching, or cutting contact?
- Is the shackle loaded in-line, or is it being side-loaded without an approved capacity reduction?
- Does the master link seat fully in the hook bowl, not on the tip or in the throat?
- Are synthetic slings protected from sharp edges at every contact point?
- Are multiple sling eyes crowded into one shackle in a way that distorts the load path?
- Are hardware sizes matched to avoid bending or prying forces?
- Does the WLL apply to the actual loading direction being used?

Compatibility failures don't always look wrong. That's what makes them dangerous.

---

## Teaching Scenario

A crew is preparing to lift a 1,000 lb truss using two synthetic roundslings, two shackles, and one master link. Sling angle: 30° from horizontal.

**Calculated leg tension:**

$$T = \frac{1000}{2\sin(30°)} = 1{,}000 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Inspection findings:**
- One sling has no readable tag
- One shackle has a bent pin
- The master link is resting on the hook tip, not seated in the bowl
- One sling crosses a sharp truss plate without edge protection

**Can the lift proceed?**

No. Even with correct math, this system fails inspection on four counts:

| Finding | Standard Applied | Required Action |
|---|---|---|
| Missing sling tag | ASME B30.9 / OSHA 1910.184 | Remove sling from service |
| Bent shackle pin | ASME B30.26 | Remove shackle from service |
| Master link on hook tip | ASME B30.10 | Reseat or replace hook/link |
| No edge protection | ASME B30.9 | Install edge protection before use |

Replace the affected components, verify all ratings against calculated leg tension, install edge protection, and recheck the full rigging geometry before the load moves.

## The Central Question

Previous lessons gave you the tools:

- **Lesson 3:** Understand what shallow angles actually do

- **Lesson 5:** Inspect before you lift

> **Given the tension, equipment, inspection condition, geometry, and worksite setup — should this lift happen?**

The answer requires math *and* judgment. Neither one alone is enough.

> **Unknown is not safe.**

## The Scenario Critique Framework

Use this seven-step sequence to evaluate any lift before the load leaves the ground.

---

**Step 1 — Identify the Load**
- What is the load weight? Is it confirmed or estimated?
- Does the weight include rigging hardware?
- Is the center of gravity known?
- Could the load shift during the lift?

**Step 2 — Identify the Geometry**
- How many sling legs are used?
- What is the sling angle from horizontal?
- What is the included angle between legs?
- Are all legs the same length and actually sharing the load?

**Step 3 — Calculate Sling-Leg Tension**

Two-leg bridle (angle from horizontal):
$$T = \frac{W}{2\sin(\theta)}$$

Two-leg bridle (included angle):
$$T = \frac{W}{2\cos(\beta/2)}$$

**Step 4 — Compare Against WLL**
- Is each sling rated for the calculated tension at the actual hitch type and angle?
- Are shackles rated for both the force and the loading direction?
- Is the master link rated for the combined load?
- Is every hook seated in the bowl — not on the tip?

**Step 5 — Inspect the Equipment**
- Are sling tags readable and capacities confirmed?
- Are shackles marked, undamaged, and correctly oriented?
- Are master links within serviceable condition?
- Is edge protection in place where slings contact sharp surfaces?

**Step 6 — Evaluate Worksite Controls**
- Is there a designated lift director?
- Is there a signalperson if required?
- Are workers clear of the suspended load?
- Is there an exclusion zone?
- Does anyone have stop-work authority?

**Step 7 — Recommend Safer Alternatives**

Options include: longer slings to increase angle, spreader beam to reduce horizontal force, higher-rated hardware, different pick points, edge protection, written lift plan, or qualified person review.

## Scenario 1: The 500 lb Hang

**Setup:** 500 lb load. Two-leg bridle. Sling angle: 30° from horizontal. Each sling WLL: 750 lb. Each shackle WLL: 1,000 lb. Both slings tagged and in good condition.

**Calculate:**

$$T = \frac{500}{2\sin(30°)} = \frac{500}{2(0.5)} = \frac{500}{1.0} = 500 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Evaluation:** Slings rated 750 lb, seeing 500 lb. Shackles rated 1,000 lb. Mathematically acceptable — but 30° is the caution threshold. Any reduction in angle immediately increases tension. This setup leaves very little margin.

**Safer Alternative:** Increase sling angle to 60° if practical.

$$T = \frac{500}{2\sin(60°)} = \frac{500}{1.732} \approx 289 \text{ lb per leg}$$

Same load, same hardware — but tension drops by nearly 42% just by improving the geometry.

**Verdict:** Lift *can* proceed, but geometry improvement is strongly recommended. Verify shackle orientation, master link fit, and load control.

---

## Scenario 2: The Hidden Overload

**Setup:** 2,000 lb scenic element. Two-leg bridle. Sling angle: 30° from horizontal. Each sling WLL: 1,500 lb. Shackles rated 2,000 lb. Crew says: *"Two slings — each one only carries 1,000 lb."*

**Calculate:**

$$T = \frac{2000}{2\sin(30°)} = \frac{2000}{2(0.5)} = \frac{2000}{1.0} = 2{,}000 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Error:** The crew divided the load by two without applying the angle factor. At 30°, each leg carries the **full load weight** — not half.

**Capacity Check:**

| Component | Calculated Tension | WLL | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Each sling | 2,000 lb | 1,500 lb | ❌ Overloaded |
| Each shackle | 2,000 lb | 2,000 lb | ⚠️ At limit |

**Verdict:** **Lift must not proceed.** Slings are overloaded by 33%.

**Safer Alternatives:** Increase sling angle, use higher-rated slings, use a spreader beam, or have a qualified rigger redesign the lift.

---

## Scenario 3: The 120-Degree Included Angle

**Setup:** 1,000 lb lighting truss. Two-leg bridle. Included angle: 120°. Each sling WLL: 1,000 lb. Each shackle WLL: 1,000 lb.

**Calculate:**

$$T = \frac{1000}{2\cos(60°)} = \frac{1000}{2(0.5)} = \frac{1000}{1.0} = 1{,}000 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Evaluation:** Every component is operating at its rated load — zero margin remaining. Any angle increase, shock load, measurement error, or unequal leg loading pushes the system into overload.

**Compare Alternatives:**

| Included Angle | Tension Per Leg | Margin vs. 1,000 lb WLL |
|---|---|---|
| 120° | 1,000 lb | 0% |
| 90° | 707 lb | 29% margin |
| 60° | 577 lb | 42% margin |

**Verdict:** **Lift should not proceed as configured.** Geometry must be improved before this can be considered a safe setup.

---

## Scenario 4: The Inspection Failure

**Setup:** 750 lb load. Two-leg bridle. Sling angle: 45° from horizontal. Each sling stated WLL: 1,000 lb. One sling tag is unreadable. One shackle has visible corrosion and an illegible WLL marking.

**Calculate:**

$$T = \frac{750}{2\sin(45°)} = \frac{750}{1.414} \approx 530 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**The Math Looks Fine.** 530 lb is below the stated 1,000 lb WLL — on paper.

**The Inspection Does Not.**

| Finding | Standard | Decision |
|---|---|---|
| Unreadable sling tag | ASME B30.9 / OSHA 1910.184 | Remove from service — capacity unverifiable |
| Illegible shackle marking | ASME B30.26 | Remove from service — capacity unverifiable |
| Visible corrosion | ASME B30.26 | Evaluate severity — may require removal |

**Verdict:** **Lift must not proceed with these components.** Correct math against an unverified rating is not a safe lift. Replace the affected components, confirm all markings and ratings, and recalculate.

---

## Scenario 5: The Sharp Edge Problem

**Setup:** 1,200 lb steel frame. Two synthetic roundslings at 60° from horizontal. Each sling WLL: 1,500 lb. Shackles rated 2,000 lb. One sling passes directly over an unprotected steel edge.

**Calculate:**

$$T = \frac{1200}{2\sin(60°)} = \frac{1200}{1.732} \approx 693 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Capacity Check:** Slings and shackles appear adequate by rating.

**The Problem:** The synthetic roundsling is in contact with an unprotected sharp steel edge. Sling tension under load increases the cutting force at the contact point. A sling can fail at a fraction of its rated tension when a sharp edge concentrates the load.

**Verdict:** **Lift must not proceed without edge protection.** A mathematically safe system can fail mechanically through cut damage.

**Safer Alternatives:** Install proper edge protection at the contact point, change the pick points, or use a spreader beam to eliminate direct sling-to-edge contact.

---

## Scenario 6: Off-Center Load and Unknown Center of Gravity

**Setup:** 3,000 lb load. Two pick points. Center of gravity closer to the left pick point. Crew uses two equal-length slings and assumes equal load sharing. Sling angles approximately 45°.

**Equal-Load Assumption:**

$$T = \frac{3000}{2\sin(45°)} = \frac{3000}{1.414} \approx 2{,}121 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**The Problem:** This calculation assumes the load is balanced. It is not. The center of gravity is offset, which shifts more force into the left leg. The actual left-leg tension is higher than 2,121 lb — by an unknown amount.

**Verdict:** **The equal-load calculation is not valid for this setup.** Actual leg tension cannot be confirmed without knowing the load distribution.

**Safer Alternatives:** Establish or estimate the center of gravity, adjust sling lengths to compensate, use load cells, use a spreader or lifting beam designed for off-center loads, or obtain a qualified engineer review.

## From Calculations to Complete Plans

The previous six lessons built the technical foundation: calculating sling-leg tension, understanding shallow angles, applying the 120-degree rule, inspecting hardware, and critiquing real scenarios.

This lesson asks the next question:

> **How do those pieces become a defensible rigging plan?**

A rigging plan is not just a drawing with a load weight on it. It is a verified system of loads, hardware, people, procedures, and controls — and it must survive both the math check and the field check.

---

## The Standards Ecosystem

Four overlapping frameworks govern professional rigging practice. Understanding how they fit together is part of working at a professional level.

---

### ANSI/ESTA — Consensus Technical Standards

ANSI standards developed through ESTA's Technical Standards Program define accepted practice for entertainment technology equipment and systems. These are **consensus standards** — developed by industry professionals, adopted by ANSI, and widely recognized as the technical benchmark for entertainment rigging work.

**ANSI E1.6-1 — Entertainment Technology: Powered Hoist Systems**
Directly relevant to bridle systems that terminate at powered hoists, motors, trusses, or overhead grids. This standard addresses hoist system design, installation, operation, inspection, and maintenance in entertainment applications.

---

### ETCP — Professional Credentialing

ETCP stands for the **Entertainment Technician Certification Program.** ETCP certification is not a law or engineering standard — it is a professional credential that demonstrates rigging knowledge has been tested and verified against an industry benchmark.

For learners in this course:

> *ANSI/ESTA standards describe recognized technical practices. ETCP certification helps identify individuals who have demonstrated the relevant knowledge to apply them.*

ETCP-certified riggers are often designated as qualified persons on production floors where rigging decisions require a higher level of accountability.

---

### OSHA — Enforceable Law

OSHA sets legally enforceable workplace safety requirements. The regulations most relevant to production rigging include:

- **29 CFR 1910.184** — Slings: use, inspection, and removal from service
- **29 CFR 1926.251** — Rigging equipment for material handling
- **29 CFR 1926 Subpart CC** — Cranes and derricks: qualified riggers, signalpersons, suspended loads

OSHA is the floor. Everything else builds on top of it.

---

### ASME B30-Series — Industry Hardware Standards

ASME consensus standards define safe practice for specific hardware categories:

- **B30.9** — Slings
- **B30.10** — Hooks
- **B30.26** — Rigging hardware: shackles, links, rings, swivels

These remain critical reference points throughout any rigging plan.

---

### Manufacturer Instructions

Manufacturer documentation is mandatory — not optional. WLL ratings, loading restrictions, temperature limits, inspection criteria, side-loading reductions, and removal criteria may only appear in manufacturer documentation. A component used outside manufacturer guidance is used outside its rating, regardless of what the standard says.

---

## The Best-Practice Hierarchy

When building a rigging plan, every layer of this hierarchy applies:

| Layer | Authority |
|---|---|
| 1. Legal requirements | OSHA — enforceable |
| 2. Consensus standards | ANSI/ESTA, ASME B30-series |
| 3. Manufacturer instructions | Component-specific requirements |
| 4. Employer and site policies | Venue, production, and crew procedures |
| 5. Engineering review | Required for elevated risk or unusual configurations |
| 6. Qualified-person judgment | ETCP-certified rigger or designated responsible person |
| 7. Field verification | Confirmed before the load moves |

If a conflict exists between layers, apply the most protective requirement.

---

## What a Complete Rigging Plan Includes

A rigging plan that satisfies professional standards addresses every item in this table:

| Plan Element | What to Verify |
|---|---|
| Load description | Weight, dimensions, center of gravity, dynamic behavior |
| Load path | From load through sling, shackle, master link, hoist, and structure |
| Bridle geometry | Sling angles, included angles, pick-point spacing, trim height |
| Tension calculations | Sling-leg tension and horizontal force at each point |
| Hardware list | Slings, shackles, master links, hoists, hooks, beam clamps |
| WLL verification | Every component rated for actual force and loading direction |
| Structural capacity | Roof, beam, grid, or truss verified for point loads and direction |
| Inspection status | Tags, markings, wear, deformation, heat damage, corrosion |
| Compatibility | Correct fit, no side loading, no point loading, proper seating |
| Edge protection | Installed wherever slings contact sharp or abrasive surfaces |
| Personnel roles | Lift lead, rigger, signalperson, operator, spotters |
| Communication | Radio protocol, hand signals, command authority, stop signal |
| Exclusion zone | All personnel clear of suspended loads and fall zones |
| Emergency procedure | Lowering plan, stuck-load response, stop-work trigger |
| Documentation | Drawings, calculations, inspection records, revisions |

A plan that addresses load weight but not leg tension is incomplete. A plan that checks slings but not structure is incomplete. A plan with no named lift lead has no one accountable for stopping the work.

---

## Applying Bridle Math Inside the Plan

The plan must calculate actual forces — not assumed or estimated ones.

**Sling-leg tension (angle from horizontal):**
$$T = \frac{W}{2\sin(\theta)}$$

**Sling-leg tension (included angle):**
$$T = \frac{W}{2\cos(\beta/2)}$$

**Horizontal force per leg:**
$$H = T\cos(\theta)$$

Horizontal force matters because it can pull pick points inward, side-load shackles and lifting lugs, compress truss members, and overload beam clamps — even when the vertical load calculation is fully compliant.

---

## Angle Limits in the Plan

Best-practice angle guidance used across professional rigging programs:

| Included Angle | Sling Angle (horiz.) | Practical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 60° | 60° | Efficient — preferred when achievable |
| 90° | 45° | Common — requires careful capacity check |
| **120°** | **30°** | **Warning threshold — each leg = full load weight** |
| Above 120° | Below 30° | High-risk — redesign or engineered review required |

> **If the included angle is increasing, sling-leg tension is increasing.** A rigging plan that shows the angle but doesn't calculate the resulting tension has skipped the most important step.

---

## When Qualified-Person Review Is Required

Not every lift can be safely approved by applying a formula. A qualified person, ETCP-certified rigger, or structural engineer should review the plan when:

- Load weight is uncertain or not formally documented
- Center of gravity is unknown or offset
- The load involves people, performers, or audience proximity
- The bridle angle approaches or exceeds the 120° warning threshold
- Multiple hoists must travel together in a synchronized lift
- Load sharing between points cannot be confirmed
- Structural capacity is undocumented or marginal
- Hardware is used in a nonstandard loading direction
- Dynamic loading, motion, wind, or impact is possible
- The lift plan changes after initial approval

When in doubt about whether review is required, the answer is: **get the review.**

## Full-System Example

**Scenario:** A 1,200 lb lighting truss will be hung from two motor points using a two-leg bridle. Included angle: 90°. Each sling WLL: 2,000 lb. Each shackle WLL: 1,500 lb. Master link WLL: 3,000 lb. Hoists rated: 1 ton each. Structure point capacity: 1,000 lb each.

**Step 1 — Calculate sling-leg tension:**

$$T = \frac{1200}{2\cos(45°)} = \frac{1200}{1.414} \approx 849 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Step 2 — Calculate horizontal force:**

$$H = 849 \times \cos(45°) = 849 \times 0.707 \approx 600 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Step 3 — Check components:**

| Component | WLL | Calculated Force | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Each sling | 2,000 lb | 849 lb | ✅ Pass |
| Each shackle | 1,500 lb | 849 lb | ✅ Pass (verify orientation) |
| Master link | 3,000 lb | 1,200 lb combined | ✅ Pass (verify hook seating) |
| Each hoist | ~2,000 lb | ~849 lb applied | ✅ Pass (verify actual distribution) |
| Structure per point | 1,000 lb | 849 lb vertical + 600 lb horizontal | ⚠️ Requires verification |

**Step 4 — Flag the structure:**
The structural rating of 1,000 lb per point must be confirmed as valid for the **actual loading direction** — including horizontal force. A point rated for 1,000 lb of vertical load may not be rated for the combined vector of 849 lb vertical and 600 lb horizontal force pulling at an angle.

**Step 5 — Plan controls:**
Inspection required on all components. Exclusion zone required. Lift lead designated. Communication protocol confirmed. Emergency lowering procedure defined.

**Decision:** The component math is acceptable as a starting point, but this lift cannot be approved without structural verification for the actual load direction. Field inspection and a named lift lead are also required before the load moves.

## Six Common Rigging Plan Failures

**1. Plan shows load weight but not leg tension**
A drawing that says "1,000 lb load" is incomplete without the calculated bridle-leg forces at actual angles.

**2. Plan checks slings but ignores hardware**
The sling capacity may be sufficient while a shackle, master link, or attachment point is overloaded.

**3. Plan ignores horizontal force**
The vertical calculation passes while horizontal force overloads a truss chord, beam clamp, or lifting lug.

**4. Plan doesn't verify structure**
Rigging hardware within capacity means nothing if the structural attachment point is not verified.

**5. Plan doesn't match the field**
If actual angles, hardware, or pick points differ from the plan, the lift must be reassessed — not assumed acceptable.

**6. No person has authority**
A plan is incomplete if no one is clearly responsible for approving, modifying, or stopping the lift.

## ETCP-Aligned Professional Habits

ETCP practice emphasizes professional behavior alongside technical knowledge. These habits apply on every production floor, regardless of certification status:

**Know the limits.** Know the limits of the equipment, the structure, the plan, and your own authority. Operating near or at those limits requires more scrutiny — not less.

**Verify before loading.** Never assume weights, ratings, angles, or inspection status. Confirm them.

**Communicate clearly.** One person in charge of the lift. Clear commands. An agreed stop signal that everyone knows and everyone respects.

**Protect the public and crew.** No one should be under a suspended load unless a specific engineered and procedural control explicitly permits it.

**Stop when conditions change.** If the plan changes, the calculation may change. If the calculation changes, the approval may no longer be valid.

**Document important decisions.** A professional rigging culture leaves a trail: drawings, calculations, inspection records, approvals, and revisions.

## Why This Lesson Exists

The previous seven lessons covered the math, the physics, the standards, the hardware, and the planning process. This lesson covers the part that determines whether any of that actually gets used on the floor: **people talking to each other.**

Rigging failures rarely trace back to a single error. They usually involve a combination — a shallow angle, a rushed schedule, an untagged sling, unclear authority, and an assumption that someone else already checked it.

Group discussion trains you to catch those combinations before the lift. A professional rigging culture normalizes asking:

- *"Who confirmed the load weight?"*
- *"What angle are we actually using?"*
- *"Is this shackle rated for this loading direction?"*
- *"What changed from the plan?"*
- *"Does anyone have a concern before we move?"*

Speaking up is not a disruption. It is part of the job.

> **Rigging is a team activity. The safest solution usually comes from shared review, clear communication, and the courage to stop when something doesn't make sense.**

---

## The Core Question for This Lesson

Use this question to frame every scenario:

> **What information would we need before saying yes?**

This shifts the team away from guessing toward professional decision-making. If the information isn't there, the answer isn't yes.

## Discussion Format

Apply this structure to every scenario in this lesson.

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1. Read the scenario | Identify the setup, load, equipment, and geometry |
| 2. Individual review | What is known? What is unknown? What calculation is needed? Would you approve the lift? |
| 3. Small-group critique | Compare answers and identify where reasoning differs |
| 4. Group decision | Choose one outcome: Approve / Approve after corrections / Do not approve — redesign required |
| 5. Safer alternative | Propose a revised setup that reduces risk |
| 6. Debrief | How did sharing change the decision? |

## Scenario 1: "It's Only 500 Pounds"

**Setup:** A 500 lb scenic element is rigged on two synthetic roundslings at 25° from horizontal. Each sling WLL: 1,000 lb. The crew says the load is light, so the shallow angle shouldn't matter.

**Calculate:**

$$T = \frac{500}{2\sin(25°)} = \frac{500}{2(0.423)} = \frac{500}{0.846} \approx 591 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Discussion Questions:**
- Is 591 lb acceptable against a 1,000 lb WLL?
- What happens to the tension if the angle measurement is off by 5° — dropping to 20°?
- Does "the load is light" justify a shallow angle?
- What safer alternative would you propose?

**The Teaching Point:** The slings may pass by WLL at this angle. But 25° leaves very little margin and reflects poor rigging geometry. At 20°, tension jumps to approximately 731 lb — a 24% increase from a 5° measurement error. The load weight was never the problem. The angle was.

**Safer Alternative:** Increase the sling angle, change pick points, shorten the horizontal spread, or use a spreader beam.

---

## Scenario 2: "The Math Passes, But the Tag Is Gone"

**Setup:** A 900 lb truss is lifted on two slings at 60° from horizontal. Calculated tension is within the expected rating. One sling tag is missing.

**Discussion Questions:**
- Can the sling be used if everyone is confident it's "probably" rated high enough?
- What is the difference between *known capacity* and *assumed capacity*?
- Who has the authority to reject the sling?
- How should the team respond when replacing the sling means a schedule delay?

**The Teaching Point:** This is where production pressure collides with inspection standards. The correct answer is to remove the untagged sling from service until capacity can be verified. Schedule pressure does not change the standard. A math calculation run against an assumed rating is not a verified safe lift.

**Community Lesson:** The person who calls this out should be supported, not pressured. That is what a functional safety culture looks like.

---

## Scenario 3: "The Included Angle Is Close Enough"

**Setup:** A rigging drawing shows a 90° included angle. On site, available pick points push the actual included angle to approximately 125°. Load weight: 1,500 lb.

**Calculate — original plan at 90°:**

$$T = \frac{1500}{2\cos(45°)} = \frac{1500}{1.414} \approx 1{,}061 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Calculate — field condition at 125°:**

$$T = \frac{1500}{2\cos(62.5°)} = \frac{1500}{2(0.462)} = \frac{1500}{0.924} \approx 1{,}623 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Difference:** The field change increases sling-leg tension by approximately **562 lb per leg** — a 53% increase over the planned geometry.

**Discussion Questions:**
- Does "close enough" apply to a 53% tension increase?
- Which components need to be rechecked?
- Who needs to approve a plan change of this magnitude?
- What is the safer option if the original points aren't available?

**The Teaching Point:** Plan changes in the field are not administrative updates. They are engineering changes. Any geometry change that increases tension must be recalculated and reapproved before the lift proceeds.

---

## Scenario 4: "The Shackle Fits, So It Must Be Fine"

**Setup:** A shackle connects a sling to a lifting lug. The shackle body fits through the lug, but the sling pulls the shackle at an angle — creating side loading. The shackle WLL exceeds the calculated load.

**Discussion Questions:**
- Does the WLL still apply when the load direction is off-axis?
- What manufacturer information is needed before using this shackle in this configuration?
- Could a different connection geometry solve the side-loading problem?
- Would a different hardware choice — different shackle orientation, a master link, or a swivel — be appropriate here?

**The Teaching Point:** WLL is not geometry-independent. Most shackles are rated for straight-line loading. Side loading may require a manufacturer-specified capacity reduction — or may not be permitted at all for a given product. A shackle that fits is not automatically a shackle that is rated for the actual force in the actual direction.

---

## Scenario 5: "The Load Is Balanced... Probably"

**Setup:** A 2,000 lb scenic wall has two pick points. The crew assumes a centered center of gravity and uses equal-length slings at 45° from horizontal. Unknown to the crew, the wall has hidden steel reinforcement on one side.

**Equal-load calculation:**

$$T = \frac{2000}{2\sin(45°)} = \frac{2000}{1.414} \approx 1{,}414 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Discussion Questions:**
- What assumption does this calculation require?
- If the center of gravity is offset, what happens to the tension in the heavier leg?
- What field signs might indicate an off-center load?
- When should this type of lift require a qualified person to review the plan?

**The Teaching Point:** The equal-load formula is only valid for a balanced, centered lift. A hidden center-of-gravity offset can put significantly more force into one leg — with no visible warning before the lift. When load distribution is uncertain, the calculation is also uncertain.

**Safer Alternatives:** Verify or measure the center of gravity, use load cells, adjust pick points, use a spreader beam designed for asymmetrical loading, or obtain a qualified person review.

---

## Scenario 6: "We Forgot Edge Protection"

**Setup:** A 1,200 lb steel-framed scenic piece is being lifted with synthetic roundslings at 60° from horizontal. All ratings pass. During final pre-lift review, a crew member notices one sling passes over an unprotected sharp steel corner.

**Discussion Questions:**
- Should the lift continue because the capacity math is correct?
- What can happen when the sling is tensioned against a sharp edge?
- Who should call stop-work in this situation?
- How should the crew respond to the person who caught the issue?

**The Teaching Point:** A sling can fail at a fraction of its rated tension if a sharp edge concentrates the cutting force. A mathematically acceptable tension does not protect against mechanical failure from edge damage. The lift stops. The edge gets protected. The person who spoke up gets thanked — because that is what professional crews do.

---

## Scenario 7: "Too Many Voices"

**Setup:** During a lift, three people are giving instructions to the hoist operator simultaneously. One says "up," another says "stop," a third is calling out an angle measurement. The load begins to drift.

**Discussion Questions:**
- What is the primary hazard in this situation?
- Who should be giving commands to the operator?
- What should the operator do when they receive conflicting commands?
- How should communication be established before the lift begins?

**The Teaching Point:** This is a command and control failure. A lift requires one person with clear authority, a single channel of communication to the operator, and a universally understood stop signal. When commands conflict, the safest default is to **stop.** Every time. The load can wait. The injury cannot be undone.

---

## Scenario 8: "The Point Was Unavailable"

**Setup:** A rigging plot specifies two overhead points at a certain spacing. On site, one point is blocked by other equipment. The crew shifts to a farther point, increasing the included angle from 80° to 115°. Load weight: 2,500 lb.

**Calculate — original plan at 80°:**

$$T = \frac{2500}{2\cos(40°)} = \frac{2500}{1.532} \approx 1{,}632 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Calculate — field condition at 115°:**

$$T = \frac{2500}{2\cos(57.5°)} = \frac{2500}{1.074} \approx 2{,}328 \text{ lb per leg}$$

**Difference:** Approximately **696 lb more per leg** — a 43% increase from the planned configuration.

**Discussion Questions:**
- What must be recalculated as a result of this field change?
- Does the new point have a verified structural rating?
- Who has the authority to approve this geometry change?
- What safer alternatives exist if the original point is truly unavailable?

**The Teaching Point:** "The point was unavailable" is not an approval. It is the beginning of a new review process. Geometry changes require new calculations, new hardware checks, new structural verification, and a named person who takes responsibility for the revised plan.

## The Capstone Principle

Eight lessons ago, this course started with a single idea: a bridle lift is safe only when the load weight, sling angle, component ratings, inspection status, and crew communication are all controlled.

This lesson is where you prove you can apply that principle end-to-end.

> **A safe rigging plan is a complete argument: it shows what is being lifted, where every force goes, why each component is acceptable, and who is responsible for the lift.**

Not just math. Not just inspection. Not just communication. All of it — together, on paper, verified before the load moves.

---

## The Capstone Scenario

A production team is preparing a small indoor concert in a multipurpose venue. The design calls for one flown lighting truss above the stage, supported at two overhead rigging points. Each point uses a two-leg bridle.

**Given Parameters:**

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Lighting truss self-weight | 300 lb |
| Lighting fixtures | 450 lb |
| Cable and accessories | 100 lb |
| Rigging hardware estimate | 50 lb |
| **Total suspended load** | **900 lb** |
| Primary suspension locations | 2 |
| Available overhead point capacity | 1,000 lb each — vertical only unless verified |
| Hoists | ½-ton motors — 1,000 lb WLL each |
| Synthetic roundslings | 1,500 lb WLL each |
| Shackles | 2,000 lb WLL each |
| Master links | 2,000 lb WLL each |
| Maximum allowed included angle | 120° |
| Edge protection | Required at all sling-to-sharp-surface contacts |

---

## Step 1: Establish the Load

**Total suspended load:**

$$W_{total} = 300 + 450 + 100 + 50 = 900 \text{ lb}$$

**Load per suspension location:**

$$W_{point} = \frac{900}{2} = 450 \text{ lb per point}$$

This assumes the truss is balanced and the center of gravity is centered between the two suspension points. If the center of gravity is offset, load distribution must be recalculated. Equal distribution is a design assumption — not a guaranteed field condition.

---

## Step 2: Evaluate Three Bridle Geometries

### Option A — 90° Included Angle ✅ Recommended

$$T = \frac{450}{2\cos(45°)} = \frac{450}{1.414} \approx 318 \text{ lb per bridle leg}$$

**Horizontal force per leg:**

$$H = 318 \times \cos(45°) \approx 225 \text{ lb}$$

**Hardware check:**

| Component | WLL | Required Force | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Synthetic roundsling | 1,500 lb | 318 lb | ✅ Pass |
| Shackle | 2,000 lb | 318 lb | ✅ Pass |
| Master link | 2,000 lb | ~450 lb combined | ✅ Pass — verify fit and seating |
| Hoist | 1,000 lb | ~450 lb | ✅ Pass |
| Overhead point | 1,000 lb vertical | 318 lb vertical + 225 lb horizontal | ⚠️ Conditional — directional loading must be verified |

**Assessment:** This is the preferred design. Good margin, reasonable horizontal force, well within all component ratings.

---

### Option B — 120° Included Angle ⚠️ Marginal

$$T = \frac{450}{2\cos(60°)} = \frac{450}{2(0.5)} = \frac{450}{1.0} = 450 \text{ lb per bridle leg}$$

**Horizontal force per leg:**

$$H = 450 \times \cos(30°) \approx 390 \text{ lb}$$

**Hardware check:**

| Component | WLL | Required Force | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Synthetic roundsling | 1,500 lb | 450 lb | ✅ Pass — but at warning threshold |
| Shackle | 2,000 lb | 450 lb | ✅ Pass |
| Overhead point | 1,000 lb vertical | 450 lb vertical + 390 lb horizontal | ⚠️ Requires verified directional capacity |

**Assessment:** Components pass by WLL, but every leg carries the full point load — zero margin before dynamic effects, measurement error, or unequal distribution push the system past rated capacity. This geometry passes the exercise rules but is not recommended when a better option is available.

---

### Option C — 140° Included Angle ❌ Rejected

$$T = \frac{450}{2\cos(70°)} = \frac{450}{2(0.342)} = \frac{450}{0.684} \approx 658 \text{ lb per bridle leg}$$

**Horizontal force per leg:**

$$H = 658 \times \cos(20°) \approx 618 \text{ lb}$$

**Assessment:** This geometry violates the 120-degree rule. Each leg exceeds the full point load. Horizontal force is nearly double that of Option A. The plan must be redesigned.

**Required redesign options:**
- Move overhead points closer together to reduce the included angle
- Use a spreader beam to eliminate the wide bridle spread
- Use a longer vertical drop to steepen the sling angles
- Select different pick points
- Obtain a qualified-person or engineering review

---

## Side-by-Side Geometry Comparison

| Included Angle | Sling Angle (horiz.) | Tension Per Leg | Horizontal Force Per Leg | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 90° | 45° | 318 lb | 225 lb | ✅ Preferred |
| 120° | 30° | 450 lb | 390 lb | ⚠️ Marginal |
| 140° | 20° | 658 lb | 618 lb | ❌ Rejected |

The wider the bridle, the more force every component in the system carries — and the more horizontal force is pushed into the overhead structure, pick points, and the truss itself.

---

## Step 3: Trace the Complete Load Path

Every link in this chain must be rated, inspected, compatible, correctly loaded, and documented:

**Lighting fixtures → truss → pick connection → sling attachment → shackle → bridle leg → master link → hoist hook → hoist body → overhead attachment → beam/grid/structure**

If any single link in this chain is not verified, the plan is incomplete.

---

## Step 4: Inspection Plan

Before this lift is approved, every component requires a documented inspection.

**Synthetic Roundslings**

| Check | Required Condition |
|---|---|
| Sling tag | Present and fully legible |
| WLL | Verified for actual hitch type and angle |
| Cover condition | No cuts, tears, holes, snags, or punctures |
| Core exposure | No exposed load-bearing yarn |
| Heat damage | No melting, charring, or glazing |
| Chemical/UV damage | No brittleness, discoloration, or fiber breakdown |
| Knots | None permitted |
| Edge protection | Installed at every sharp or abrasive contact point |

**Shackles**

| Check | Required Condition |
|---|---|
| Manufacturer marking | Legible |
| WLL marking | Legible |
| Pin | Correct pin, fully engaged, undamaged threads |
| Body | No bending, spreading, twisting, or elongation |
| Surface | No cracks, severe nicks, gouges, or heat damage |
| Corrosion | No pitting or significant section loss |
| Loading direction | In-line unless manufacturer permits and reduction is applied |

**Master Links**

| Check | Required Condition |
|---|---|
| Identification/WLL | Legible |
| Shape | No stretch, bending, twisting, or distortion |
| Surface | No cracks, deep gouges, or severe wear |
| Corrosion/heat | No pitting, weld spatter, or arc strikes |
| Hook fit | Seated in bowl — not on tip or throat |

**Hoists**

| Check | Required Condition |
|---|---|
| Rated capacity | Verified and documented |
| Inspection status | Current per employer program |
| Hook and latch | Serviceable |
| Chain condition | No damage, corrosion, or deformation |
| Controls | Tested before load |
| Orientation | Correct for intended use |

---

## Step 5: Structure Verification

The overhead points are rated **1,000 lb — vertical only — unless verified otherwise.**

A bridle introduces non-vertical forces into every attachment point. At a 90° included angle, each point sees approximately 318 lb vertically and 225 lb horizontally. At 120°, those numbers become 450 lb and 390 lb.

**The structure must be verified for the actual combined load direction — not just the vertical component.** A structure rated for 1,000 lb of vertical force may have a significantly different limit when loaded at an angle.

**This verification must come from documented structural capacity — not from visual inspection, past experience, or assumption.** If it is not confirmed, the lift is not approved.

---

## Step 6: Communication and Safety Controls

**Lift Lead**
One named person holds command authority for the lift. All hoist commands go through that person — no exceptions.

**Stop Signal**
Every crew member present must know the stop signal before the load moves. The safest default when commands conflict or a concern is raised: **stop.**

**Exclusion Zone**
No personnel under or within the fall zone of the suspended load. Confirmed before the lift begins and maintained throughout.

**Post-Tensioning Check**
The lift pauses after initial tensioning. The team checks sling seating, load balance, angle confirmation, and hardware alignment before the load is brought to trim height.

**Stop-Work Triggers**
The lift stops immediately if:

- The actual angle does not match the plan
- Any sling tag is missing or unreadable
- Any component shows damage or deformation
- The load shifts or appears unbalanced
- A person enters the exclusion zone
- Commands are unclear or in conflict
- The structural rating cannot be confirmed
- Any crew member raises a concern

   381	### Critical Rules for docx-js
   382	
   383	- **Set page size explicitly** - docx-js defaults to A4; use US Letter (12240 x 15840 DXA) for US documents
   384	- **Landscape: pass portrait dimensions** - docx-js swaps width/height internally; pass short edge as `width`, long edge as `height`, and set `orientation: PageOrientation.LANDSCAPE`
   385	- **Never use `\n`** - use separate Paragraph elements
   386	- **Never use unicode bullets** - use `LevelFormat.BULLET` with numbering config
   387	- **PageBreak must be in Paragraph** - standalone creates invalid XML
   388	- **ImageRun requires `type`** - always specify png/jpg/etc
   389	- **Always set table `width` with DXA** - never use `WidthType.PERCENTAGE` (breaks in Google Docs)
   390	- **Tables need dual widths** - `columnWidths` array AND cell `width`, both must match
   391	- **Table width = sum of columnWidths** - for DXA, ensure they add up exactly
   392	- **Always add cell margins** - use `margins: { top: 80, bottom: 80, left: 120, right: 120 }` for readable padding
   393	- **Use `ShadingType.CLEAR`** - never SOLID for table shading
   394	- **Never use tables as dividers/rules** - cells have minimum height and render as empty boxes (including in headers/footers); use `border: { bottom: { style: BorderStyle.SINGLE, size: 6, color: "2E75B6", space: 1 } }` on a Paragraph instead. For two-column footers, use tab stops (see Tab Stops section), not tables
   395	- **TOC requires HeadingLevel only** - no custom styles on heading paragraphs
   396	- **Override built-in styles** - use exact IDs: "Heading1", "Heading2", etc.
   397	- **Include `outlineLevel`** - required for TOC (0 for H1, 1 for H2, etc.)

   401	## Editing Existing Documents
   402	
   403	**Follow all 3 steps in order.**
   404	
   405	### Step 1: Unpack
   406	```bash
   407	python scripts/office/unpack.py document.docx unpacked/
   408	```
   409	Extracts XML, pretty-prints, merges adjacent runs, and converts smart quotes to XML entities (`&#x201C;` etc.) so they survive editing. Use `--merge-runs false` to skip run merging.
   410	
   411	### Step 2: Edit XML
   412	
   413	Edit files in `unpacked/word/`. See XML Reference below for patterns.
   414	
   415	**Use "Claude" as the author** for tracked changes and comments, unless the user explicitly requests use of a different name.
   416	
   417	**Use the Edit tool directly for string replacement. Do not write Python scripts.** Scripts introduce unnecessary complexity. The Edit tool shows exactly what is being replaced.
   418	
   419	**CRITICAL: Use smart quotes for new content.** When adding text with apostrophes or quotes, use XML entities to produce smart quotes:
   420	```xml
   421	<!-- Use these entities for professional typography -->
   422	<w:t>Here&#x2019;s a quote: &#x201C;Hello&#x201D;</w:t>
   423	```
   424	| Entity | Character |
   425	|--------|-----------|
   426	| `&#x2018;` | ‘ (left single) |
   427	| `&#x2019;` | ’ (right single / apostrophe) |
   428	| `&#x201C;` | “ (left double) |
   429	| `&#x201D;` | ” (right double) |
   430	
   431	**Adding comments:** Use `comment.py` to handle boilerplate across multiple XML files (text must be pre-escaped XML):
   432	```bash
   433	python scripts/comment.py unpacked/ 0 "Comment text with &amp; and &#x2019;"
   434	python scripts/comment.py unpacked/ 1 "Reply text" --parent 0  # reply to comment 0
   435	python scripts/comment.py unpacked/ 0 "Text" --author "Custom Author"  # custom author name
   436	```
   437	Then add markers to document.xml (see Comments in XML Reference).
   438	
   439	### Step 3: Pack
   440	```bash
   441	python scripts/office/pack.py unpacked/ output.docx --original document.docx
   442	```
   443	Validates with auto-repair, condenses XML, and creates DOCX. Use `--validate false` to skip.
   444	
   445	**Auto-repair will fix:**
   446	- `durableId` >= 0x7FFFFFFF (regenerates valid ID)
   447	- Missing `xml:space="preserve"` on `<w:t>` with whitespace
   448	
   449	**Auto-repair won't fix:**
   450	- Malformed XML, invalid element nesting, missing relationships, schema violations
   451	
   452	### Common Pitfalls
   453	
   454	- **Replace entire `<w:r>` elements**: When adding tracked changes, replace the whole `<w:r>...</w:r>` block with `<w:del>...<w:ins>...` as siblings. Don't inject tracked change tags inside a run.
   455	- **Preserve `<w:rPr>` formatting**: Copy the original run's `<w:rPr>` block into your tracked change runs to maintain bold, font size, etc.

   459	## XML Reference
   460	
   461	### Schema Compliance
   462	
   463	- **Element order in `<w:pPr>`**: `<w:pStyle>`, `<w:numPr>`, `<w:spacing>`, `<w:ind>`, `<w:jc>`, `<w:rPr>` last
   464	- **Whitespace**: Add `xml:space="preserve"` to `<w:t>` with leading/trailing spaces
   465	- **RSIDs**: Must be 8-digit hex (e.g., `00AB1234`)
   466	
   467	### Tracked Changes
   468	
   469	**Insertion:**
   470	```xml
   471	<w:ins w:id="1" w:author="Claude" w:date="2025-01-01T00:00:00Z">
   472	  <w:r><w:t>inserted text</w:t></w:r>
   473	</w:ins>
   474	```
   475	
   476	**Deletion:**
   477	```xml
   478	<w:del w:id="2" w:author="Claude" w:date="2025-01-01T00:00:00Z">
   479	  <w:r><w:delText>deleted text</w:delText></w:r>
   480	</w:del>
   481	```
   482	
   483	**Inside `<w:del>`**: Use `<w:delText>` instead of `<w:t>`, and `<w:delInstrText>` instead of `<w:instrText>`.
   484	
   485	**Minimal edits** - only mark what changes:
   486	```xml
   487	<!-- Change "30 days" to "60 days" -->
   488	<w:r><w:t>The term is </w:t></w:r>
   489	<w:del w:id="1" w:author="Claude" w:date="...">
   490	  <w:r><w:delText>30</w:delText></w:r>
   491	</w:del>
   492	<w:ins w:id="2" w:author="Claude" w:date="...">
   493	  <w:r><w:t>60</w:t></w:r>
   494	</w:ins>
   495	<w:r><w:t> days.</w:t></w:r>
   496	```
   497	
   498	**Deleting entire paragraphs/list items** - when removing ALL content from a paragraph, also mark the paragraph mark as deleted so it merges with the next paragraph. Add `<w:del/>` inside `<w:pPr><w:rPr>`:
   499	```xml
   500	<w:p>
   501	  <w:pPr>
   502	    <w:numPr>...</w:numPr>  <!-- list numbering if present -->
   503	    <w:rPr>
   504	      <w:del w:id="1" w:author="Claude" w:date="2025-01-01T00:00:00Z"/>
   505	    </w:rPr>
   506	  </w:pPr>
   507	  <w:del w:id="2" w:author="Claude" w:date="2025-01-01T00:00:00Z">
   508	    <w:r><w:delText>Entire paragraph content being deleted...</w:delText></w:r>
   509	  </w:del>
   510	</w:p>
   511	```
   512	Without the `<w:del/>` in `<w:pPr><w:rPr>`, accepting changes leaves an empty paragraph/list item.
   513	
   514	**Rejecting another author's insertion** - nest deletion inside their insertion:
   515	```xml
   516	<w:ins w:author="Jane" w:id="5">
   517	  <w:del w:author="Claude" w:id="10">
   518	    <w:r><w:delText>their inserted text</w:delText></w:r>
   519	  </w:del>
   520	</w:ins>
   521	```
   522	
   523	**Restoring another author's deletion** - add insertion after (don't modify their deletion):
   524	```xml
   525	<w:del w:author="Jane" w:id="5">
   526	  <w:r><w:delText>deleted text</w:delText></w:r>
   527	</w:del>
   528	<w:ins w:author="Claude" w:id="10">
   529	  <w:r><w:t>deleted text</w:t></w:r>
   530	</w:ins>
   531	```
   532	
   533	### Comments
   534	
   535	After running `comment.py` (see Step 2), add markers to document.xml. For replies, use `--parent` flag and nest markers inside the parent's.
   536	
   537	**CRITICAL: `<w:commentRangeStart>` and `<w:commentRangeEnd>` are siblings of `<w:r>`, never inside `<w:r>`.**
   538	
   539	```xml
   540	<!-- Comment markers are direct children of w:p, never inside w:r -->
   541	<w:commentRangeStart w:id="0"/>

   549	<!-- Comment 0 with reply 1 nested inside -->
