# Lesson 1: Introduction to Predictive Hazard Recognition
### Course: Predictive Hazard Recognition | The Crew Blueprint

---

## Lesson Overview

**Audience:** Live event crews and stagehands
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Lesson Position:** Course foundation — sets the mindset for every lesson that follows

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Define predictive hazard recognition in your own words
2. Distinguish reactive safety from proactive safety
3. Identify examples of static and dynamic hazards in live-event environments
4. Explain why live events demand anticipation, not just compliance
5. Describe how this course builds on each concept, lesson by lesson

---

## Opening Scenario

Picture this.

It's load-in day. Your crew is pulling feeder cable across a path that scenic carpenters are actively using. A lift is running upstage. Road cases are stacked near the dock door. Lighting in the wing is dim. Cable ramps haven't been installed yet.

Ask yourself three questions:

- **What hazards exist right now?**
- **What hazards could appear in the next ten minutes?**
- **What makes this scene significantly more dangerous during show mode or strike?**

If your answers to questions two and three came slower than question one — this course is built for exactly that gap.

Spotting what's already dangerous is the baseline. **Predicting what becomes dangerous is the skill.**

---

## What Is Predictive Hazard Recognition?

**Predictive hazard recognition** is the practice of identifying unsafe conditions, behaviors, interactions, and environmental changes *before* they produce harm — by actively scanning your work environment and anticipating how hazards may evolve over time.

It is not a checklist. It is not just rule compliance. It is a mental habit built on a single recurring question:

> *"What is safe right now that won't be safe once the show starts moving?"*

Live events are dynamic systems. Crews, performers, forklifts, rigging, scenic pieces, temporary power, lighting shifts, crowd movement, fatigue, and schedule pressure all interact in the same compressed space. A condition that looks controlled at 10 AM can become a serious hazard by doors-open.

Predictive hazard recognition is the habit of reading that shift before it happens.

---

## Reactive vs. Proactive Safety

Both approaches have a role in live-event safety. But they are not equal.

| Approach | Core Question | Live-Event Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Reactive** | What happened? | A stagehand trips on a cable — then a ramp gets installed |
| **Proactive** | What could happen next? | Cable is rerouted before doors open |
| **Reactive** | Who was involved? | A near miss is reported after a ladder shifts underfoot |
| **Proactive** | What conditions made this likely? | Ladder placement, deck traffic, and low light are corrected before use |
| **Reactive** | How do we prevent recurrence? | Procedure changes after an incident |
| **Proactive** | How do we prevent occurrence? | Crew scans for weak signals before work begins |

Reactive safety is necessary — but in live events, it often means the exposure already happened. Proactive safety moves the decision point *before* the harm.

---

## Static vs. Dynamic Hazards

Understanding this distinction is the foundation of everything else in this course.

### Static Hazards
A **static hazard** is a relatively fixed unsafe condition in the environment. It may not move, but it still creates risk.

**Live-event examples:**
- Uncovered cable runs across a walking path
- Low-hanging truss or scenery
- Open orchestra pit
- Uneven deck transition
- Poorly lit backstage passage
- Stacked road cases blocking egress
- Exposed power distribution equipment

Static hazards are often visible — but they are frequently *normalized* by experienced crews. That familiarity is its own risk. People stop noticing what they see every day.

### Dynamic Hazards
A **dynamic hazard** changes because people, equipment, timing, visibility, or energy conditions change.

**Live-event examples:**
- Forklift movement during active load-in
- Moving scenic wagons during show
- Performers entering from blind backstage corners
- Rigging operations happening overhead while deck work continues below
- Atmospheric effects — haze, pyro, lasers — obscuring sight lines
- Crowd surges near barricades
- Crew fatigue compounding risk during late-night strike
- Low-light transitions into show mode
- Multiple departments working simultaneously in the same zone

---

> **The key distinction:**
> *Static hazards are what is already there. Dynamic hazards are what happens when the show starts moving.*

---

## Why Live Events Demand Predictive Thinking

A concert stage is not a stable workplace. It is a temporary, compressed, multi-employer environment that transforms three times in a single day — construction site at load-in, performance space at show, demolition zone at strike.

Risk multipliers stack fast:

- Time pressure and tight schedules
- Changing or low-light conditions
- Noise and communication barriers
- Multiple crews operating simultaneously
- Heavy equipment sharing space with pedestrians
- Fatigue during extended calls
- Last-minute production changes
- Audience presence adding pressure and unpredictability
- Weather exposure at outdoor events

Experienced workers sometimes miss hazards because conditions are familiar. Newer workers miss them because they haven't yet built pattern recognition. **Predictive hazard training helps both groups** by making the scanning process explicit and repeatable.

Compliance with rules matters — but live-event environments change too quickly for rules alone to catch every risk. Real safety requires anticipation and adaptive judgment alongside the rulebook.

# Lesson 2: The Three-Zone Assessment Method
### Course: Predictive Hazard Recognition | The Crew Blueprint

---

## Lesson Overview

**Audience:** Live event crews and stagehands
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Lesson Position:** Translates the Lesson 1 mindset into a repeatable physical scanning routine

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Identify potential hazards in the overhead, deck, and peripheral zones of a live-event environment
2. Distinguish static hazards from dynamic hazards within each zone
3. Apply the Three-Zone Assessment Method to real production scenarios
4. Explain why random or task-focused scanning leaves hazards undetected
5. Describe when and how to rescan as conditions change

---

## From Mindset to Method

Lesson 1 established the core question: *What could become unsafe when the show starts moving?*

Lesson 2 gives you a structure for answering it.

The problem with unstructured scanning is that people naturally look at whatever their task demands — usually straight ahead, at eye level. That leaves entire categories of hazard unexamined. What is above you. What is at your feet. What is entering the space from outside your line of sight.

The **Three-Zone Assessment Method** solves this by dividing the work environment into three spatial zones and building a scanning habit around all three — every time, before conditions change.

> **Before you start work: scan the air, the deck, and the edges.**

---

## The Three Zones

---

### Zone 1 — Overhead

**What it covers:** Everything above head level that could fall, swing, descend, shift, energize, or create a struck-by hazard below.

**Live-event examples:**
- Truss, rigging points, and chain motors
- Lighting fixtures and video walls
- Line arrays and speaker hangs
- Scenic pieces and soft goods on tracks
- Ladders and elevated work platforms
- Suspended loads mid-pick
- Dropped tools from above
- Automated rigging in motion

**The key risk:** Workers focus at eye level and task level. The overhead zone gets missed — until something comes down.

A suspended truss is a static overhead condition. The moment a crew begins focusing fixtures above an active work area, that same truss becomes a dynamic hazard zone. The predictive control is not "watch your head." It is establishing an exclusion zone *before* overhead work begins.

**Overhead zone prompts:**
- What is suspended above me?
- Could anything fall, swing, drop, or be lowered?
- Is anyone working above this area right now?
- Are tools, fixtures, and loads properly secured?
- Will rigging, automation, or lighting change during the next phase?

---

### Zone 2 — Deck

**What it covers:** The floor, stage deck, ramps, stairs, platforms, and any object or condition that affects walking, pushing, rolling, lifting, or access.

**Live-event examples:**
- Cable runs and cable ramps
- Uneven deck seams and transitions
- Open traps and orchestra pits
- Temporary stairs without adequate marking
- Wet or slick surfaces near catering, dock, or exterior entries
- Road cases, carts, and rolling equipment
- Floor pockets and power distribution
- Low scenic braces and ground-level rigging hardware
- Blocked exits and congested aisles
- Trash, tape, soft goods, or tools left underfoot

**The key risk:** The deck zone is where the majority of common injuries occur — slips, trips, falls, struck-by, caught-between, and ergonomic strain. Many of these hazards are visible but normalized. Crews stop seeing what they walk past every day.

A cable run taped across the stage is a static deck hazard. During a blackout transition, with performers moving at speed, it becomes a dynamic one. The condition did not change — the environment around it did.

**Deck zone prompts:**
- Where are my feet going?
- What could trip, slip, roll, or shift?
- Can cases, lifts, carts, or performers pass through safely?
- Are aisles, exits, and access routes clear?
- Will this deck look different in show lighting?
- Could this path become congested during a cue, changeover, or strike?

---

### Zone 3 — Peripheral

**What it covers:** What is beside, behind, approaching, or adjacent to the worker's immediate task area — blind spots, moving equipment, crossing departments, audience flow, and changing access routes.

**Live-event examples:**
- Forklifts entering from dock doors
- Performers moving through wings
- Stagehands pushing cases from behind
- Scenic wagons crossing from offstage
- Security or medical moving through backstage corridors
- Audience overflow near barricades
- Vehicles entering outdoor event sites
- Adjacent departments working at the edges of your zone
- Blind corners and temporary walls blocking sightlines
- Crew stepping into lift or rigging areas without announcement

**The key risk:** Peripheral hazards are dangerous precisely because they approach from outside the worker's visual and mental focus. Under time pressure and noise, attention narrows. You become locked onto your task while something enters your space from a direction you are not watching.

A stagehand kneeling to secure a scenic element near an offstage wing may have a clear deck in front of them. But a rolling case or a performer in a quick-change rush can enter from the side in seconds. The hazard was never in their direct line of sight.

**Peripheral zone prompts:**
- Who or what could enter my space?
- What is behind me right now?
- Where are the blind corners?
- Are different departments crossing paths nearby?
- Could audience, performer, vehicle, or crew traffic shift suddenly?
- Do I have a clear escape path or a safe standing position?

---

## Static and Dynamic Hazards Across All Three Zones

Every zone contains both. The critical teaching point is that **static hazards become significantly more dangerous when dynamic activity enters the zone.**

| Zone | Static Hazard | Dynamic Hazard |
|---|---|---|
| **Overhead** | Suspended truss at trim | Truss being raised or lowered |
| **Overhead** | Tools on lift platform | Worker using tools above active crew |
| **Deck** | Cable run taped to stage | Cable run in use during blackout transition |
| **Deck** | Road case parked in wing | Road case pushed through crowded strike path |
| **Peripheral** | Blind corner backstage | Forklift entering from that blind corner |
| **Peripheral** | Adjacent crew work area | Two departments converging during a changeover |

This is Lesson 1 applied spatially. The mindset asks *what could change*. The method tells you *where to look for it*.

---

## Applying the Method: Three Production Scenarios

### Scenario A — Load-In on Stage
Lighting is assembling truss. Audio is pulling feeder cable. Scenic is unloading carts. Forklifts are moving through the dock.

- **Overhead:** Truss, motors, tools, lift work, rigging hardware
- **Deck:** Cables, cases, uneven ramps, open floor pockets, rolling carts
- **Peripheral:** Forklifts, adjacent departments, blind dock entrances, pedestrians

**Predictive controls:** Exclusion zones, marked travel lanes, staged work sequencing, radio communication, spotters

---

### Scenario B — Show Mode Backstage
House is open. Backstage lights are dimmed. Performers are crossing through wings. Props and wardrobe are staged near quick-change areas.

- **Overhead:** Low scenic elements, hanging soft goods, moving automation
- **Deck:** Cables, props, glow tape condition, costume pieces, low-light surfaces
- **Peripheral:** Performers entering at speed, crew moving silently, cue-driven traffic

**Predictive controls:** Show-light walkthrough before doors, marked traffic routes, removal of nonessential cases, cue-based crew awareness

---

### Scenario C — Strike and Load-Out
Show is over. Crew are fatigued. Cases return to the deck. Multiple departments working simultaneously to remove gear.

- **Overhead:** De-rigging operations, lowered truss, chain motors, tools
- **Deck:** Clutter, cables being pulled, rolling cases, trash, wet floors
- **Peripheral:** Forklifts, trucks, crews moving fast with reduced attention

**Predictive controls:** Strike safety huddle, refreshed deck lighting, department sequencing, clear travel lanes, spotters, fatigue checks

---

## How to Run a Zone Scan

Use this six-step sequence every time you enter a work area or conditions change:

1. **Freeze the scene** — Pause before beginning work. Don't just walk in and start.
2. **Scan overhead** — Look for suspended, elevated, moving, or falling-object hazards.
3. **Scan the deck** — Look for trip, slip, roll, pinch, access, and electrical hazards.
4. **Scan the peripheral space** — Look for approaching people, equipment, blind spots, and adjacent operations.
5. **Predict the next change** — Ask what will happen when another department, cue, lighting shift, vehicle, or performer enters this space.
6. **Communicate or control** — Mark it, move it, stop work, report it, reroute it, or escalate it.

---

## Common Scanning Errors

**Scanning only at eye level** — Most people default to what is directly in front of them. Make looking up and looking down a deliberate habit.

**Treating hazards as fixed** — A cable is not just a cable. It is a different hazard when the lights go out and people start moving fast.

**Ignoring adjacent work** — Your task may be safe in isolation. It may not be safe when another department starts working beside you.

**Assuming someone else handled it** — Live events involve multiple employers and crews. Verify that controls are in place. Do not assume.

**Failing to rescan** — A zone scan is not one-and-done. Rescan when the phase changes, when lighting changes, when equipment moves, or when new crews enter.

---

## Lesson 2 Takeaway

> **The Three-Zone Assessment Method teaches workers to scan overhead, deck, and peripheral spaces so they can recognize hazards before movement, timing, lighting, or other crews turn them into incidents.**

Scan the air. Scan the deck. Scan the edges. Then ask what changes next.

# Lesson 3: Show Flow Risk Lifecycle
### Course: Predictive Hazard Recognition | The Crew Blueprint

---

## Lesson Overview

**Audience:** Live event crews and stagehands
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Lesson Position:** Applies the three-zone scanning habit across all three phases of the production day

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Identify the three phases of the Show Flow Risk Lifecycle and their primary risk patterns
2. Recognize how the same hazard changes across build, push, and strike
3. Anticipate pinch points and explain when and why they become dangerous
4. Describe how low-light conditions change hazard visibility and worker behavior
5. Identify fatigue as a predictable risk multiplier and apply proactive controls

---

## The Central Idea

Lesson 2 taught you *where* to look — overhead, deck, and peripheral zones.

Lesson 3 teaches you *when* those zones change.

The most important shift in thinking for this lesson is this: **a hazard check is only valid for the conditions present at the time it was made.** Once lighting, crew traffic, audience presence, work sequencing, or worker fatigue changes — the hazard profile changes with it.

A cable ramp, a parked lift, a backstage wing, a dock entrance — none of these present the same risk from load-in through load-out. The environment transforms three times in a single production day, and each transformation brings its own risk pattern.

> **Build creates hazards. Push hides hazards. Strike multiplies hazards.**

---

## The Three Phases

| Phase | Common Term | Primary Risk Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **Build** | Load-in / Setup | Heavy equipment, rigging, temporary structures, simultaneous departments |
| **Push** | Show / Doors-Open | Low light, performers, audience movement, cue pressure, restricted access |
| **Strike** | Load-out / Teardown | Fatigue, rushing, clutter, reduced attention, reverse sequencing |

---

## Phase 1 — Build / Load-In

The build phase functions like a temporary construction site. Structures, power, rigging, audio, lighting, video, scenic, barricade, and staging elements are all being installed simultaneously, often under time pressure, in a space that is not yet finished.

**Common build-phase hazards:**
- Active overhead work and suspended loads mid-pick
- Forklifts, telehandlers, and pallet jacks moving through shared paths
- Temporary power being installed and energized
- Cable pulls crossing pedestrian routes
- Unfinished walking surfaces, open pits, and unmarked edges
- Multiple departments working in overlapping zones
- Gear staged in ways that temporarily block access
- Incomplete barricades and unclear traffic routes
- Weather exposure at outdoor sites

**The core build-phase risk** is that systems are not yet complete. A stage may not have all rails installed. Cable ramps may not be placed yet. One department may assume another has already controlled a hazard. What looks like a finished condition may still be a work-in-progress.

**Build-phase prompts:**
- What is incomplete right now?
- What is moving overhead?
- What is energized?
- What department is about to enter this space?
- What temporary condition could someone mistake for a finished one?

---

## Phase 2 — Push / Show Operation

When doors open, the environment changes fundamentally. The space becomes operationally constrained. There is less room, less light, more people, more noise, and far less ability to stop and correct hazards once the show is running.

**Common push-phase hazards:**
- Low light and blackout transitions
- Performer traffic through wings and backstage routes
- Quick changes requiring fast movement through tight spaces
- Moving scenery and automation cues
- Cue pressure reducing time available to respond
- Crowd density near barricades, entrances, exits, and concessions
- Restricted emergency access paths
- Noise interfering with communication
- Atmospheric effects — haze, pyro, lasers — obscuring sightlines
- Backstage clutter hidden from audience view
- Hazards that can no longer be easily corrected mid-show

**The core push-phase risk** is concealment. A deck hazard that was obvious under work light becomes invisible under show light. A backstage path that was clear during soundcheck becomes crowded during quick changes. The hazards did not necessarily get worse — but your ability to see and respond to them did.

**Push-phase prompts:**
- What changes when the house opens?
- What disappears when work lights go out?
- Where will performers move quickly in low light?
- Where could audience flow block emergency movement?
- What hazard can no longer be corrected once the show starts?

---

## Phase 3 — Strike / Load-Out

The show is over. The audience is leaving or gone. The crew is tired. The schedule is compressed. Every department is working in reverse — and multiple departments may be doing it at the same time.

Strike is frequently one of the highest-risk phases of the production day, precisely because it feels like the hard part is over.

**Common strike-phase hazards:**
- Fatigue-induced errors and reduced attention
- Rushing to meet truck call or venue curfew
- Road cases returning to deck pathways
- Cables being pulled quickly across active routes
- Overhead de-rigging with crew moving below
- Dropped objects from elevated work
- Clutter building as gear comes down faster than it can be organized
- Low light after show systems are dimmed or shut down
- Incomplete communication between departments working simultaneously
- Complacency — the psychological trap of "it's basically done"

**The core strike-phase risk** is the convergence of fatigue, speed, and reduced controls. Controls that were in place during build may already be removed. Lighting may be partial. The site is becoming less organized, not more. And the crew has been on their feet since load-in.

**Strike-phase prompts:**
- What safety controls are being removed right now?
- What hazards are returning to the deck?
- Who is tired? Who is rushing?
- What overhead work is happening while others move below?
- What would we do differently if this were the start of the day?

---

## Pinch Points

A pinch point is any location or moment where people, equipment, materials, or time pressure converge and reduce the margin for safe movement.

**Physical pinch points in live events:**
- Dock doors and truck ramps
- Stage wings and crossovers
- Backstage corridors
- Barricade openings
- FOH mix positions
- Quick-change areas
- Narrow gaps between scenic pieces
- Case storage zones
- Temporary stair units

**Operational pinch points:**
- Final hour before doors
- Audience ingress and egress
- Set changes and quick changes
- Department changeovers
- Strike start

Pinch points are not always hazardous in isolation. They become dangerous when flow increases, space narrows, and escape routes disappear. A dock door is low risk during a quiet moment of load-in. It becomes high risk when trucks, forklifts, pedestrians, and rolling cases converge at the same time under schedule pressure.

**Pinch-point prompts:**
- What space becomes crowded during the next phase?
- What path has no good alternate route?
- Where could someone be caught between moving equipment and a fixed structure?
- What happens if this area is blocked during an emergency?

---

## Low-Light Conditions

Live events intentionally reduce light for artistic and operational reasons. This is not an exception — it is routine. Every production day includes at least one significant lighting transition, and many include prolonged periods of low or no working light backstage.

**What low light does to hazard visibility:**
- Trip hazards disappear visually
- Deck edges become harder to judge
- Cable ramps blend into the floor
- Performers move quickly through dark wing spaces
- Hand signals and non-verbal communication fail
- Black scenic elements obscure obstacles
- Glow tape that is missing, worn, or blocked provides no protection
- Peripheral movement becomes harder to detect

**Low-light prompts:**
- What hazard is clearly visible now but will disappear under show light?
- What needs glow tape, supplemental lighting, or rerouting before work lights go down?
- What path will performers use at speed in the dark?
- What control still works when visibility drops?

---

## Fatigue-Induced Errors

Fatigue is predictable. On a long production day — especially one that ends with a late-night strike — it will be present. The question is not whether it will affect the crew. The question is whether you account for it before it produces an incident.

**Fatigue risk indicators:**
- Long work hours or early call following late load-out
- Missed breaks and dehydration
- Repeated heavy lifts across a full shift
- Increasing near misses or close calls
- Workers skipping steps or taking shortcuts
- Poor housekeeping as energy drops
- Irritability and reduced communication
- Overconfidence: *"Let's just get it done"*

**What fatigue affects:** attention, reaction time, balance, memory, judgment, and willingness to stop and raise a concern. All of these are exactly what live-event safety depends on.

**Fatigue-related prompts:**
- What task is being performed after people are already tired?
- What work should slow down or be sequenced differently?
- Where do we need a spotter or second set of eyes?
- What would this look like if we were doing it at the start of the day?

---

## Lifecycle Risk Matrix

The same hazard presents differently in every phase:

| Hazard | Build / Load-In | Push / Show | Strike / Load-Out |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cable runs** | Being installed, ramps incomplete | Hidden in low light, performer traffic | Pulled quickly, tangled, active trip hazard |
| **Road cases** | Staging clutter, rolling movement | Backstage obstruction | Heavy rolling traffic, rushed truck pack |
| **Rigging / Truss** | Active overhead work | Suspended load over performance area | De-rigging, dropped-object risk |
| **Temporary power** | Installation, energization | Protected but operationally critical | Disconnects, exposed lines, reduced lighting |
| **Lighting** | Work light available | Show light, blackout, cue transitions | Partial teardown lighting, fatigue added |
| **Crew condition** | Fresh but schedule-pressured | Focused, cue-driven | Fatigued, rushing, reduced attention |
| **Access paths** | Changing as build progresses | Restricted by show operation | Blocked by cases, cable, and trucks |

---

## Lesson 3 Takeaway

> **The same space becomes a different risk environment as the show moves from build to push to strike. Every phase deserves a fresh scan.**

Hazards do not stay where you last left them. Build creates them. Push hides them. Strike multiplies them.

# Lesson 4: Stop-Work Authority Protocol
### Course: Predictive Hazard Recognition | The Crew Blueprint

---

## Lesson Overview

**Audience:** Live event crews and stagehands
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Lesson Position:** Teaches how to act on what earlier lessons trained you to see

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Define stop-work authority and explain when it applies in live-event environments
2. Use clear, direct language to communicate a stop under production pressure
3. Execute the stop-work protocol from recognition through safe resumption
4. Identify common barriers that prevent workers from calling a stop
5. Describe what a safe restart requires — and what it does not

---

## From Recognition to Action

Lessons 1 through 3 built a scanning and prediction habit. You learned what hazards are, where to look for them, and how they change across the production day.

Lesson 4 is about what happens when you find one mid-operation.

Seeing a hazard is not enough. In a live-event environment — where lifts are moving, cases are rolling, truss is flying, performers are crossing in the dark, and the pressure to make the cue is real — recognition only matters if you are willing and able to act on it.

Stop-work authority is that action.

> **A stop is not a failure. A stop is a control.**

---

## What Stop-Work Authority Is

**Stop-work authority** is the right and responsibility of any crew member to pause a task, activity, movement, or operation when they believe there is an imminent or developing safety concern.

This applies to:
- Rigging and truss operations
- Lift and forklift movement
- Truck loading and dock operations
- Scenic movement and automation
- Cable pulls
- Electrical work
- Work at height
- Strike and load-out activities
- Any unclear, uncontrolled, or unexpected hazard condition

Two words in that definition carry equal weight: **right** and **responsibility.**

Stop-work authority is not a privilege reserved for supervisors. It belongs to every person on the crew. And it is not optional — when the trigger is present, calling the stop is part of the job.

---

## When to Use It

A practical rule:

> **Use stop-work when continuing the task could expose someone to harm before the hazard can be clarified or controlled.**

Stop-work applies when there is:

- Immediate or developing danger
- Unclear or missing communication
- A missing control measure
- Unexpected movement or equipment in the area
- A person in a restricted or exclusion zone
- Low visibility obscuring a hazard
- Equipment that appears unstable or failing
- Unplanned overlap between departments
- Unsafe rushing or fatigue-related errors
- A mismatch between the plan and actual conditions
- A gut feeling that something is not right

That last one matters. **Uncertainty is a valid stop-work trigger when potential harm is serious.** You do not need to be certain a hazard exists. You need to believe it might — and that the consequence of being wrong is unacceptable.

---

## Stop-Work Does Not Always Mean Full Shutdown

In live-event production, learners sometimes hesitate to call a stop because they believe it means halting the entire show or event. That is rarely the case.

Stop-work is often targeted:

- *"Stop the lift."*
- *"Hold the truck pack."*
- *"Pause the cable pull."*
- *"Hold scenic movement."*
- *"Do not lower the truss."*
- *"Freeze the deck."*

A targeted stop interrupts a specific task or movement while the rest of the operation continues. Full escalation is only required when the hazard affects the broader operation and cannot be isolated.

---

## How to Communicate a Stop

In noisy production environments, vague language fails. Under pressure, people hear what they expect to hear — which means soft warnings get ignored.

**Weak communication:**
- *"Careful."*
- *"Watch out."*
- *"I don't know about this."*
- *"Maybe hold on."*
- *"That seems weird."*

**Effective stop-work communication:**
- *"STOP WORK."*
- *"Stop the truss. Person underneath."*
- *"Hold the lift. No spotter."*
- *"Freeze the deck. Cable hazard in performer path."*
- *"Stop. Do not move. Person in the zone."*
- *"Hold that case. Wet ramp, no assist."*

The structure is simple:

> **Stop → State the hazard → Secure the area → Notify the lead → Resolve → Resume only after confirmation**

Clear. Specific. Loud enough to be heard. No ambiguity about whether work should continue.

---

## The Stop-Work Protocol: Step by Step

### Step 1 — Recognize the Trigger
A worker sees an unsafe or unclear condition that could cause harm before it can be passively controlled.

*Example: A truss is being lowered while a crew member is still crossing underneath.*

---

### Step 2 — Call the Stop Clearly
Use direct, specific language. No hedging.

*"Stop the truss. Person underneath. Clear the overhead zone."*

---

### Step 3 — Make the Area Safe
Pause movement. Secure equipment. Clear people from the hazard zone. Prevent new exposure from entering.

---

### Step 4 — Notify the Responsible Lead
This may be a department head, stage manager, production manager, rigging lead, electrician, forklift operator, or venue representative — whoever owns that activity.

---

### Step 5 — Identify and Control the Hazard
Ask:
- What changed from the plan?
- Who is exposed?
- What control is missing?
- What needs to be moved, marked, rerouted, powered down, or re-briefed?

---

### Step 6 — Confirm Resumption Conditions
Work resumes **only** when the hazard is controlled and the responsible lead communicates that it is safe to continue. Not when the moment passes. Not when someone says *"it's fine."*

> **No restart without a changed condition.**

---

### Step 7 — Capture the Learning
For recurring hazards, document the condition — in a safety huddle note, hazard report, production meeting log, or post-event review. One stop-work event that goes unrecorded is a hazard that will reappear on the next call.

---

## Safe Resumption

Stopping work is half the protocol. Safe restart is the other half.

Work should **not** resume because:
- The immediate moment passed
- A supervisor is frustrated with the delay
- The crew is behind schedule
- The person who called the stop feels pressured to back down
- The hazard is no longer visible but has not been controlled

A safe restart requires:
- Hazard identified and understood
- Affected people cleared or briefed
- Control selected and implemented
- Responsible lead confirms readiness
- Crew understands what changed

> **Restart phrase: "Hazard controlled. Zone clear. Everyone briefed. Resume on lead's call."**

Unsafe restarts recreate the original hazard. In live events, this is common when crews pause briefly, feel the schedule pressure, and go back to work without changing the condition that created the risk.

---

## Live-Event Stop-Work Examples

### Overhead Zone
A rigger begins lowering a truss while a crew member is still crossing underneath.

**Call:** *"Stop the truss. Person underneath. Clear the overhead zone."*
**Resume after:** Zone confirmed clear, spotter assigned, movement resumes on rigging lead's command.

---

### Deck Zone
A performer path crosses a cable ramp that shifted during rehearsal and is now misaligned.

**Call:** *"Stop. Performer path unsafe. Cable ramp shifted."*
**Resume after:** Ramp reset, path verified under show light, stage management notified.

---

### Peripheral Zone
A forklift enters a dock lane while stagehands are pushing cases through the same path.

**Call:** *"Hold forklift. Pedestrians in lane. Need spotter and clear route."*
**Resume after:** Pedestrian movement paused, spotter assigned, lane cleared, forklift resumes with guide.

---

### Strike Fatigue
A tired crew begins pulling feeder cable while another crew rolls heavy cases through the same area.

**Call:** *"Stop cable pull. Case traffic crossing. We need sequencing."*
**Resume after:** Lane separation established, crew briefed, deck lighting improved.

---

## Crew Culture Around Stop-Work

Stop-work authority only functions if the crew culture supports it. A policy that says *"anyone can stop work"* is meaningless if people believe they will be punished, mocked, ignored, or labeled as difficult for using it.

**A healthy stop-work culture looks like this:**
- No retaliation — ever
- No ridicule for raising a concern
- No *"production over safety"* messaging from leadership
- The person who called the stop is thanked, not pressured
- The debrief focuses on the condition, not the personality
- Leaders model the behavior by calling stops themselves

**Common barriers that kill stop-work culture:**

| Barrier | What It Sounds Like | The Reality |
|---|---|---|
| **Hierarchy** | *"Who am I to stop the rigger?"* | Stop-work belongs to everyone — no exceptions |
| **Fear of slowing the show** | *"We'll look bad if we fall behind"* | A stop is professional risk control, not a disruption |
| **Ambiguity** | *"I wasn't totally sure, so I let it go"* | Uncertainty is a valid trigger when stakes are high |
| **Normalization** | *"We always do it this way"* | Familiarity is not the same as safety |
| **Incomplete restart** | *"We paused for a second, it's fine"* | If nothing changed, the hazard is probably still there |

Leadership sets the standard. Department heads, production managers, stage managers, and crew chiefs who respond constructively when someone stops work create an environment where the protocol actually gets used.

---

## Lesson 4 Takeaway

> **Stop-work authority gives every crew member the power and responsibility to pause unsafe work, communicate the hazard clearly, control the condition, and restart only when the crew has confirmed it is safe to continue.**

Anyone can call it. Everyone must honor it. Nothing restarts until something changes.

# Lesson 5: Practical Hazard Identification Exercise
### Course: Predictive Hazard Recognition | The Crew Blueprint

---

## Lesson Overview

**Audience:** Live event crews and stagehands
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Lesson Position:** Applied practice — learners demonstrate the skills built across Lessons 1–4

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Analyze a 360° stage or event-site image to identify visible and hidden hazards
2. Classify each hazard by zone, show phase, and static or dynamic status
3. Identify missing controls and explain what should be in place before work continues
4. Connect hazard observations to recognized safety expectations including OSHA concepts and ANSI/ASSP risk-management principles
5. Determine when stop-work authority is appropriate based on visual evidence

---

## From Discussion to Practice

Every lesson so far has built toward this one.

Lesson 1 gave you the mindset. Lesson 2 gave you the zones. Lesson 3 showed you how risk changes across the production day. Lesson 4 gave you the intervention protocol.

Lesson 5 is where you put all of it to work.

Discussing hazard recognition is not the same as practicing it. In a real venue, you are not handed a list of what to look for. You walk into a space, scan the environment, and decide what needs attention before someone gets hurt. That skill requires practice under realistic visual conditions — not just knowledge of the concepts.

This lesson uses structured 360° stage photo analysis to build that skill.

> **Hazard identification is not a scavenger hunt. It is a disciplined scan for visible hazards, hidden hazards, changing conditions, and missing controls.**

---

## Why Visual Practice Works

People miss hazards not because they are careless, but because of attention limits, visual clutter, task fixation, and unstructured scanning. Research in hazard-recognition training consistently shows that visual practice — analyzing realistic images or panoramic environments — improves a worker's ability to detect hazards because it forces active searching, not passive reading.

The key difference between a knowledge test and a hazard-identification exercise is this: **in a knowledge test, you recall what you know. In a visual exercise, you search for what is actually there — including what is easy to miss.**

That is the skill that keeps people safe on a real call.

---

## How to Run the Exercise

The exercise uses a 360° stage, backstage, dock, or event-site photo. Work through five structured passes. Do not move to the next pass until you have completed the current one.

---

### Pass 1 — First-Pass Scan: What Is Obviously Wrong?

Look at the overall scene. Identify hazards that are immediately visible without additional analysis.

**Common first-pass findings:**
- Cable crossing a walking path
- Road cases blocking an exit or egress route
- Lift parked in a traffic lane
- Unmarked stage edge or open pit
- Ladder in a poor position
- Temporary power distribution in a pathway
- Clutter and poor housekeeping
- Crew working overhead with people below

Write down everything you spot. This is your baseline.

---

### Pass 2 — Three-Zone Scan: Where Is Each Hazard?

Now classify every hazard from Pass 1 — and look for anything you missed — using the three-zone method.

| Zone | What to Inspect |
|---|---|
| **Overhead** | Rigging, truss, motors, fixtures, tools, elevated platforms, secondary safety on fixtures, exclusion zone presence |
| **Deck** | Cables, ramps, floor openings, edges, clutter, rolling cases, wet surfaces, power distribution, access routes, housekeeping |
| **Peripheral** | Blind corners, forklift paths, performer crossover routes, adjacent crew activity, dock doors, audience access points |

For each hazard, assign a zone. Some hazards may span more than one.

---

### Pass 3 — Show-Flow Analysis: When Does Each Hazard Get Worse?

For every hazard identified, predict which production phase increases the risk.

- **Build / Load-in:** Heavy equipment moving, structures incomplete, departments overlapping, controls not yet in place
- **Push / Show:** Low light, performers moving fast, audience present, cues running, no ability to stop and reconfigure
- **Strike / Load-out:** Fatigue, rushing, clutter returning, overhead de-rigging, reduced lighting, multiple departments working simultaneously

A hazard that appears manageable during load-in under work light may become the most dangerous thing on the deck once the show goes into show-mode blackout. Show-flow analysis forces you to think past the moment in front of you.

---

### Pass 4 — Hidden Risk Identification: What Looks Safe Now but Won't Be?

This is the most important pass in the exercise — and the one that separates trained hazard scanners from everyone else.

Look specifically for conditions that appear acceptable under current circumstances but will change.

**Examples of hidden or predictive hazards:**
- A cable ramp that is visible under work light but disappears under show light
- An open deck path that becomes crowded at strike start
- A parked lift that will block emergency access once the show begins
- A backstage route clear now that becomes a performer quick-change path
- A case storage area that becomes a pinch point when doors open
- A truss that appears static but will be lowered during strike while crew works below
- A temporary platform that will be overloaded during a show changeover
- A weather-exposed structure that becomes hazardous in wind or rain

> **The hidden hazard is often the one that appears safe under current conditions.**

---

### Pass 5 — Protocol Critique: What Safety System Failed Here?

Look at the overall scene and ask not just *what is wrong* but *what safety system failed to prevent this.*

**Protocol critique questions:**
- Was there a pre-task safety huddle?
- Was the hazard identified before work began?
- Is the zone controlled and communicated?
- Is the path marked, barricaded, or rerouted?
- Is there a spotter where equipment is moving?
- Is lighting adequate for the task?
- Is PPE appropriate and in use?
- Is the work sequenced safely across departments?
- Is stop-work authority needed right now?
- Is there a safe restart plan if work is paused?

This pass connects visual observation to safety management — and shifts the question from *who missed this* to *what system should have caught it.*

---

## The Hazard Analysis Worksheet

Use this format for every hazard you identify:

| Item | Your Response |
|---|---|
| **Hazard observed** | What do you see? |
| **Zone** | Overhead, deck, or peripheral |
| **Phase affected** | Build, push, strike, or all three |
| **Static or dynamic** | Fixed, changing, or both |
| **Who is exposed** | Crew, performer, audience, vendor, operator |
| **Hidden risk** | What could change later that makes this worse? |
| **Missing control** | Guard, mark, reroute, brief, isolate, spotter, PPE, lighting, housekeeping |
| **Stop-work needed** | Yes or no — and why |
| **Standards reference** | OSHA category, ANSI/ASSP risk concept, or entertainment industry standard |
| **Corrective action** | What must happen before work continues? |

Do not stop at naming the hazard. The worksheet is not complete until you have explained exposure, phase, hidden risk, and control. A hazard without a control recommendation is an observation, not an analysis.

---

## Sample High-Quality Analyses

### Hazard: Cable crossing downstage performer path

- **Zone:** Deck
- **Phase:** Build and push
- **Static or dynamic:** Static now; dynamic during performer movement and blackout transitions
- **Exposed group:** Performers and crew
- **Hidden risk:** Cable is clearly visible under work light. It disappears under show light. A performer moving at speed in a blackout will not see it.
- **Missing control:** Reroute, proper cable ramp, path marking, or route restriction
- **Stop-work needed:** Yes — if performers are about to rehearse or cross before control is installed
- **Standards reference:** OSHA walking-working surface concepts; ANSI/ASSP hazard-control hierarchy
- **Corrective action:** Reroute cable or install cable ramp. Verify visibility and safety under show-light conditions before doors open.

---

### Hazard: Crew working overhead with no exclusion zone below

- **Zone:** Overhead
- **Phase:** Build and strike
- **Static or dynamic:** Dynamic — active work creates falling-object potential
- **Exposed group:** Crew members crossing below elevated work area
- **Hidden risk:** A tool, fixture, or hardware dropped from above may not be visible to workers below until impact
- **Missing control:** Overhead exclusion zone, barricade, spotter, and verbal/radio communication
- **Stop-work needed:** Yes — if people are currently moving through the area below active overhead work
- **Standards reference:** OSHA falling-object protection and work-zone control; entertainment rigging best practices for exclusion zones
- **Corrective action:** Clear the area, establish and mark an exclusion zone, assign a spotter, resume foot traffic only after overhead work is paused or zone is secured

---

## Connecting Observations to Safety Standards

Lesson 5 does not require learners to become legal experts. But it does require learners to understand that recognized safety standards exist for a reason — and that the hazards you identify in a photo are usually covered by one.

**OSHA concepts most relevant to live-event hazard analysis:**
- Walking-working surfaces — slips, trips, falls, and surface conditions
- Fall protection — edges, elevated platforms, open pits
- Electrical safety — exposed power, energized systems
- Material handling — cases, lifts, forklifts, manual handling
- Exit routes and emergency access — egress obstruction
- General Duty Clause — recognized hazards with available controls

**ANSI/ASSP risk-management principles:**
- Identify the hazard
- Assess the risk
- Apply the hierarchy of controls — eliminate, substitute, engineer, administrate, PPE
- Verify the control is working

**Entertainment-industry standards:**
- ANSI E1.21 and related ESTA entertainment rigging standards address overhead rigging inspection, load limits, secondary safety, and exclusion zones
- Stage rigging best practices emphasize visual inspection of hardware, clear exclusion zones during aerial work, and communication between rigging and deck crews

> **A simple learner-facing rule: OSHA identifies the duty. ANSI/ASSP structures the response. Entertainment standards address what is specific to our environment.**

---

## Performance Levels

| Level | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| **Basic** | Identifies obvious visible hazards only |
| **Developing** | Classifies hazards by overhead, deck, and peripheral zone |
| **Proficient** | Predicts how each hazard changes by build, push, and strike phase |
| **Advanced** | Identifies hidden risks, missing protocols, exposure groups, and controls |
| **Expert** | Connects all findings to stop-work authority decisions and recognized safety standards |

The goal of this exercise is to reach proficient and push toward advanced. Expert-level analysis becomes faster and more automatic with repeated practice across multiple scenarios.

---

## Lesson 5 Takeaway

> **Hazard identification is not a scavenger hunt. It is a disciplined scan for visible hazards, hidden hazards, changing conditions, and missing controls — followed by a clear answer to the question: what must change before work continues?**

Do not stop at "there is a cable." Ask who crosses it, when, under what lighting, moving at what speed, with what consequence — and what control belongs there before that moment arrives.

# Lesson 6: Developing Safety Tools for Live Events
### Course: Predictive Hazard Recognition | The Crew Blueprint

---

## Lesson Overview

**Audience:** Live event crews and stagehands
**Duration:** 3–5 minutes
**Type:** Upskilling
**Lesson Position:** Converts hazard-recognition skills into repeatable field tools

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

1. Explain why safety tools are essential in multi-department, multi-employer live-event environments
2. Build a pre-task safety huddle checklist tailored to a specific event phase
3. Complete a hazard reporting template that captures the condition, exposure, and corrective action
4. Describe when and how PPE infographics support crew safety across tasks and phases
5. Select and apply the right tool for a specific event type and risk scenario

---

## From Awareness to Action

Every lesson in this course has built a skill:

- Lesson 1 — Predictive mindset
- Lesson 2 — Zone scanning
- Lesson 3 — Phase-based risk recognition
- Lesson 4 — Stop-work intervention
- Lesson 5 — Applied hazard analysis

Lesson 6 asks a different question: **once you recognize a hazard, what do you leave behind to make sure the next crew member, the next department, and the next phase are also protected?**

Awareness that lives only in your head disappears the moment you walk off the deck. Safety tools make awareness repeatable, communicable, and accountable.

> **A safety tool is only successful if crews actually use it under show conditions.**

That is the design standard for everything in this lesson. Not whether it looks good on paper — whether a tired stagehand will pick it up at 1:00 a.m. during strike and actually use it.

---

## Tool 1 — Safety Huddle Checklist

A safety huddle checklist is a short pre-task or pre-phase discussion guide. It should be used before load-in, before doors, before show operation, before strike, and before any high-risk task.

Keep it brief. A huddle that is too detailed gets skipped. The best huddle tool covers the highest-risk conditions specific to the work about to happen — nothing more.

**Live-event safety huddle checklist:**

| Huddle Item | Prompt |
|---|---|
| **Phase** | Are we in build, push/show, or strike? |
| **Top hazards** | What are the three highest-risk conditions right now? |
| **Overhead zone** | Is anyone working above or below suspended/elevated equipment? |
| **Deck zone** | Are walking paths, cable paths, stairs, ramps, and exits clear? |
| **Peripheral zone** | What vehicles, lifts, performers, audience, or other crews may enter? |
| **Pinch points** | Where will people, cases, carts, or equipment converge? |
| **Low light** | What will change when work lights or show lights shift? |
| **Fatigue** | Is anyone tired, rushing, overloaded, or working alone? |
| **PPE** | What PPE is required for this task or phase? |
| **Stop-work** | Who can call stop-work? Everyone. What phrase will we use? |
| **Changes** | What changed since the last huddle? |
| **Action owner** | Who is fixing each hazard before work continues? |

> **A huddle should end with controls, not just reminders.**

The difference between a useful huddle and a wasted one is whether someone walks away with a task. Identifying a hazard without assigning an owner is not a safety action — it is a conversation.

---

## Tool 2 — Hazard Reporting Template

A hazard reporting template gives any crew member a fast, structured way to document unsafe conditions, near misses, weak signals, and recurring problems. In live events, this tool must be quick to complete. If it feels like bureaucracy, freelancers and local crew will avoid it.

**Hazard report fields:**

| Field | Example |
|---|---|
| **Date / Time** | June 12, 2026 — 9:15 PM |
| **Location** | Stage left wing / dock / FOH / backstage corridor |
| **Show phase** | Build / push / strike |
| **Zone** | Overhead / deck / peripheral |
| **Hazard observed** | Road cases blocking performer crossover path |
| **Who is exposed** | Performers, stagehands, forklift operator, audience |
| **Immediate risk** | Trip, struck-by, blocked egress, pinch point |
| **Static or dynamic** | Static now; dynamic during blackout transition |
| **Immediate action taken** | Cases moved; path taped; department lead notified |
| **Stop-work used** | Yes / No |
| **Further action needed** | Establish permanent case-parking zone before doors |
| **Responsible person** | Department lead / safety lead / production manager |
| **Follow-up status** | Open / Controlled / Resolved |

> **If a hazard is seen twice, it needs a tool, not just a warning.**

A hazard report without an owner is only a note. The template is only complete when someone is named, a deadline is assigned, and the status is tracked.

---

## Tool 3 — PPE Infographic

A PPE infographic is a quick visual reference that tells crew what protective equipment is required for a specific task, zone, or event phase. It is especially useful when teams include workers from multiple employers, departments, and experience levels who may not share the same baseline expectations.

**Live-event PPE reference by condition:**

| Work Condition | PPE Reminder |
|---|---|
| **General load-in / load-out** | Closed-toe safety footwear; gloves as task-appropriate |
| **Forklift / dock / vehicle area** | High-visibility vest; clear pedestrian routes |
| **Overhead work nearby** | Hard hat or bump cap per site policy and hazard assessment |
| **Cable pull / feeder work** | Gloves as appropriate; authorized personnel only near energized systems |
| **Rigging or lift work** | Hard hat; fall protection where required; tool tethering where required |
| **Pyro / special effects area** | Task-specific PPE; access restricted to authorized crew |
| **Loud environments** | Hearing protection |
| **Outdoor daytime work** | Sun and heat protection; hydration |
| **Low-light strike** | High-visibility marking; task lighting; gloves for handling |

PPE infographics work best when they are visual, posted or distributed at the work location, and written in plain language that reads quickly under pressure.

One important design principle:

> **PPE is the last line of defense, not the whole safety plan.**

Hearing protection does not eliminate noise. A hard hat does not prevent dropped objects — it reduces injury if one falls. PPE infographics should always be paired with higher-level controls: eliminate the hazard, substitute a safer method, engineer a guard, or sequence the work differently.

---

## Tool 4 — Phase-Specific Safety Cards

A phase card is a pocket-sized or posted reminder of the top hazards and controls for one specific production phase. It focuses crew attention before the risk shifts — not after.

### Build / Load-In Card

**Focus areas:** Overhead work, temporary structures, forklifts and trucks, cable pulls, incomplete decks, uncontrolled access, power distribution, weather, housekeeping

**Card language:**
*Before build starts — confirm overhead zones, vehicle lanes, cable paths, power locations, and case parking areas. Identify what is incomplete and who owns each control.*

---

### Push / Show Card

**Focus areas:** Low light, performer traffic, audience movement, emergency access, cue pressure, backstage clutter, special effects, noise and communication

**Card language:**
*Before doors — walk performer paths under show-light conditions. Confirm egress, clear wings, secure cable paths, and identify who can call stop-work during show operations.*

---

### Strike / Load-Out Card

**Focus areas:** Fatigue, rushing, low light, rolling cases, de-rigging, cable pulls, truck loading, forklift/pedestrian separation

**Card language:**
*Before strike — reset work lights, hold a strike huddle, separate case traffic from cable pulls, assign spotters, and slow down high-risk tasks.*

---

## Tool 5 — Stop-Work Pocket Card

Lesson 4 taught the protocol. This card makes it operational — something a crew member can carry and reference when they need exact language fast.

**Front — Call Phrases:**

> *Anyone can stop unsafe work.*
>
> - *"Stop work."*
> - *"Hold the lift."*
> - *"Stop the truss."*
> - *"Freeze the deck."*
> - *"Hold that case."*
> - *"Person underneath."*
> - *"Path blocked."*
> - *"No spotter."*

> - Hazard identified
> - Area secured
> - Lead notified
> - Control implemented
> - Crew briefed
> - Resume confirmed by lead

Under pressure, people reach for language they have already practiced. A pocket card removes ambiguity and gives the crew a shared vocabulary.

---

## Tool Design Principles

Every tool built for live-event safety should follow the same design standard:

**Build tools that are:**
- Brief enough to use under pressure
- Visual where possible
- Task and phase specific
- Written in plain language
- Available at the point of work
- Connected to a named action owner
- Reviewed and updated when hazards repeat

**Avoid tools that are:**
- Full of long paragraphs or legal language
- Generic across all events and phases
- Loaded with checkboxes that trigger no action
- Missing a named responsible person
- Completed and then filed without follow-up

> **One tool, one job.**

A huddle checklist guides conversation. A hazard report captures and assigns action. A PPE infographic clarifies protective requirements. A stop-work card provides direct language. A phase card focuses attention before the next risk shift. None of these should try to do all five at once.

---

## Matching Tools to Scenarios

Tools must fit the actual risk profile of the event. A generic checklist is less useful than one built for the specific environment.

| Event Type | Priority Tools |
|---|---|
| **Arena concert strike** | Truck-pack traffic plan, forklift/pedestrian checklist, fatigue risk card, de-rigging exclusion-zone card, stop-work pocket card |

| **Corporate ballroom event** | Cable ramp checklist, public egress checklist, power distribution map, vendor safety note, FOH trip-hazard form |

The tool kit changes because the risk changes. What protects a crew during a festival build is not the same set of tools that protects them during a late-night arena strike.

## The 5-Part Predictive Safety Habit

| Step | Field Question |

## Building a Proactive Safety Culture

Your personal habits are stronger when the crew around you shares the same standard. That is what safety culture means at the crew level — not a policy posted on a wall, but the way the team actually behaves when conditions get difficult.

   453

   459	## XML Reference
   460	
   461	### Schema Compliance
   462	
   463	- **Element order in `<w:pPr>`**: `<w:pStyle>`, `<w:numPr>`, `<w:spacing>`, `<w:ind>`, `<w:jc>`, `<w:rPr>` last
   464	- **Whitespace**: Add `xml:space="preserve"` to `<w:t>` with leading/trailing spaces
   465	- **RSIDs**: Must be 8-digit hex (e.g., `00AB1234`)
   466	
   467	### Tracked Changes
   468	
   469	**Insertion:**
   470	```xml
   471	<w:ins w:id="1" w:author="Claude" w:date="2025-01-01T00:00:00Z">
   472	  <w:r><w:t>inserted text</w:t></w:r>
   473	</w:ins>
   474	```
   475	
   476	**Deletion:**
   477	```xml
   478	<w:del w:id="2" w:author="Claude" w:date="2025-01-01T00:00:00Z">
   479	  <w:r><w:delText>deleted text</w:delText></w:r>
   480	</w:del>
   481	```
   482	
   483	**Inside `<w:del>`**: Use `<w:delText>` instead of `<w:t>`, and `<w:delInstrText>` instead of `<w:instrText>`.
   484	
   485	**Minimal edits** - only mark what changes:
   486	```xml
   487	<!-- Change "30 days" to "60 days" -->
   488	<w:r><w:t>The term is </w:t></w:r>
   489	<w:del w:id="1" w:author="Claude" w:date="...">
   490	  <w:r><w:delText>30</w:delText></w:r>
   491	</w:del>
   492	<w:ins w:id="2" w:author="Claude" w:date="...">
   493	  <w:r><w:t>60</w:t></w:r>
   494	</w:ins>
   495	<w:r><w:t> days.</w:t></w:r>
   496	```
   497	
   498	**Deleting entire paragraphs/list items** - when removing ALL content from a paragraph, also mark the paragraph mark as deleted so it merges with the next paragraph. Add `<w:del/>` inside `<w:pPr><w:rPr>`:
   499	```xml
   500	<w:p>
   501	  <w:pPr>
   502	    <w:numPr>...</w:numPr>  <!-- list numbering if present -->
   503	    <w:rPr>
   504	      <w:del w:id="1" w:author="Claude" w:date="2025-01-01T00:00:00Z"/>
   505	    </w:rPr>
   506	  </w:pPr>
   507	  <w:del w:id="2" w:author="Claude" w:date="2025-01-01T00:00:00Z">
   508	    <w:r><w:delText>Entire paragraph content being deleted...</w:delText></w:r>
   509	  </w:del>
   510	</w:p>
   511	```
   512	Without the `<w:del/>` in `<w:pPr><w:rPr>`, accepting changes leaves an empty paragraph/list item.
   513	
   514	**Rejecting another author's insertion** - nest deletion inside their insertion:
   515	```xml
   516	<w:ins w:author="Jane" w:id="5">
   517	  <w:del w:author="Claude" w:id="10">
   518	    <w:r><w:delText>their inserted text</w:delText></w:r>
   519	  </w:del>
   520	</w:ins>
   521	```
   522	
   523	**Restoring another author's deletion** - add insertion after (don't modify their deletion):
   524	```xml
   525	<w:del w:author="Jane" w:id="5">
   526	  <w:r><w:delText>deleted text</w:delText></w:r>
   527	</w:del>
   528	<w:ins w:author="Claude" w:id="10">
   529	  <w:r><w:t>deleted text</w:t></w:r>
   530	</w:ins>
   531	```
   532	
   533	### Comments
   534	
   535	After running `comment.py` (see Step 2), add markers to document.xml. For replies, use `--parent` flag and nest markers inside the parent's.
   536	
   537	**CRITICAL: `<w:commentRangeStart>` and `<w:commentRangeEnd>` are siblings of `<w:r>`, never inside `<w:r>`.**
   538	
   539	```xml
   540	<!-- Comment markers are direct children of w:p, never inside w:r -->
   541	<w:commentRangeStart w:id="0"/>
   542	<w:del w:id="1" w:author="Claude" w:date="2025-01-01T00:00:00Z">
   543	  <w:r><w:delText>deleted</w:delText></w:r>
   544	</w:del>
   545	<w:r><w:t> more text</w:t></w:r>
   546	<w:commentRangeEnd w:id="0"/>
   547	<w:r><w:rPr><w:rStyle w:val="CommentReference"/></w:rPr><w:commentReference w:id="0"/></w:r>
   548	
   549	<!-- Comment 0 with reply 1 nested inside -->
   550	<w:commentRangeStart w:id="0"/>
   551	  <w:commentRangeStart w:id="1"/>
   552	  <w:r><w:t>text</w:t></w:r>
   553	  <w:commentRangeEnd w:id="1"/>
   554	<w:commentRangeEnd w:id="0"/>
   555	<w:r><w:rPr><w:rStyle w:val="CommentReference"/></w:rPr><w:commentReference w:id="0"/></w:r>
   556	<w:r><w:rPr><w:rStyle w:val="CommentReference"/></w:rPr><w:commentReference w:id="1"/></w:r>
   557	```
