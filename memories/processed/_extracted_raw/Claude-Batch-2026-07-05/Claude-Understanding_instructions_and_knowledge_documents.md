# The Crew Blueprint
## Course 1: Your First Work Call
### Full Lesson Draft — Step 5

---

## Lesson Purpose

Most new stagehands show up to their first call knowing almost nothing about how the day actually works. They know the address. They know the call time. That's usually it.

This lesson closes that gap.

By the time you finish this lesson, you will have a clear mental map of how a professional work call is structured, what is expected of you at each phase, and what separates the crew members who get called back from the ones who don't.

This is not about mastering a craft. That comes later, with time and experience. This is about understanding the environment you are walking into so you can show up ready, stay useful, and leave with your reputation intact.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

- Describe the three primary phases of a work call and what happens during each one
- Identify your place in the chain of command and explain who you report to
- Demonstrate the basic expectations of show call behavior and dock etiquette
- Recognize which tasks require authorization and explain why you do not perform them without proper assignment
- Define the professional habits that drive rehire decisions in this industry

---

## Section 1: The Shape of a Show Day

### What You Are Walking Into

A live event work call is a coordinated, time-compressed operation. Dozens or hundreds of people — sometimes from multiple cities, multiple union locals, and multiple touring organizations — arrive at the same venue and execute a shared plan under serious time pressure.

From the outside, it can look like controlled chaos. From the inside, once you understand the structure, it is actually a highly organized sequence of phases with clear objectives at each stage.

Every call, whether you are working an arena concert, a stadium show, a festival, or a one-off corporate production, follows the same basic shape:

**Load-In → Show Call → Load-Out**

Those three phases define your day. Everything else — the tasks, the pace, the communication, the culture — fits within that structure.

---

### How the Day Is Divided

A typical arena or stadium show day might run something like this:

**Early Morning — Dock Opens, Trucks Arrive**
The day begins at the dock. Trucks from the touring production arrive and are staged in a specific order. The production team already knows which truck holds what and in what sequence it needs to be unloaded. The building has been prepped. Department heads have received their assignments. When local crew arrives, the work begins immediately.

**Morning Through Afternoon — Load-In**
This is the build phase. Audio, lighting, video, rigging, and staging departments each work their own zone while sharing the building. Cases come off trucks, equipment is positioned, systems are built. The pace is steady and purposeful.

**Afternoon — Production Advance and Systems Check**
As departments complete their builds, the focus shifts to testing, adjusting, and preparing for the show. Touring crew leads this phase. Local crew may be transitioning out or moving into show call assignments.

**Evening — Show Call**
Doors open. The audience arrives. The show runs. This phase requires a different kind of professionalism — quiet, positioned, alert, and ready to respond without being in the way.

**After the Show — Load-Out / Strike**
The moment the last note plays, load-out begins. Everything that came in goes back out. It goes faster than load-in. The fatigue is real. The expectations remain high.

---

### Touring Crew vs. Local Crew

Understanding this distinction matters from day one.

**Touring crew** travels with the production. They built the show, they know the system, and they are responsible for how it gets executed at every venue on the run. They are the experts on this specific production.

**Local crew** is hired market by market to provide labor support. That is the role most new stagehands step into first. You are there to support the touring crew's plan — not to improvise your own.

This is not a hierarchy designed to make you feel less important. It is a practical reality of how touring productions function. Your job on your first calls is to be a reliable, efficient, responsive member of the local crew. That is genuinely valuable work. Do it well.

---

## Section 2: Load-In Operations

### What Load-In Actually Looks Like

Load-in is physical, repetitive, and detail-dependent. For entry-level crew, the work often centers on a specific set of tasks:

- Unloading trucks at the dock
- Moving road cases from the dock to the stage or arena floor
- Running cable under the direction of a department lead
- Setting up specific equipment or infrastructure as assigned
- Keeping pathways clear and maintaining organized staging areas

There is a rhythm to it. Experienced hands move with purpose. They are not sprinting, but they are not wandering either. The phrase you will hear — and eventually say — is **hurry without rushing.** Move efficiently. Stay focused. Don't create problems by going too fast.

---

### Dock Etiquette

The dock is one of the most important and most misunderstood spaces on a work call. It is a high-traffic, safety-critical area where trucks, forklifts, road cases, and crew all occupy the same limited space.

A few fundamentals:

**Know where you are supposed to be.** If you were assigned to the dock, be at the dock. If you were assigned to the floor, be on the floor. Do not migrate based on curiosity.

**Follow the flow.** Cases move in a specific direction. Pathways are established for a reason. Move with the flow, not across it.

**Stay aware of equipment in motion.** Forklifts, motorized pallet movers, and large road cases can cause serious injury if you are not paying attention to your surroundings.

**Do not operate equipment you have not been trained and assigned to use.** Forklifts, elevated work platforms, and any powered equipment require proper training, authorization, and assignment. If no one has specifically trained you, assigned you, and cleared you to operate it — your answer is no. This is not optional. This is a safety boundary.

---

### Following Lead Direction

On load-in, your primary relationship is with your lead. The lead has been assigned to your department or your zone, and they are responsible for directing your work.

When a lead gives you an instruction:
- Listen completely before you move
- Confirm you understood if there is any doubt
- Execute the task as directed
- Report back when complete

When you are unsure what to do:
- Ask your lead
- Do not ask random people on the floor
- Do not freelance a task because it seems like the obvious next step

"I don't know — let me check with my lead" is a professional answer. Acting on a guess and getting it wrong is not.

---

### Tasks That Remain Awareness-Only

Some tasks you will observe during load-in require specific training, certification, or employer authorization before anyone performs them. These include:

- Rigging and motor operation
- Forklift and aerial lift operation
- Power tie-ins and electrical connections
- Elevated or climbing work

As a beginning stagehand, your relationship to these tasks is **awareness only.** You should understand that these tasks exist, recognize when they are happening, and stay clear of the work zone. You do not perform them. You do not assist unless a qualified lead specifically directs you to do so in a support role.

If you are ever uncertain whether a task requires authorization, the correct answer is: stop and ask your lead.

---

## Section 3: Show Call Expectations

### The Shift in Energy

Load-in is physical and active. Show call is different.

When the building opens and the audience arrives, the energy of the venue changes completely. The job now requires a different kind of discipline — one that is less about moving cases and more about being exactly where you are supposed to be, staying out of the way, and being instantly available when you are needed.

New crew members sometimes misread show call as downtime. It is not. It is a sustained state of readiness.

---

### Quiet Professionalism

Backstage and in the wings during a show, the standard is quiet professionalism.

That means:
- No unnecessary conversation
- No phone use in positions where you are on call
- No wandering through spaces you have not been assigned to
- No standing in pathways or blocking sight lines

The show is running. Your job is to support it without disrupting it.

---

### Staying Visible and Available

One of the most common complaints production managers and leads have about local crew during show call is simple: **they disappear.**

They finish a task, don't know what's next, and drift somewhere comfortable instead of returning to a visible, accessible position.

Do not do this.

When you complete a task during show call, return to your assigned position. If you do not have a position, check in with your lead. Make yourself easy to find. The crew members who get called back are the ones who were always where they were supposed to be.

---

### Radio Etiquette

On many calls, especially larger productions, crew communicates via two-way radio. If you are issued a radio, a few basics apply:

**Listen before you transmit.** If someone is already talking, wait.

**Keep transmissions short and clear.** Say what you need. Stop.

**Use the channel you were assigned.** Do not monitor channels outside your assignment.

**Do not use the radio for casual conversation.** Production channels are working channels.

If you are not sure how to use the radio system, ask your lead before the show starts — not during it.

---

### Backstage Awareness and Movement

During a show, people and equipment move through backstage spaces on specific cues and timelines. As a newer crew member, you need to understand:

- **Do not cross in front of performance areas during active scenes or cues.** If something is moving or about to move, wait.
- **Keep emergency pathways clear at all times.** This is not optional.
- **Know where exits are.** Not just for your own safety — because if something happens, you may be asked to direct others.
- **If you see a safety concern, report it immediately to your lead.** Do not wait. Do not assume someone else saw it.

---

## Section 4: Load-Out and Strike Culture

### Why Load-Out Moves Fast

The moment the show ends, a countdown starts. The venue has a curfew. The trucks have a departure window. The next city is already scheduled.

Load-out is faster than load-in by design. Equipment is pre-staged, cases are labeled, and experienced hands know exactly what goes where. The pace is real and the expectation is that everyone keeps up.

That said: **speed does not replace safety.** This is where the tension lives, and where experienced hands earn their reputation.

---

### Why Mistakes Happen During Strike

Load-out is when the most preventable incidents occur. The reasons are predictable:

**Fatigue.** You have been on your feet for hours. Your reaction time is slower. Your judgment is affected.

**Pace pressure.** The energy of a fast strike can make people feel like stopping to think is falling behind. It is not.

**Communication breakdown.** When everyone is moving fast, fewer words get exchanged. Assumptions fill the gaps.

**Freelancing.** Someone decides they know what needs to happen next and acts without direction. They are wrong. Something gets damaged or someone gets hurt.

The antidote to all of these is the same: **stay in your lane, stay with your lead, and stop and ask when you are not sure.**

---

### Chain-of-Command Discipline During Load-Out

Load-out is not the time for independence. It is the time for the tightest chain-of-command discipline of the day.

During strike, you may receive instructions from multiple people. Some of those people may not be your lead. Some of them may not even be in your department.

The correct response to instructions from someone outside your chain is not to ignore them entirely — but it is to check with your lead before changing your task. A simple "I'm assigned to [lead's name] on [task] — let me check with them" is the right answer.

Your lead knows the plan. Random people on the floor do not necessarily know your part of it.

---

### Fatigue Management

By load-out, you are tired. Everyone is tired. That is normal and expected.

What is not acceptable is letting fatigue make you careless.

Practical habits:
- Keep moving at a controlled pace, not an exhausted shuffle
- Stay hydrated throughout the day — not just at the end
- If you feel genuinely unsafe — dizzy, disoriented, physically compromised — tell your lead immediately
- Watch out for your coworkers the same way you watch out for yourself

The culture of this industry rewards people who stay solid all the way through. The load-out is the last impression you leave.

---

## Section 5: Professionalism and Rehire Culture

### What Actually Gets You Called Back

The live event industry is relationship-driven and reputation-based. Hiring decisions — especially for local crew — happen fast, often based on what a lead or steward remembers about working with you.

What they remember is rarely your technical skill at the entry level. What they remember is simpler than that:

**Were you where you were supposed to be?**
**Did you do what you were asked without needing to be managed?**
**Did you stay off your phone?**
**Did you ask when you didn't know instead of guessing?**
**Did you make the lead's job easier or harder?**

That is the rehire calculus. It is not complicated. But it requires consistency.

---

### The Language of the Call

Experienced crew communicate in shorthand. Here are phrases that carry real meaning in this culture:

**"Be where you're supposed to be."**
This sounds simple. It is also the most common failure point for new workers. Know your assignment. Know your position. Be there.

**"If you don't know, ask."**
Guessing wrong costs time, equipment, and sometimes safety. The crew that asks gets the answer. The crew that guesses causes problems.

**"Stay off your phone."**
During a work call — especially during show call — your phone is not your priority. Your job is.

**"Don't disappear."**
The moment a lead can't find you, you become a liability instead of an asset.

**"Easy to lead."**
This is a genuine compliment in this industry. It means you take direction well, you execute cleanly, and you do not create friction. It is not an insult. It is the foundation of being trusted with more responsibility over time.

**"Hard to lose."**
The flip side of easy to lead. Once a lead finds someone reliable, they hold onto them. Be that person.

---

### Reliability Is the Core Skill

Before technique, before speed, before specialized knowledge — reliability is the skill that opens doors in this industry.

Reliability means:
- Showing up on time, every time
- Doing what you said you would do
- Returning to your position without being chased
- Communicating when something changes
- Not disappearing after a task is complete

It sounds unglamorous. It is also the reason certain crew members work 200+ days a year and others struggle to get called back.

---

### Awareness and Initiative

There is a balance to strike as a new crew member between being proactive and overstepping.

**Awareness** means staying observant. You notice when a pathway is getting blocked. You notice when a road case is about to roll uncontrolled. You notice when a coworker looks like they need a second set of hands. That awareness, communicated to your lead, is valuable.

**Inappropriate initiative** means acting on that awareness without direction. Deciding to reroute the case flow. Helping with a task that belongs to a different department. Taking on work you have not been cleared for.

The difference is: observe and report. Do not observe and act independently until you have earned the experience and the trust.

---

### Humility as a Professional Tool

This industry will teach you things that no course can fully replicate. The way to access that learning is humility.

The most respected professionals in live events consistently credit the same source: they learned by watching carefully, asking good questions, and accepting correction without defensiveness.

You are not expected to know everything on your first call. You are expected to be honest about what you know and what you do not. That honesty is what makes you safe to work with.

---

## Section 6: Chain of Command

### Why This Matters

On a work call with dozens or hundreds of crew members working across multiple departments, clear communication depends on a functioning chain of command. Without it, instructions conflict, tasks get duplicated, and safety gaps appear.

Understanding the chain of command is not about politics. It is about operational clarity.

---

### How the Structure Typically Works

While every production is organized slightly differently, most touring and venue productions follow a structure that looks like this:

**Production Manager**
Oversees the entire production. Responsible for budget, schedule, logistics, and crew coordination across all departments. You will rarely receive direct instruction from the PM on a first call.

**Tour Manager** *(on touring productions)*
Manages the touring organization's daily operations, travel, and logistics. Keeps the machine moving from city to city.

**Department Heads**
Each major department — audio, lighting, video, rigging, staging, carpentry — has a head who manages that department's personnel and execution. On a large production, you may never speak directly to a department head.

**Leads**
This is your primary chain. Your lead has been assigned to your area, knows your task, and is the person who gives you direction. When you complete a task, you report back to your lead. When you have a question, you ask your lead.

**Touring Crew**
Skilled specialists who travel with the production. They are the technical authorities on this specific show. They do not generally assign or manage local crew directly — that happens through leads.

**Local Crew**
You. Your job is to support the plan.

**Labor Steward** *(where applicable)*
In union environments, a steward represents the local labor workforce. They handle labor-related concerns, compliance, and communication between the local and the production. If you work union calls, you will be oriented to this role through your local.

---

### The Practical Rule

You need to know three things at all times:

1. **Who gave you your assignment.** That is the person accountable for directing your work.
2. **Who you report back to when the task is complete.** Usually the same person.
3. **Who can redirect you.** Only someone in your direct chain — typically your lead or the person your lead reports to.

If someone outside that chain asks you to change your task or take on something new, the correct response is: "Let me check with [lead's name]."

This is not insubordination. It is how professional crew operations stay coordinated.

---

## Beginner Mistakes

These are the most common mistakes new crew members make. Recognizing them is the first step to avoiding them.

**Disappearing after completing a task.**
Finish the job, then drift somewhere unassigned. Your lead cannot find you when the next task is ready.

**Taking direction from the wrong person.**
Someone you don't know tells you to do something. You do it. Your lead needed you for something else. Now both tasks are compromised.

**Acting on assumption instead of asking.**
You think you know what the next step is. You do it. You were wrong.

**Using your phone during show call.**
You miss a cue. You miss a direction. You are no longer where your mind needs to be.

**Going into spaces you have not been assigned to.**
Curiosity is understandable. Wandering into restricted or active work areas creates risk for you and for others.

**Touching equipment you have not been cleared to operate.**
This is the most safety-critical mistake on the list. If you have not been trained, authorized, and assigned — do not touch it.

---

## Professional Habits

These habits, practiced consistently from your first call, build the kind of reputation that creates a career.

- Arrive early. Not on time — early.
- Know your assignment before you move.
- Ask before you assume.
- Report back when complete.
- Stay in your assigned position during show call.
- Keep your phone put away during active work.
- Move with purpose, not with panic.
- Communicate clearly and briefly.
- Watch out for your coworkers.
- Leave the space better than you found it.

---

## Safety Notes

The following reminders apply to every phase of a work call.

**Operational safety is everyone's responsibility.** You do not need a title or a supervisor to identify a hazard and report it.

**Authorized work requires authorization.** Rigging, power, lifts, forklifts, elevated work, and structural decisions are performed only by trained, assigned, authorized personnel. This boundary exists to protect you and everyone around you.

**Stop and ask is always the right call.** If you are unsure whether something is safe, stop. Ask your lead. Do not proceed on a guess.

**Emergency pathways must remain clear.** Always. No exceptions.

**Fatigue impairs judgment.** If you are in a state where you cannot work safely, communicate that to your lead immediately.

**If you see something, say something.** Report hazards to your lead. Do not assume someone else already saw it.

---

## Scenario

You are on your first arena show call as local crew. You have been assigned to the audio department under a lead named Marcus. Your task for load-in was running cable from the FOH position to the stage. You finished early.

Marcus is on the far side of the floor dealing with something. You see two road cases near the dock that look like they need to go to the stage. No one is moving them.

**What do you do?**

You return to your position near the area where Marcus last directed you. You do not move the road cases — you do not know which department they belong to, what is in them, or whether they are staged in that location intentionally. You wait until Marcus returns or signals you, then you report that you are finished and ask what he needs next.

**Why?**

Because acting on assumption — even with the best intentions — can disrupt someone else's plan, move the wrong equipment, or put you in the path of a task that has an authorized workflow you do not know about.

"I finished the cable run. I'm back at position. What do you need?" is a sentence that gets you more work. And more calls.

The live event industry rewards one thing above everything else at the entry level: **reliability.**

You do not need to be the fastest. You do not need to know the most. You do not need to impress anyone.

# Course 1: Your First Work Call

---

## Lesson Purpose

Most new stagehands show up to their first call knowing almost nothing about how the day actually works. They know the address. They know the call time. That's usually it.

This lesson closes that gap.

By the time you finish this lesson, you will have a clear mental map of how a professional work call is structured, what is expected of you at each phase, and what separates the crew members who get called back from the ones who don't.

This is not about mastering a craft. That comes later, with time and experience. This is about understanding the environment you are walking into so you can show up ready, stay useful, and leave with your reputation intact.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

- Describe the three primary phases of a work call and what happens during each one
- Identify your place in the chain of command and explain who you report to
- Demonstrate the basic expectations of show call behavior and dock etiquette
- Recognize which tasks require authorization and explain why you do not perform them without proper assignment
- Define the professional habits that drive rehire decisions in this industry

---

## Section 1: The Shape of a Show Day

### What You Are Walking Into

A live event work call is a coordinated, time-compressed operation. Dozens or hundreds of people — sometimes from multiple cities, multiple union locals, and multiple touring organizations — arrive at the same venue and execute a shared plan under serious time pressure.

From the outside, it can look like controlled chaos. From the inside, once you understand the structure, it is actually a highly organized sequence of phases with clear objectives at each stage.

Every call, whether you are working an arena concert, a stadium show, a festival, or a one-off corporate production, follows the same basic shape:

**Load-In → Show Call → Load-Out**

Those three phases define your day. Everything else — the tasks, the pace, the communication, the culture — fits within that structure.

---

### How the Day Is Divided

A typical arena or stadium show day might run something like this:

**Early Morning — Dock Opens, Trucks Arrive**
The day begins at the dock. Trucks from the touring production arrive and are staged in a specific order. The production team already knows which truck holds what and in what sequence it needs to be unloaded. The building has been prepped. Department heads have received their assignments. When local crew arrives, the work begins immediately.

**Morning Through Afternoon — Load-In**
This is the build phase. Audio, lighting, video, rigging, and staging departments each work their own zone while sharing the building. Cases come off trucks, equipment is positioned, systems are built. The pace is steady and purposeful.

**Afternoon — Production Advance and Systems Check**
As departments complete their builds, the focus shifts to testing, adjusting, and preparing for the show. Touring crew leads this phase. Local crew may be transitioning out or moving into show call assignments.

**Evening — Show Call**
Doors open. The audience arrives. The show runs. This phase requires a different kind of professionalism — quiet, positioned, alert, and ready to respond without being in the way.

**After the Show — Load-Out / Strike**
The moment the last note plays, load-out begins. Everything that came in goes back out. It goes faster than load-in. The fatigue is real. The expectations remain high.

---

### Touring Crew vs. Local Crew

Understanding this distinction matters from day one.

**Touring crew** travels with the production. They built the show, they know the system, and they are responsible for how it gets executed at every venue on the run. They are the experts on this specific production.

**Local crew** is hired market by market to provide labor support. That is the role most new stagehands step into first. You are there to support the touring crew's plan — not to improvise your own.

This is not a hierarchy designed to make you feel less important. It is a practical reality of how touring productions function. Your job on your first calls is to be a reliable, efficient, responsive member of the local crew. That is genuinely valuable work. Do it well.

---

## Section 2: Load-In Operations

### What Load-In Actually Looks Like

Load-in is physical, repetitive, and detail-dependent. For entry-level crew, the work often centers on a specific set of tasks:

- Unloading trucks at the dock
- Moving road cases from the dock to the stage or arena floor
- Running cable under the direction of a department lead
- Setting up specific equipment or infrastructure as assigned
- Keeping pathways clear and maintaining organized staging areas

There is a rhythm to it. Experienced hands move with purpose. They are not sprinting, but they are not wandering either. The phrase you will hear — and eventually say — is **hurry without rushing.** Move efficiently. Stay focused. Don't create problems by going too fast.

---

### Dock Etiquette

The dock is one of the most important and most misunderstood spaces on a work call. It is a high-traffic, safety-critical area where trucks, forklifts, road cases, and crew all occupy the same limited space.

A few fundamentals:

**Know where you are supposed to be.** If you were assigned to the dock, be at the dock. If you were assigned to the floor, be on the floor. Do not migrate based on curiosity.

**Follow the flow.** Cases move in a specific direction. Pathways are established for a reason. Move with the flow, not across it.

**Stay aware of equipment in motion.** Forklifts, motorized pallet movers, and large road cases can cause serious injury if you are not paying attention to your surroundings.

**Do not operate equipment you have not been trained and assigned to use.** Forklifts, elevated work platforms, and any powered equipment require proper training, authorization, and assignment. If no one has specifically trained you, assigned you, and cleared you to operate it — your answer is no. This is not optional. This is a safety boundary.

---

### Following Lead Direction

On load-in, your primary relationship is with your lead. The lead has been assigned to your department or your zone, and they are responsible for directing your work.

When a lead gives you an instruction:
- Listen completely before you move
- Confirm you understood if there is any doubt
- Execute the task as directed
- Report back when complete

When you are unsure what to do:
- Ask your lead
- Do not ask random people on the floor
- Do not freelance a task because it seems like the obvious next step

"I don't know — let me check with my lead" is a professional answer. Acting on a guess and getting it wrong is not.

---

### Tasks That Remain Awareness-Only

Some tasks you will observe during load-in require specific training, certification, or employer authorization before anyone performs them. These include:

- Rigging and motor operation
- Forklift and aerial lift operation
- Power tie-ins and electrical connections
- Elevated or climbing work

As a beginning stagehand, your relationship to these tasks is **awareness only.** You should understand that these tasks exist, recognize when they are happening, and stay clear of the work zone. You do not perform them. You do not assist unless a qualified lead specifically directs you to do so in a support role.

If you are ever uncertain whether a task requires authorization, the correct answer is: stop and ask your lead.

---

## Section 3: Show Call Expectations

### The Shift in Energy

Load-in is physical and active. Show call is different.

When the building opens and the audience arrives, the energy of the venue changes completely. The job now requires a different kind of discipline — one that is less about moving cases and more about being exactly where you are supposed to be, staying out of the way, and being instantly available when you are needed.

New crew members sometimes misread show call as downtime. It is not. It is a sustained state of readiness.

---

### Quiet Professionalism

Backstage and in the wings during a show, the standard is quiet professionalism.

That means:
- No unnecessary conversation
- No phone use in positions where you are on call
- No wandering through spaces you have not been assigned to
- No standing in pathways or blocking sight lines

The show is running. Your job is to support it without disrupting it.

---

### Staying Visible and Available

One of the most common complaints production managers and leads have about local crew during show call is simple: **they disappear.**

They finish a task, don't know what's next, and drift somewhere comfortable instead of returning to a visible, accessible position.

Do not do this.

When you complete a task during show call, return to your assigned position. If you do not have a position, check in with your lead. Make yourself easy to find. The crew members who get called back are the ones who were always where they were supposed to be.

---

### Radio Etiquette

On many calls, especially larger productions, crew communicates via two-way radio. If you are issued a radio, a few basics apply:

**Listen before you transmit.** If someone is already talking, wait.

**Keep transmissions short and clear.** Say what you need. Stop.

**Use the channel you were assigned.** Do not monitor channels outside your assignment.

**Do not use the radio for casual conversation.** Production channels are working channels.

If you are not sure how to use the radio system, ask your lead before the show starts — not during it.

---

### Backstage Awareness and Movement

During a show, people and equipment move through backstage spaces on specific cues and timelines. As a newer crew member, you need to understand:

- **Do not cross in front of performance areas during active scenes or cues.** If something is moving or about to move, wait.
- **Keep emergency pathways clear at all times.** This is not optional.
- **Know where exits are.** Not just for your own safety — because if something happens, you may be asked to direct others.
- **If you see a safety concern, report it immediately to your lead.** Do not wait. Do not assume someone else saw it.

---

## Section 4: Load-Out and Strike Culture

### Why Load-Out Moves Fast

The moment the show ends, a countdown starts. The venue has a curfew. The trucks have a departure window. The next city is already scheduled.

Load-out is faster than load-in by design. Equipment is pre-staged, cases are labeled, and experienced hands know exactly what goes where. The pace is real and the expectation is that everyone keeps up.

That said: **speed does not replace safety.** This is where the tension lives, and where experienced hands earn their reputation.

---

### Why Mistakes Happen During Strike

Load-out is when the most preventable incidents occur. The reasons are predictable:

**Fatigue.** You have been on your feet for hours. Your reaction time is slower. Your judgment is affected.

**Pace pressure.** The energy of a fast strike can make people feel like stopping to think is falling behind. It is not.

**Communication breakdown.** When everyone is moving fast, fewer words get exchanged. Assumptions fill the gaps.

**Freelancing.** Someone decides they know what needs to happen next and acts without direction. They are wrong. Something gets damaged or someone gets hurt.

The antidote to all of these is the same: **stay in your lane, stay with your lead, and stop and ask when you are not sure.**

---

### Chain-of-Command Discipline During Load-Out

Load-out is not the time for independence. It is the time for the tightest chain-of-command discipline of the day.

During strike, you may receive instructions from multiple people. Some of those people may not be your lead. Some of them may not even be in your department.

The correct response to instructions from someone outside your chain is not to ignore them entirely — but it is to check with your lead before changing your task. A simple "I'm assigned to [lead's name] on [task] — let me check with them" is the right answer.

Your lead knows the plan. Random people on the floor do not necessarily know your part of it.

---

### Fatigue Management

By load-out, you are tired. Everyone is tired. That is normal and expected.

What is not acceptable is letting fatigue make you careless.

Practical habits:
- Keep moving at a controlled pace, not an exhausted shuffle
- Stay hydrated throughout the day — not just at the end
- If you feel genuinely unsafe — dizzy, disoriented, physically compromised — tell your lead immediately
- Watch out for your coworkers the same way you watch out for yourself

The culture of this industry rewards people who stay solid all the way through. The load-out is the last impression you leave.

---

## Section 5: Professionalism and Rehire Culture

### What Actually Gets You Called Back

The live event industry is relationship-driven and reputation-based. Hiring decisions — especially for local crew — happen fast, often based on what a lead or steward remembers about working with you.

What they remember is rarely your technical skill at the entry level. What they remember is simpler than that:

**Were you where you were supposed to be?**
**Did you do what you were asked without needing to be managed?**
**Did you stay off your phone?**
**Did you ask when you didn't know instead of guessing?**
**Did you make the lead's job easier or harder?**

That is the rehire calculus. It is not complicated. But it requires consistency.

---

### The Language of the Call

Experienced crew communicate in shorthand. Here are phrases that carry real meaning in this culture:

**"Be where you're supposed to be."**
This sounds simple. It is also the most common failure point for new workers. Know your assignment. Know your position. Be there.

**"If you don't know, ask."**
Guessing wrong costs time, equipment, and sometimes safety. The crew that asks gets the answer. The crew that guesses causes problems.

**"Stay off your phone."**
During a work call — especially during show call — your phone is not your priority. Your job is.

**"Don't disappear."**
The moment a lead can't find you, you become a liability instead of an asset.

**"Easy to lead."**
This is a genuine compliment in this industry. It means you take direction well, you execute cleanly, and you do not create friction. It is not an insult. It is the foundation of being trusted with more responsibility over time.

**"Hard to lose."**
The flip side of easy to lead. Once a lead finds someone reliable, they hold onto them. Be that person.

---

### Reliability Is the Core Skill

Before technique, before speed, before specialized knowledge — reliability is the skill that opens doors in this industry.

Reliability means:
- Showing up on time, every time
- Doing what you said you would do
- Returning to your position without being chased
- Communicating when something changes
- Not disappearing after a task is complete

It sounds unglamorous. It is also the reason certain crew members work 200+ days a year and others struggle to get called back.

---

### Awareness and Initiative

There is a balance to strike as a new crew member between being proactive and overstepping.

**Awareness** means staying observant. You notice when a pathway is getting blocked. You notice when a road case is about to roll uncontrolled. You notice when a coworker looks like they need a second set of hands. That awareness, communicated to your lead, is valuable.

**Inappropriate initiative** means acting on that awareness without direction. Deciding to reroute the case flow. Helping with a task that belongs to a different department. Taking on work you have not been cleared for.

The difference is: observe and report. Do not observe and act independently until you have earned the experience and the trust.

---

### Humility as a Professional Tool

This industry will teach you things that no course can fully replicate. The way to access that learning is humility.

The most respected professionals in live events consistently credit the same source: they learned by watching carefully, asking good questions, and accepting correction without defensiveness.

You are not expected to know everything on your first call. You are expected to be honest about what you know and what you do not. That honesty is what makes you safe to work with.

---

On a work call with dozens or hundreds of crew members working across multiple departments, clear communication depends on a functioning chain of command. Without it, instructions conflict, tasks get duplicated, and safety gaps appear.

Understanding the chain of command is not about politics. It is about operational clarity.
