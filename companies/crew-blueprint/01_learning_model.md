# 01 — Learning Model

**Status:** Documented (core model decided; repository discipline/pathway
mapping is the active documentation work, roadmap `cb-2`).

## Teaching philosophy

The Crew Blueprint teaches the way the job is actually done: **visual and
workflow-based**, not academic. A learner should come away understanding not
just the step but the *reason* for it and *where it sits in the show*. The
throughline of every course:

> Learn **what** you're doing, **why** you're doing it, and **how it fits the
> bigger picture.**

Instruction is **field-experience-driven** — grounded in real jobsite reality,
practical over theoretical, with an emphasis on job readiness.

## Discipline / pathway mapping

Content is organized by **discipline** (the craft area) and **pathway** (a
learner's route through skill levels within and across disciplines). This lets a
learner navigate logically — by the role they work and the level they're at —
rather than through an undifferentiated pile of lessons. Building this granular
discipline/pathway structure in the repository is tracked as roadmap `cb-2`.

**This map is the skill tree.** Per the 2026-07-18 delivery decision (doc 05), the
Blueprint is delivered as a **gamified static experience**, and the
discipline/pathway map *is* its skill tree — nodes to unlock across disciplines
and levels, with progress kept client-side. So `cb-2` does double duty: it
organizes the content *and* defines the game structure.

## Progression philosophy

- Courses build from **fundamentals** upward; a learner establishes a base
  before specialized material.
- Each course applies the **standardized architecture** in doc 03 (consistent
  lesson/module/quiz format, an 80% passing score, safety callouts, professional
  habits, common beginner mistakes, reflection questions, and jobsite examples).
- Consistency is deliberate: the same structure across every course means a
  learner always knows how to learn here, and new courses reuse a proven mold.

## Long-term discipline scope

The library is intended to expand, over time, to cover every major production
discipline and the professional skills around it:

- Lighting
- Audio
- Video
- Rigging
- Carpentry
- Stage management
- Touring
- Corporate events
- Leadership
- OSHA awareness
- Contractor education
- Mental-health awareness for production workers

This is the destination, not the near-term plan — near-term work is
content-first on foundational courses (see doc 08). The scope is recorded here so
the structure is built to accommodate it from the start.
