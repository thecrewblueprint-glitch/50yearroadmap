**Why this doc:** the owner is weighing a **new branch of the plan** — traveling to

big question that research answers: *can the van and the travel be a write-off?*
The short answer shapes the whole move.

---

## Bottom line

The van and travel are deductible **only** as legitimate expenses of an **active
self-employed business** (1099 / LLC — i.e. **Deadhang Labor LLC**) that is
traveling for **documented business**, in the same trade. They are **not**
deductible for:

- **W-2 job hunting** — the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act eliminated the federal deduction
  for job-search travel for employees. If the goal is a regular W-2 job, there is
  **no** federal write-off.
- **Speculative travel** — driving to a new state "hoping to find work" with no
  concrete leads is treated by the IRS as personal exploration (a "fishing
  expedition"), **not** a business trip. *Intent alone fails the test.*

So the deduction is **gated on operating as an active contractor with real,
documented gigs** — not on the wish to find work.

---

## The van itself (Section 179)

- **Heavy-vehicle rule:** a vehicle with a **GVWR over 6,000 lbs** used **more than
  50% for business** can qualify for favorable first-year expensing under
  **Section 179**.
- **Proportional only:** the deduction is limited to the **exact business-use %**.
  Fuel, insurance, maintenance, and repairs are deductible on the same ratio.
- **Living upgrades are NOT deductible** — a bed, kitchen, solar, camping
  comfort build-out are **personal** expenses, even in a business vehicle.

## Mileage, lodging, meals

- **Mileage:** log business miles (home → client/interview, between work sites) and
  multiply by the IRS **standard mileage rate**. **Commuting and leisure miles do
  not count.**
- **Lodging / campgrounds:** deductible when you're **away from your tax home** for
  a business overnight. Paid RV lot / campground between workdays = deductible
  lodging; a **free** campground or Walmart lot = no lodging expense (but the direct
  business miles may still count).
- **Meals:** business portion only.
- **Mixed trips:** only the **business portion** of transportation, lodging, and
  meals — and if the trip is *primarily personal*, transportation isn't deductible
  at all.

## The "tax home" trap

A traveling worker with **no fixed main place of business** can lose travel
deductions **entirely** (documented cases exist). Keeping/establishing a **tax
home** matters if the plan is long-term mobile work — worth a specific question to
the tax pro given the van-life angle.

---

## What makes it hold up — the checklist

1. **Operate an active business** in the trade (Deadhang Labor LLC / sole prop),
   really running, not dormant.
2. **Secure specific leads / contracts / meetings BEFORE crossing state lines** —
   emails and calendar invites are the audit proof that the trip's *primary purpose*
   is business.
3. **Structure the days around work** — the trip must be primarily business; a few
   hours of work across several personal days gets the whole trip rejected.
4. **Keep a mileage log** with, per business segment: date, **odometer start/end**,
   destination address, and **business purpose / client name**.
5. **Separate business from personal** — leisure miles, sightseeing detours, and
   personal days are excluded; log only the direct business routes.
6. **Keep receipts** for campground/lodging tied to actual workdays.

## What you cannot write off

- Speculative job-hunting travel (no concrete leads).
- Any W-2 job-search expenses.
- The van's living / lifestyle build-out.
- Personal / sightseeing days and detours.
